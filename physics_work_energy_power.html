Chapter One

Work

In physics, the word "work" has a very precise meaning that differs considerably from its everyday usage. This chapter develops the concept of work rigorously and explores its relationship with force and displacement.

SECTION 1.1
Basic Concept of Work

1.1.1 Definition of Work

In everyday language, we say that a person is "doing work" when they are busy or exerting effort. In physics, however, work has a very specific and restricted meaning. Work is done on an object only when a force acts on the object and the object moves in the direction of that force (or has a component of displacement in the direction of the force).

If a person pushes against a wall with all their might but the wall does not move, no work is done in the physics sense, regardless of how much effort the person expends.

Important: For work to be done in physics, two conditions must be satisfied simultaneously: (1) A force must be applied to the object, and (2) The object must undergo a displacement in the direction of the force.

1.1.2 The Formula for Work

When a constant force F acts on an object and the object undergoes a displacement d in the exact direction of the force, the work done is defined as:

W = F × d
Where:
W = Work done (measured in Joules, J)
F = Applied force (measured in Newtons, N)
d = Displacement of the object (measured in metres, m)

1.1.3 The Unit of Work

The SI unit of work is the Joule (J). One Joule is defined as the work done when a force of one Newton displaces an object by one metre in the direction of the force.

1 Joule = 1 Newton × 1 metre = 1 N·m

1.1.4 Work as a Scalar Quantity

Work is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude but no direction. Even though both force and displacement are vectors, the result of multiplying them in the manner defined for work produces a scalar. This is because work involves the dot product of the force and displacement vectors.

1.1.5 Worked Examples Using W = F × d

EXAMPLE 1.1 Pushing a Box Across a Floor
Given: A person applies a horizontal force of 50 N to push a box across a smooth floor. The box moves a horizontal distance of 8 m in the direction of the force.
Find: The work done by the person on the box.
Solution
Step 1
Write down the known values from the problem.
Force applied: F = 50 N
Displacement: d = 8 m
Step 2
Identify the correct formula to use.
W = F × d
This formula applies because the force and displacement are in the same direction (both horizontal).
Step 3
Substitute the known values into the formula.
W = 50 N × 8 m
Step 4
Perform the multiplication.
W = 400 N·m
Step 5
Convert to the standard unit of work.
W = 400 J
(Since 1 N·m = 1 J)
∴ The work done by the person on the box is 400 Joules
EXAMPLE 1.2 A Horse Pulling a Cart
Given: A horse exerts a force of 750 N on a cart along a straight road. The cart moves a distance of 200 m in the direction of the applied force.
Find: The total work done by the horse.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the known values.
F = 750 N
d = 200 m
Step 2
Write the formula for work.
W = F × d
Step 3
Substitute the values.
W = 750 N × 200 m
Step 4
Calculate the result.
W = 150,000 J
Step 5
Express in kilojoules for convenience.
W = 150 kJ
(Since 1 kJ = 1000 J)
∴ The work done by the horse is 150,000 J (150 kJ)
EXAMPLE 1.3 Finding the Force from Work Done
Given: A worker does 1,200 J of work pushing a trolley a distance of 15 m along a straight path. The force is applied in the direction of motion.
Find: The force applied by the worker.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the known values.
W = 1,200 J
d = 15 m
F = ?
Step 2
Write the formula for work.
W = F × d
Step 3
Rearrange the formula to make F the subject.
F = W ÷ d
Step 4
Substitute the known values.
F = 1,200 J ÷ 15 m
Step 5
Calculate the result.
F = 80 N
∴ The force applied by the worker is 80 Newtons
EXAMPLE 1.4 Finding the Displacement from Work Done
Given: An engine applies a constant force of 2,000 N on a car and does 500,000 J of work.
Find: The distance the car travels.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
F = 2,000 N
W = 500,000 J
d = ?
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = F × d
Step 3
Rearrange for d.
d = W ÷ F
Step 4
Substitute values.
d = 500,000 J ÷ 2,000 N
Step 5
Calculate.
d = 250 m
∴ The car travels a distance of 250 metres
EXAMPLE 1.5 Zero Work — Carrying a Bag Horizontally
Given: A student carries a bag of mass 5 kg horizontally across a corridor of length 20 m at a constant height.
Find: The work done by the student against gravity.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the forces. The student exerts an upward force to support the bag against gravity. This upward force equals the weight of the bag.
F = mg = 5 × 10 = 50 N (upward)
Step 2
Identify the displacement. The student moves horizontally for 20 m.
d = 20 m (horizontal)
Step 3
Observe the relationship between force and displacement. The upward force (50 N) is perpendicular to the horizontal displacement (20 m). When force is perpendicular to displacement, no work is done by that force.
Step 4
Apply the formula.
W = F × d × cos(90°) = 50 × 20 × 0 = 0 J
∴ The work done against gravity is 0 Joules — gravity does no work when motion is horizontal
  • Work is done only when a force causes a displacement of an object in the direction of the force.
  • The formula for work when force and displacement are parallel is: W = F × d
  • The SI unit of work is the Joule (J), where 1 J = 1 N·m
  • Work is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude only, no direction.
  • No work is done if: (a) there is no displacement, or (b) the force is perpendicular to the displacement.
  • Work can be found by rearranging the formula: F = W/d or d = W/F
SECTION 1.2
Work Done at an Angle

In many practical situations, the applied force is not exactly in the direction of motion. For example, when a person pulls a suitcase using a handle tilted at an angle, the force has a component in the direction of motion and a component perpendicular to it. Only the component in the direction of motion contributes to the work done.

Figure 1.1 — A force F applied at angle θ to the horizontal. Only the horizontal component F cosθ contributes to work done along the displacement d.
W = F d cosθ
Where:
W = Work done (J)
F = Magnitude of the applied force (N)
d = Magnitude of displacement (m)
θ = Angle between the force vector and the displacement vector

1.2.1 Special Cases of the Angle

Angle θcos θWork DoneType
0°1W = Fd (maximum)Positive work
90°0W = 0Zero work
180°−1W = −Fd (minimum)Negative work
Between 0° and 90°PositiveW > 0Positive work
Between 90° and 180°NegativeW < 0Negative work

1.2.2 Understanding Positive, Negative, and Zero Work

Positive work is done when the force has a component in the same direction as the displacement (e.g., pushing a box forward). Energy is transferred to the object.

Negative work is done when the force has a component opposite to the displacement (e.g., friction opposing motion). Energy is removed from the object.

Zero work is done when the force is perpendicular to displacement (e.g., the normal force on a horizontally moving object).

EXAMPLE 1.6 Pulling a Suitcase at an Angle
Given: A traveller pulls a suitcase with a force of 60 N at an angle of 30° above the horizontal. The suitcase moves a horizontal distance of 12 m.
Find: The work done by the traveller.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
F = 60 N, d = 12 m, θ = 30°
Step 2
Write the correct formula since force is at an angle.
W = F d cosθ
Step 3
Find the value of cos 30°.
cos 30° = √3/2 = 0.866
Step 4
Substitute all values into the formula.
W = 60 N × 12 m × 0.866
Step 5
Perform the multiplication step by step.
W = 60 × 12 × 0.866
W = 720 × 0.866
W = 623.5 J
∴ The work done by the traveller is 623.5 Joules
EXAMPLE 1.7 Pushing a Lawnmower
Given: A gardener pushes a lawnmower with a force of 80 N directed at 40° below the horizontal (i.e., pushing downward and forward). The lawnmower moves 15 m horizontally.
Find: The work done by the gardener.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the known values.
F = 80 N, d = 15 m, θ = 40°
Note: The angle between the force direction and the displacement (horizontal) is 40°.
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = F d cosθ
Step 3
Find the value of cos 40°.
cos 40° = 0.766
Step 4
Substitute the values.
W = 80 × 15 × 0.766
Step 5
Calculate step by step.
W = 1200 × 0.766
W = 919.2 J
∴ The work done by the gardener is 919.2 Joules
EXAMPLE 1.8 Work Done by Friction (Negative Work)
Given: A book slides along a table. The frictional force acting on the book is 5 N opposing the direction of motion. The book travels 3 m before stopping.
Find: The work done by friction.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the given values.
F = 5 N (friction, opposing motion)
d = 3 m
The angle between friction force and displacement = 180° (they are opposite)
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = F d cosθ
Step 3
Evaluate cos 180°.
cos 180° = −1
Step 4
Substitute the values.
W = 5 × 3 × (−1)
Step 5
Calculate the result.
W = −15 J
The negative sign confirms that friction removes energy from the book.
∴ The work done by friction is −15 Joules (negative work — energy is removed)
EXAMPLE 1.9 Finding the Angle from Work Done
Given: A force of 100 N acts on a crate and does 433 J of work while the crate moves 5 m.
Find: The angle between the force and the direction of motion.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
F = 100 N, W = 433 J, d = 5 m, θ = ?
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = F d cosθ
Step 3
Rearrange to make cosθ the subject.
cosθ = W ÷ (F × d)
Step 4
Substitute the values.
cosθ = 433 ÷ (100 × 5)
cosθ = 433 ÷ 500
cosθ = 0.866
Step 5
Find the angle using the inverse cosine.
θ = cos⁻¹(0.866)
θ = 30°
∴ The angle between the force and the displacement is 30°
  • When force is applied at an angle θ to the displacement, work is: W = Fd cosθ
  • Only the component of force along the displacement (F cosθ) does work.
  • When θ = 0°: maximum positive work (W = Fd). When θ = 90°: zero work. When θ = 180°: maximum negative work (W = −Fd).
  • Positive work adds energy to the object; negative work removes energy from the object.
  • Forces perpendicular to motion (like normal force, centripetal force) do zero work.
  • The angle θ is always the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.
SECTION 1.3
Work Done by Multiple Forces

When more than one force acts on an object simultaneously, the total (or net) work done is the algebraic sum of the work done by each individual force. This is the principle of superposition of work.

Wnet = W₁ + W₂ + W₃ + ...
Where:
W_net = Total (net) work done on the object (J)
W₁, W₂, W₃... = Work done by each individual force, including sign

Alternatively, the net work can be calculated using the net force directly:

Wnet = Fnet × d
Where:
F_net = Net force acting on the object (N)
d = Displacement of the object (m)

1.3.1 Work Done by Common Forces

Work done by friction: Friction always opposes motion (θ = 180°), so it always does negative work: W_friction = −f × d, where f is the magnitude of the frictional force.

Work done by the normal force: The normal force is perpendicular to motion (θ = 90°), so it always does zero work.

Work done by gravity: Depends on direction of motion relative to gravity (discussed in Section 1.4).

EXAMPLE 1.10 Net Work with Applied Force and Friction
Given: A box is pushed horizontally with an applied force of 120 N. The frictional force opposing the motion is 40 N. The box moves 10 m horizontally.
Find: (a) Work done by the applied force, (b) Work done by friction, (c) Net work done on the box.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
F_applied = 120 N (forward), F_friction = 40 N (backward), d = 10 m
Step 2
Calculate work done by the applied force (θ = 0°, force and displacement in same direction).
W_applied = F_applied × d × cos(0°)
W_applied = 120 × 10 × 1
W_applied = 1,200 J
Step 3
Calculate work done by friction (θ = 180°, friction opposes motion).
W_friction = F_friction × d × cos(180°)
W_friction = 40 × 10 × (−1)
W_friction = −400 J
Step 4
Calculate the net work by adding all work values algebraically.
W_net = W_applied + W_friction
W_net = 1,200 J + (−400 J)
W_net = 1,200 − 400
W_net = 800 J
Step 5
Verify using net force method.
F_net = 120 − 40 = 80 N
W_net = F_net × d = 80 × 10 = 800 J ✓
∴ Work by applied force = +1,200 J, Work by friction = −400 J, Net work = 800 J
EXAMPLE 1.11 Three Forces Acting on a Sliding Block
Given: A block slides 6 m on a horizontal surface. Three forces act: Applied force = 200 N (forward), Friction = 60 N (backward), Normal force = 150 N (upward).
Find: The net work done on the block.
Solution
Step 1
Work done by applied force (θ = 0°):
W₁ = 200 × 6 × cos(0°) = 200 × 6 × 1 = 1,200 J
Step 2
Work done by friction (θ = 180°):
W₂ = 60 × 6 × cos(180°) = 60 × 6 × (−1) = −360 J
Step 3
Work done by normal force (θ = 90°):
W₃ = 150 × 6 × cos(90°) = 150 × 6 × 0 = 0 J
Step 4
Add all work contributions algebraically:
W_net = W₁ + W₂ + W₃
W_net = 1,200 + (−360) + 0
W_net = 840 J
∴ The net work done on the block is 840 Joules
  • Net work = algebraic sum of work done by all individual forces: W_net = ΣW
  • Alternatively: W_net = F_net × d (using the net force directly).
  • Friction always does negative work (opposes motion, θ = 180°).
  • Normal force always does zero work (perpendicular to motion, θ = 90°).
  • Always include the sign (+ or −) when adding work contributions — signs matter!
  • Net work can be zero even when individual forces do work (e.g., if a body moves at constant velocity).
SECTION 1.4
Work Done Against Gravity

Gravity is a special and important force in mechanics. When an object is lifted vertically, work must be done against the gravitational force. This work is stored as gravitational potential energy in the object.

W = mgh
Where:
W = Work done against gravity (J)
m = Mass of the object (kg)
g = Acceleration due to gravity (≈ 10 m/s² or 9.8 m/s²)
h = Height through which the object is raised (m)
1
Weight of object = mg
The force needed to lift an object at constant speed equals its weight (mg), because the net force must be zero for constant velocity.
2
W = F × d
The basic formula for work. Here, the applied force F = mg (upward) and the displacement d = h (upward).
3
W = mg × h
Substituting F = mg and d = h into the work formula, since force and displacement are both upward (θ = 0°).
4
∴ W = mgh
This is the work done against gravity when an object of mass m is raised through a height h.
EXAMPLE 1.12 Lifting a Crate
Given: A worker lifts a crate of mass 25 kg vertically to a height of 3 m. (Take g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The work done against gravity.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
m = 25 kg, g = 10 m/s², h = 3 m
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = mgh
Step 3
Substitute the values.
W = 25 × 10 × 3
Step 4
Multiply step by step.
W = 250 × 3
W = 750 J
∴ The work done against gravity is 750 Joules
EXAMPLE 1.13 Climbing a Staircase
Given: A student of mass 60 kg climbs a flight of stairs to a height of 5 m. (Take g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The work done against gravity.
Solution
Step 1
Identify known values.
m = 60 kg, g = 10 m/s², h = 5 m
Step 2
Apply the formula.
W = mgh
Step 3
Substitute values.
W = 60 × 10 × 5
Step 4
Calculate.
W = 600 × 5 = 3,000 J
∴ The work done against gravity is 3,000 Joules (3 kJ)
EXAMPLE 1.14 Finding the Height from Work Done
Given: A mass of 10 kg requires 980 J of work to be lifted. (g = 9.8 m/s²)
Find: The height to which the object is raised.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
m = 10 kg, W = 980 J, g = 9.8 m/s², h = ?
Step 2
Write the formula.
W = mgh
Step 3
Rearrange for h.
h = W ÷ (mg)
Step 4
Calculate mg first.
mg = 10 × 9.8 = 98 N
Step 5
Substitute and compute.
h = 980 ÷ 98 = 10 m
∴ The object is raised to a height of 10 metres
  • Work done against gravity when lifting: W = mgh
  • The work done against gravity is independent of the path taken — only the vertical height matters.
  • When lowering an object, gravity does positive work: W_gravity = +mgh
  • When lifting an object, gravity does negative work on the object: W_gravity = −mgh
  • This work done against gravity is stored as gravitational potential energy.
  • g = 10 m/s² (approximate) or 9.8 m/s² (more precise) — always state which you are using.
SECTION 1.5
Graphical Interpretation of Work

Work can be represented and calculated graphically using a Force–Displacement graph (also called an F–d graph). The area under the F–d graph represents the work done.

W = Area under the F–d graph
Constant force: Rectangle — Area = F × d
Variable force: The area under the curve must be calculated (e.g., triangles, trapezoids, or integration)
Figure 1.2 — Left: Constant force produces a rectangular area = Fd. Right: Linearly increasing force produces a triangular area = ½Fd.
EXAMPLE 1.15 Work from a Rectangular F–d Graph
Given: A constant force of 40 N is applied to an object over a displacement of 8 m, as shown on an F–d graph.
Find: The work done using the graphical method.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the shape formed under the F–d graph.
Since the force is constant, the graph forms a rectangle with height = 40 N and base = 8 m.
Step 2
Apply the formula: Area under graph = Work done.
W = Area of rectangle = height × base
Step 3
Substitute the values.
W = 40 N × 8 m
Step 4
Calculate.
W = 320 J
∴ The work done is 320 Joules
EXAMPLE 1.16 Work from a Triangular F–d Graph
Given: A force increases linearly from 0 N to 60 N as an object moves from 0 to 10 m. The graph is a straight line from the origin to (10 m, 60 N).
Find: The work done.
Solution
Step 1
Identify the shape formed. Since the force increases linearly from 0, the shape under the graph is a triangle.
Step 2
Write the formula for the area of a triangle.
Area = ½ × base × height
Step 3
Identify base and height from the graph.
Base (displacement) = 10 m
Height (maximum force) = 60 N
Step 4
Apply the formula.
W = ½ × 10 × 60
Step 5
Calculate.
W = ½ × 600 = 300 J
∴ The work done is 300 Joules
  • The area under a Force–Displacement (F–d) graph equals the work done.
  • For a constant force: the graph is a horizontal line → area is a rectangle → W = F × d
  • For a linearly increasing force from zero: the graph is a diagonal → area is a triangle → W = ½ × F_max × d
  • For complex shapes: break the area into simple shapes (rectangles + triangles) and add.
  • Negative area (graph below the axis) represents negative work.

Chapter Two

Energy

Energy is one of the most fundamental concepts in all of science. It cannot be seen directly, yet its effects are everywhere. This chapter explores the nature of energy, its many forms, and the powerful law of conservation of energy.

SECTION 2.1
Basic Concept of Energy

2.1.1 Definition of Energy

Energy is defined as the capacity (or ability) to do work. An object possesses energy if it is capable of doing work. The SI unit of energy is the same as the unit of work: the Joule (J).

Energy, like work, is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude but no direction. This is a key reason why energy methods are often simpler to use than force-based methods in solving physics problems.

  • The SI unit of energy is the Joule (J), same as the unit of work.
  • Energy is a scalar quantity — it has no direction.
  • Energy exists in many forms: kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear, etc.
  • Energy and work are so closely linked that 1 Joule of work transfers exactly 1 Joule of energy.
SECTION 2.2
Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy (KE) is the energy possessed by an object by virtue of its motion. Any object that is moving — whether it's a rolling ball, a flying bird, or a moving car — possesses kinetic energy.

Kinetic energy depends on two factors: the mass of the object and its velocity. Importantly, kinetic energy depends on the square of the velocity, meaning that doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy.

KE = ½mv²
Where:
KE = Kinetic energy (J)
m = Mass of the object (kg)
v = Speed (velocity) of the object (m/s)
1
Start with the equation of motion: v² = u² + 2as
An object of mass m starts from rest (u = 0) and accelerates uniformly over displacement s.
2
Since u = 0: v² = 2as → a = v²/(2s)
Simplifying the kinematic equation for an object starting from rest.
3
Net force: F = ma = m × v²/(2s)
By Newton's second law, substituting the expression for acceleration.
4
Work done = F × s = [m × v²/(2s)] × s
Multiplying the force by the displacement s. The s in the numerator and denominator cancel.
5
∴ KE = W = ½mv²
The kinetic energy equals the net work done to accelerate the object from rest to speed v.
EXAMPLE 2.1Kinetic Energy of a Running Athlete
Given: An athlete of mass 70 kg runs at a speed of 8 m/s.
Find: The kinetic energy of the athlete.
Solution
Step 1
Write the known values.
m = 70 kg, v = 8 m/s
Step 2
Write the formula.
KE = ½mv²
Step 3
Calculate v² first.
v² = 8² = 64 m²/s²
Step 4
Substitute the values.
KE = ½ × 70 × 64
Step 5
Calculate step by step.
KE = ½ × 4,480
KE = 2,240 J
∴ The kinetic energy of the athlete is 2,240 Joules
EXAMPLE 2.2Kinetic Energy of a Moving Car
Given: A car of mass 1,200 kg moves at a speed of 20 m/s.
Find: The kinetic energy of the car.
Solution
Step 1
Known values: m = 1,200 kg, v = 20 m/s
Step 2
Formula: KE = ½mv²
Step 3
Compute v²: v² = 20² = 400 m²/s²
Step 4
Substitute: KE = ½ × 1,200 × 400
Step 5
Calculate: KE = ½ × 480,000 = 240,000 J = 240 kJ
∴ The kinetic energy of the car is 240,000 Joules (240 kJ)
EXAMPLE 2.3Finding Speed from Kinetic Energy
Given: A ball of mass 0.5 kg has a kinetic energy of 100 J.
Find: The speed of the ball.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 0.5 kg, KE = 100 J, v = ?
Step 2
Formula: KE = ½mv²
Step 3
Rearrange for v²: v² = 2KE ÷ m
Step 4
Substitute: v² = (2 × 100) ÷ 0.5 = 200 ÷ 0.5 = 400
Step 5
Take the square root: v = √400 = 20 m/s
∴ The speed of the ball is 20 m/s
EXAMPLE 2.4Comparing KE at Different Speeds
Given: A truck of mass 3,000 kg travels at (a) 10 m/s and (b) 20 m/s.
Find: The kinetic energy at each speed and compare.
Solution — Part (a): v = 10 m/s
Step 1
Known: m = 3,000 kg, v = 10 m/s
Step 2
Compute v²: v² = 100
Step 3
KE: KE₁ = ½ × 3,000 × 100 = 150,000 J
Solution — Part (b): v = 20 m/s
Step 4
Compute v²: v² = 400
Step 5
KE: KE₂ = ½ × 3,000 × 400 = 600,000 J
Step 6
Ratio: KE₂ ÷ KE₁ = 600,000 ÷ 150,000 = 4
Doubling the speed increases the kinetic energy by a factor of 4 (since KE ∝ v²).
∴ KE at 10 m/s = 150 kJ; KE at 20 m/s = 600 kJ — speed doubled, KE quadrupled
EXAMPLE 2.5Finding Mass from Kinetic Energy
Given: An object moving at 6 m/s has a kinetic energy of 270 J.
Find: The mass of the object.
Solution
Step 1
Known: v = 6 m/s, KE = 270 J, m = ?
Step 2
Formula: KE = ½mv²
Step 3
Rearrange for m: m = 2KE ÷ v²
Step 4
Calculate v²: v² = 36
Step 5
Substitute: m = (2 × 270) ÷ 36 = 540 ÷ 36 = 15 kg
∴ The mass of the object is 15 kg
  • Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: KE = ½mv²
  • Kinetic energy is always positive (since m and v² are always positive).
  • KE depends on the square of speed: doubling speed → 4× the KE; tripling speed → 9× the KE.
  • Doubling the mass doubles the KE (direct proportion).
  • To find speed: rearrange to v = √(2KE/m)
  • To find mass: rearrange to m = 2KE/v²
  • A stationary object has zero kinetic energy.
SECTION 2.3
Potential Energy

Potential energy (PE) is stored energy — energy that an object possesses due to its position, configuration, or state. There are two main types of potential energy in mechanics: gravitational potential energy and elastic potential energy.

2.3.1 Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy is the energy stored in an object due to its height above a reference point (usually the ground).

GPE = mgh
Where:
GPE = Gravitational potential energy (J)
m = Mass of the object (kg)
g = Acceleration due to gravity (≈ 10 m/s²)
h = Height above the reference point (m)

2.3.2 Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic potential energy is the energy stored in an elastic material (like a spring or rubber band) when it is stretched or compressed from its natural length. This is governed by Hooke's Law.

EPE = ½kx²
Where:
EPE = Elastic potential energy (J)
k = Spring constant (stiffness) in N/m
x = Extension or compression of the spring from its natural length (m)

2.3.3 Reference Point and Its Importance

The value of gravitational potential energy depends on the chosen reference point (where h = 0). This is usually taken as the ground or the lowest point in the problem. The change in potential energy (ΔGPE = mgΔh) is physically meaningful and does not depend on the choice of reference point.

EXAMPLE 2.6GPE of a Book on a Shelf
Given: A book of mass 2 kg is placed on a shelf 1.5 m above the floor. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The gravitational potential energy of the book.
Solution
Step 1
Known values: m = 2 kg, g = 10 m/s², h = 1.5 m
Step 2
Formula: GPE = mgh
Step 3
Substitute: GPE = 2 × 10 × 1.5
Step 4
Calculate: GPE = 20 × 1.5 = 30 J
∴ The gravitational potential energy is 30 Joules
EXAMPLE 2.7Finding Height from GPE
Given: A 5 kg object has 250 J of gravitational potential energy. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The height of the object above the reference point.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 5 kg, GPE = 250 J, g = 10 m/s², h = ?
Step 2
Formula: GPE = mgh
Step 3
Rearrange for h: h = GPE ÷ (mg)
Step 4
Compute mg: mg = 5 × 10 = 50 N
Step 5
Compute h: h = 250 ÷ 50 = 5 m
∴ The height of the object is 5 metres
EXAMPLE 2.8Elastic Potential Energy of a Compressed Spring
Given: A spring with spring constant k = 500 N/m is compressed by 0.04 m from its natural length.
Find: The elastic potential energy stored in the spring.
Solution
Step 1
Known: k = 500 N/m, x = 0.04 m
Step 2
Formula: EPE = ½kx²
Step 3
Calculate x²: x² = (0.04)² = 0.0016 m²
Step 4
Substitute: EPE = ½ × 500 × 0.0016
Step 5
Calculate: EPE = 250 × 0.0016 = 0.4 J
∴ The elastic potential energy stored is 0.4 Joules
  • Gravitational PE is stored due to height: GPE = mgh
  • Elastic PE is stored in stretched/compressed springs: EPE = ½kx²
  • GPE increases as height increases; EPE increases as extension/compression increases.
  • Both types of PE are measured in Joules (J).
  • The reference point (h = 0) is chosen for convenience. Only changes in GPE have absolute physical meaning.
  • GPE can be negative if the object is below the chosen reference point.
SECTION 2.4
The Work–Energy Theorem

The Work–Energy Theorem is one of the most important and useful results in mechanics. It states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Wnet = ΔKE = KEf − KEi
Where:
W_net = Net work done on the object (J)
ΔKE = Change in kinetic energy (J)
KE_f = Final kinetic energy = ½mv² (J)
KE_i = Initial kinetic energy = ½mu² (J)
Wnet = ½mv² − ½mu²
v = Final speed (m/s)
u = Initial speed (m/s)
m = Mass (kg)
1
W_net = F_net × d
Start from the definition of net work done by the net force over displacement d.
2
By Newton's 2nd Law: F_net = ma
Substitute Newton's second law for the net force.
3
W_net = ma × d = mad
Combining the two equations above.
4
From kinematics: v² = u² + 2ad → ad = (v² − u²)/2
Using the kinematic equation relating v, u, a, and d.
5
W_net = m × (v² − u²)/2 = ½mv² − ½mu²
Substituting the expression for ad into the work equation. This is the Work-Energy Theorem.
EXAMPLE 2.9Applying the Work-Energy Theorem
Given: A net force does 800 J of work on a 4 kg object. The object starts from rest.
Find: The final speed of the object.
Solution
Step 1
Known: W_net = 800 J, m = 4 kg, u = 0 m/s, v = ?
Step 2
Apply the Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ½mv² − ½mu²
Step 3
Since u = 0: 800 = ½ × 4 × v² − 0
800 = 2v²
Step 4
Solve for v²: v² = 800 ÷ 2 = 400
Step 5
Take square root: v = √400 = 20 m/s
∴ The final speed of the object is 20 m/s
EXAMPLE 2.10Change in KE due to Braking
Given: A car of mass 1,000 kg decelerates from 30 m/s to 10 m/s.
Find: The work done by the braking force (i.e., the net work done).
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 1,000 kg, u = 30 m/s, v = 10 m/s
Step 2
Apply: W_net = ½mv² − ½mu²
Step 3
Calculate initial KE: KE_i = ½ × 1000 × 30² = ½ × 1000 × 900 = 450,000 J
Step 4
Calculate final KE: KE_f = ½ × 1000 × 10² = ½ × 1000 × 100 = 50,000 J
Step 5
Net work done: W_net = 50,000 − 450,000 = −400,000 J
The negative sign confirms that the braking force removes energy from the car.
∴ The work done by the braking force is −400,000 J (−400 kJ)
  • Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
  • Positive net work → the object speeds up (KE increases).
  • Negative net work → the object slows down (KE decreases).
  • Zero net work → the object moves at constant speed (KE unchanged).
  • This theorem connects force, displacement, mass, and speed in one powerful equation.
  • It works even when the force is not constant — as long as we know the net work done.
SECTION 2.5
Conservation of Energy

One of the greatest laws of nature is the Law of Conservation of Energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another or transferred from one object to another. The total energy of a closed system remains constant.

KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂
This applies when no non-conservative forces (like friction) are present.
KE₁, PE₁ = Kinetic and potential energy at position 1 (initial)
KE₂, PE₂ = Kinetic and potential energy at position 2 (final)

Expanded form: ½mu₁² + mgh₁ = ½mv₂² + mgh₂
When is mechanical energy NOT conserved? Mechanical energy is not conserved when non-conservative forces (like friction, air resistance, tension in non-elastic ropes) do work. In these cases, some mechanical energy is converted to heat or other forms. However, total energy (including heat) is always conserved.
EXAMPLE 2.11Ball Dropped from a Height
Given: A ball of mass 0.2 kg is dropped from a height of 5 m. (g = 10 m/s², ignore air resistance)
Find: The speed of the ball just before it hits the ground.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 0.2 kg, h = 5 m, g = 10 m/s²
At the top: the ball is at rest, so KE₁ = 0, and PE₁ = mgh = 0.2 × 10 × 5 = 10 J
Step 2
At the bottom (ground level): h₂ = 0, so PE₂ = 0 and KE₂ = ½mv²
Step 3
Apply conservation of energy: KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂
0 + 10 = ½mv² + 0
10 = ½ × 0.2 × v²
10 = 0.1v²
Step 4
Solve for v²: v² = 10 ÷ 0.1 = 100
Step 5
Find v: v = √100 = 10 m/s
∴ The speed of the ball just before impact is 10 m/s
EXAMPLE 2.12Pendulum Swing
Given: A pendulum bob of mass 0.5 kg is raised to a height of 0.2 m above its lowest point and released from rest. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The speed of the bob at the lowest point of the swing.
Solution
Step 1
At the highest point: KE₁ = 0 (at rest), PE₁ = mgh = 0.5 × 10 × 0.2 = 1 J
Step 2
At the lowest point: PE₂ = 0 (reference), KE₂ = ½mv²
Step 3
Conservation of energy: 0 + 1 = ½ × 0.5 × v² + 0
1 = 0.25v²
Step 4
Solve: v² = 1 ÷ 0.25 = 4
v = √4 = 2 m/s
∴ The speed at the lowest point is 2 m/s
  • Law of Conservation of Energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
  • Conservation of mechanical energy (no friction): KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂
  • When an object falls, PE decreases and KE increases by the same amount.
  • When an object rises, KE decreases and PE increases by the same amount.
  • Friction converts mechanical energy into heat — mechanical energy is NOT conserved but total energy IS.
  • At the highest point of a projectile or pendulum, KE is minimum; at the lowest point, KE is maximum.

Energy transformation (or energy conversion) occurs when energy changes from one form to another. Energy transfer occurs when energy moves from one object to another. Both happen continuously in nature and in technology.

  • Energy transformation = energy changing from one form to another.
  • Energy transfer = energy moving from one object/location to another.

  • Most practical devices convert energy with some losses, typically as heat.
  • The more efficient a device, the more useful energy it produces per unit of input energy.
SECTION 2.8
Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy (ME) is the sum of the kinetic energy and potential energy of an object. It represents the total energy associated with the motion and position of an object.

ME = KE + PE
Expanded: ME = ½mv² + mgh

ME = Total mechanical energy (J)
KE = Kinetic energy = ½mv² (J)
PE = Gravitational potential energy = mgh (J)
EXAMPLE 2.13Total Mechanical Energy of a Projectile
Given: A 0.5 kg ball is thrown upward and at a certain instant is 3 m above the ground moving at 6 m/s. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The total mechanical energy of the ball at that instant.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 0.5 kg, v = 6 m/s, h = 3 m, g = 10 m/s²
Step 2
Calculate KE: KE = ½ × 0.5 × 6² = ½ × 0.5 × 36 = 9 J
Step 3
Calculate PE: PE = 0.5 × 10 × 3 = 15 J
Step 4
Total mechanical energy: ME = KE + PE = 9 + 15 = 24 J
∴ The total mechanical energy is 24 Joules
EXAMPLE 2.14Roller Coaster Conservation
Given: A roller coaster car of mass 500 kg starts from rest at the top of a hill 20 m high. It reaches the bottom of the hill. (g = 10 m/s², no friction)
Find: The speed at the bottom.
Solution
Step 1
At top: KE₁ = 0 (at rest), PE₁ = mgh = 500 × 10 × 20 = 100,000 J
Step 2
At bottom: PE₂ = 0, KE₂ = ½mv²
Step 3
Conservation of ME: 100,000 = ½ × 500 × v²
100,000 = 250v²
Step 4
Solve: v² = 100,000 ÷ 250 = 400
v = √400 = 20 m/s
∴ The speed at the bottom of the hill is 20 m/s
  • Mechanical energy: ME = KE + PE = ½mv² + mgh
  • In the absence of friction and other non-conservative forces, ME is conserved.
  • As an object falls: PE decreases and KE increases, but ME stays constant.
  • As an object rises: KE decreases and PE increases, but ME stays constant.
  • Friction converts ME into heat, so ME decreases in real systems.

Chapter Three

Power

Two workers may do the same amount of work, but one may do it in half the time. The concept of power tells us how quickly work is done. Power is the rate of doing work or the rate of energy transfer.

SECTION 3.1
Basic Concept of Power

Power is defined as the rate at which work is done, or equivalently, the rate at which energy is transferred or transformed.

P = W ÷ t
Where:
P = Power (Watts, W)
W = Work done (Joules, J)
t = Time taken to do the work (seconds, s)

3.1.1 The Unit of Power — the Watt

The SI unit of power is the Watt (W), named after James Watt. One Watt is defined as one Joule of work done per second:

1 Watt = 1 Joule per second = 1 J/s

Larger units: 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1,000 W; 1 megawatt (MW) = 1,000,000 W

3.1.2 Power as a Scalar

Like work and energy, power is a scalar quantity. It tells you how fast work is being done, but not in which direction.

EXAMPLE 3.1Power of a Lifting Motor
Given: A motor lifts a load and does 6,000 J of work in 30 seconds.
Find: The power of the motor.
Solution
Step 1
Known: W = 6,000 J, t = 30 s
Step 2
Formula: P = W ÷ t
Step 3
Substitute: P = 6,000 ÷ 30
Step 4
Calculate: P = 200 W
∴ The power of the motor is 200 Watts
EXAMPLE 3.2Power of a Climbing Person
Given: A person of mass 65 kg climbs a vertical height of 4 m in 8 seconds. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The power developed by the person.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 65 kg, h = 4 m, t = 8 s, g = 10 m/s²
Step 2
First calculate the work done (against gravity):
W = mgh = 65 × 10 × 4 = 2,600 J
Step 3
Apply the power formula: P = W ÷ t
Step 4
Substitute: P = 2,600 ÷ 8
Step 5
Calculate: P = 325 W
∴ The power developed is 325 Watts
EXAMPLE 3.3Finding Time from Power and Work
Given: A machine has a power output of 500 W and does 15,000 J of work.
Find: The time taken.
Solution
Step 1
Known: P = 500 W, W = 15,000 J, t = ?
Step 2
Formula: P = W ÷ t
Step 3
Rearrange: t = W ÷ P
Step 4
Substitute: t = 15,000 ÷ 500
Step 5
Calculate: t = 30 s
∴ The time taken is 30 seconds
EXAMPLE 3.4Finding Work Done from Power
Given: An electric motor operates at 750 W for 2 minutes.
Find: The total work done by the motor.
Solution
Step 1
Known: P = 750 W, t = 2 min = 2 × 60 = 120 s
Step 2
Formula: P = W ÷ t → rearranged: W = P × t
Step 3
Substitute: W = 750 × 120
Step 4
Calculate: W = 90,000 J = 90 kJ
∴ The total work done is 90,000 Joules (90 kJ)
  • Power is the rate of doing work: P = W/t
  • The SI unit of power is the Watt (W): 1 W = 1 J/s
  • Power is a scalar quantity.
  • Rearranging: W = P × t and t = W/P
  • A powerful machine does the same work in less time, not necessarily more work.
  • 1 kW = 1,000 W; 1 MW = 1,000,000 W
SECTION 3.2
Average vs Instantaneous Power

Average power is the total work done divided by the total time taken. It is used when the power varies over a period of time.

Instantaneous power is the power at a specific instant in time. It is found using the rate of work at that exact moment.

Pavg = Wtotal ÷ ttotal
P_avg = Average power over a time interval (W)
W_total = Total work done in that interval (J)
t_total = Total time for that interval (s)
Pinst = F × v
Derivation: P = W/t = (F × d)/t = F × (d/t) = F × v

P_inst = Instantaneous power (W)
F = Force applied at that instant (N)
v = Instantaneous velocity at that moment (m/s)
EXAMPLE 3.5Average Power Over Multiple Stages
Given: A machine does 4,000 J in the first 10 s, then 6,000 J in the next 15 s.
Find: The average power over the entire 25 seconds.
Solution
Step 1
Calculate the total work done across both stages.
W_total = 4,000 + 6,000 = 10,000 J
Step 2
Calculate the total time.
t_total = 10 + 15 = 25 s
Step 3
Apply the average power formula.
P_avg = W_total ÷ t_total
Step 4
Substitute.
P_avg = 10,000 ÷ 25
Step 5
Calculate.
P_avg = 400 W
∴ The average power is 400 Watts
EXAMPLE 3.6Instantaneous Power of a Car Engine
Given: A car engine exerts a force of 3,000 N at an instant when the car's speed is 25 m/s.
Find: The instantaneous power of the engine at that moment.
Solution
Step 1
Known: F = 3,000 N, v = 25 m/s
Step 2
Formula for instantaneous power: P = Fv
Step 3
Substitute: P = 3,000 × 25
Step 4
Calculate: P = 75,000 W = 75 kW
∴ The instantaneous power is 75,000 W (75 kW)
  • Average power = total work done ÷ total time: P_avg = W/t
  • Instantaneous power = force × velocity at that instant: P = Fv
  • Use average power when power varies over time and you need an overall measure.
  • Use instantaneous power (P = Fv) when you need the power at one specific moment.
  • If power is constant throughout, average power = instantaneous power.
SECTION 3.3
Power and Velocity

The formula P = Fv shows the direct relationship between power, force, and velocity. This is particularly useful for analysing the power output of engines and motors at different speeds.

At constant power, if the speed increases, the force must decrease (and vice versa). This is why cars have different gears — lower gears provide more force but less speed; higher gears provide less force but more speed.

EXAMPLE 3.7Maximum Speed of a Car
Given: A car engine has a constant power output of 60,000 W. The total resistive force (friction + air resistance) at maximum speed is 2,000 N.
Find: The maximum speed of the car.
Solution
Step 1
At maximum speed, the driving force equals the resistive force (no acceleration).
F = 2,000 N, P = 60,000 W
Step 2
Formula: P = Fv
Step 3
Rearrange for v: v = P ÷ F
Step 4
Substitute: v = 60,000 ÷ 2,000
Step 5
Calculate: v = 30 m/s
∴ The maximum speed of the car is 30 m/s
EXAMPLE 3.8Finding Force from Power and Speed
Given: A boat engine delivers 12,000 W of power when the boat travels at 6 m/s.
Find: The driving force exerted by the engine.
Solution
Step 1
Known: P = 12,000 W, v = 6 m/s, F = ?
Step 2
Formula: P = Fv
Step 3

Step 4

Step 5

∴ The driving force is 2,000 Newtons
  • Instantaneous power relates force and velocity: P = Fv
  • Rearranging: F = P/v and v = P/F
  • At constant power, force and velocity are inversely proportional: as speed increases, force decreases.
  • Maximum speed occurs when driving force equals resistive force (zero net force, zero acceleration).
SECTION 3.4
Efficiency

No machine or engine converts all its input energy into useful output energy. Some energy is always wasted, usually as heat or sound. Efficiency is a measure of how well a machine converts input energy into useful output energy.

η = (Euseful out ÷ Etotal in) × 100%
Where:
η (eta) = Efficiency (expressed as a percentage, %)
E_useful out = Useful output energy (J)
E_total in = Total input energy (J)

Also written in terms of power:
η = (P_out ÷ P_in) × 100%
Key principle: Efficiency is always less than 100% for real machines. A 100% efficient machine is an ideal that does not exist in practice because energy is always lost as heat due to friction.
EXAMPLE 3.9Efficiency of an Electric Motor
Given: An electric motor receives 5,000 J of electrical energy and produces 3,500 J of mechanical energy.
Find: The efficiency of the motor.
Solution
Step 1
Known: E_in = 5,000 J, E_out = 3,500 J
Step 2
Formula: η = (E_out ÷ E_in) × 100%
Step 3
Substitute: η = (3,500 ÷ 5,000) × 100%
Step 4
Divide first: 3,500 ÷ 5,000 = 0.7
Step 5
Multiply by 100: η = 0.7 × 100% = 70%
∴ The efficiency of the motor is 70% (30% of energy is wasted as heat)
EXAMPLE 3.10Finding Useful Output from Efficiency
Given: A petrol engine with 35% efficiency receives 80,000 J of chemical energy.
Find: The useful mechanical energy output.
Solution
Step 1
Known: η = 35%, E_in = 80,000 J, E_out = ?
Step 2
Formula: η = (E_out ÷ E_in) × 100%
Step 3
Rearrange: E_out = (η ÷ 100) × E_in
Step 4
Substitute: E_out = (35 ÷ 100) × 80,000
E_out = 0.35 × 80,000
Step 5
Calculate: E_out = 28,000 J
∴ The useful mechanical energy output is 28,000 Joules (28 kJ)
EXAMPLE 3.11Power Efficiency
Given: A pump has an input power of 2,000 W and an output power of 1,600 W.
Find: The efficiency of the pump.
Solution
Step 1
Known: P_in = 2,000 W, P_out = 1,600 W
Step 2
Formula: η = (P_out ÷ P_in) × 100%
Step 3
Substitute: η = (1,600 ÷ 2,000) × 100%
Step 4
Divide: 1,600 ÷ 2,000 = 0.8
Step 5
Multiply: η = 0.8 × 100% = 80%
∴ The efficiency of the pump is 80%
  • Efficiency = (Useful output energy / Total input energy) × 100%: η = (E_out/E_in) × 100%
  • Efficiency is always less than 100% for real machines.
  • Wasted energy = Input energy − Useful output energy.
  • Efficiency can also be expressed as: η = (P_out/P_in) × 100%
  • The higher the efficiency, the less energy is wasted.
  • Friction is the main cause of energy waste in mechanical systems.
SECTION 3.5
Horsepower

Before the Watt was adopted as the SI unit, engineers used a unit called horsepower (hp) to measure power. James Watt introduced this unit to help customers understand the power of his steam engines by comparing them to horses.

1 hp = 746 W
Conversion formulas:
Power in watts = Power in hp × 746
Power in hp = Power in watts ÷ 746

3.5.1 Historical Origin

James Watt observed that a horse could turn a mill wheel approximately 2.4 times per minute, with a force of about 180 pounds, lifting material a distance of approximately 12 feet per revolution. This gave approximately 33,000 foot-pounds of work per minute, which Watt defined as 1 horsepower. Today, 1 horsepower is standardised at exactly 745.7 watts (commonly rounded to 746 W).

EXAMPLE 3.12Converting Horsepower to Watts
Given: A car engine is rated at 150 hp.
Find: The power output in Watts and kilowatts.
Solution
Step 1
Known: Power = 150 hp
Conversion: 1 hp = 746 W
Step 2
Formula: P (W) = hp × 746
Step 3
Substitute: P = 150 × 746
Step 4
Calculate: P = 111,900 W
Step 5
Convert to kW: P = 111,900 ÷ 1,000 = 111.9 kW
∴ 150 hp = 111,900 W ≈ 111.9 kW
EXAMPLE 3.13Converting Watts to Horsepower
Given: An electric motor has a power output of 37,300 W.
Find: The power in horsepower.
Solution
Step 1
Known: P = 37,300 W
Step 2
Conversion: P (hp) = P (W) ÷ 746
Step 3
Substitute: P = 37,300 ÷ 746
Step 4
Calculate: P = 50 hp
∴ The motor's power is 50 horsepower (50 hp)
  • 1 horsepower (hp) = 746 Watts
  • To convert hp → W: multiply by 746.
  • To convert W → hp: divide by 746.
  • Horsepower is still widely used for car engines, electric motors, and industrial machinery.
  • The SI unit (Watt) is preferred in scientific contexts; hp is used in engineering and everyday contexts.

Chapter Four

The Connections

Work, Energy, and Power are not separate islands. They are deeply interconnected through fundamental laws of physics. This chapter explores these connections in depth and shows how they combine to solve complex problems.

SECTION 4.1
The Work–Energy Theorem (Full Treatment)

We now state the Work-Energy Theorem in its most complete and general form. This theorem is more powerful than Newton's laws in many situations because it involves scalar quantities (energy) and avoids vector analysis.

Wnet = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
The net work done by ALL forces on an object equals the change in the object's kinetic energy.

Combined with energy conservation:
W_net = ΔKE + ΔPE + W_friction
(where W_friction represents energy dissipated by non-conservative forces)
EXAMPLE 4.1Work-Energy Theorem with Friction
Given: A 10 kg block slides 5 m down an incline. It starts from rest and reaches a speed of 6 m/s at the bottom. The height of the incline is 3 m. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The work done by friction.
Solution
Step 1
Known: m = 10 kg, u = 0, v = 6 m/s, h = 3 m, g = 10 m/s²
Step 2
Calculate the initial GPE (taking the bottom as reference):
GPE_i = mgh = 10 × 10 × 3 = 300 J
Step 3
Calculate the final KE:
KE_f = ½mv² = ½ × 10 × 36 = 180 J
Step 4
By energy conservation with friction:
GPE_i = KE_f + |W_friction|
300 = 180 + |W_friction|
Step 5
Solve for work done by friction:
|W_friction| = 300 − 180 = 120 J
Since friction removes energy: W_friction = −120 J
∴ Friction does −120 J of work (120 J of energy lost to heat)
EXAMPLE 4.2Full Energy Analysis — Projectile
Given: A 0.3 kg ball is thrown vertically upward with an initial speed of 20 m/s. (g = 10 m/s², ignore air resistance)
Find: (a) Initial KE, (b) Maximum height, (c) Speed at 15 m height.
Solution — Part (a)
Step 1
Initial KE: KE_i = ½ × 0.3 × 20² = ½ × 0.3 × 400 = 60 J
Solution — Part (b): Maximum height
Step 2
At max height, v = 0. All KE converts to GPE:
KE_i = mgh_max
60 = 0.3 × 10 × h_max
60 = 3 h_max
h_max = 20 m
Solution — Part (c): Speed at h = 15 m
Step 3
GPE at 15 m: GPE = mgh = 0.3 × 10 × 15 = 45 J
Step 4
KE at 15 m: KE = KE_i − GPE = 60 − 45 = 15 J
Step 5
Speed: 15 = ½ × 0.3 × v² → v² = 100 → v = 10 m/s
∴ (a) Initial KE = 60 J, (b) Max height = 20 m, (c) Speed at 15 m = 10 m/s
  • The Work-Energy Theorem is: W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
  • It applies to all forces combined — the NET work equals the change in KE.
  • With friction: Initial ME = Final ME + Energy lost to friction
  • Energy lost to friction = |W_friction| = Initial ME − Final ME
  • The theorem works for any path — curved, straight, or inclined.
SECTION 4.2
Conservation of Energy and Power Together

Power tells us how fast energy is transferred or converted. When combined with conservation of energy, we can solve problems that involve both the quantity and the rate of energy changes.

EXAMPLE 4.3Power Needed to Maintain Speed on a Hill
Given: A 1,000 kg car moves at a constant speed of 20 m/s up a slope that rises 1 m for every 10 m along the slope. The frictional resistance is 500 N. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: The power output of the engine.
Solution
Step 1
The component of gravity along the slope (opposing motion going uphill):
sin θ = 1/10 = 0.1
F_gravity_along_slope = mg sinθ = 1000 × 10 × 0.1 = 1,000 N
Step 2
Total resistive force:
F_total = F_gravity_along + F_friction = 1,000 + 500 = 1,500 N
Step 3
At constant speed, engine force = total resistance:
F_engine = 1,500 N
Step 4
Power: P = Fv = 1,500 × 20
Step 5
Calculate: P = 30,000 W = 30 kW
∴ The engine power required is 30,000 W (30 kW)
  • Power and energy conservation combine when asking "how fast must energy be supplied?"
  • At constant speed: engine force = total resistive force, then P = Fv.
  • Energy must be supplied to: (1) do work against friction, and (2) increase gravitational PE when climbing.
  • The rate at which these energies must be supplied is the required power.
SECTION 4.3
Efficiency — The Full Picture

Efficiency connects all three concepts — work, energy, and power — into a single measure of how effectively a system operates. A thorough understanding of efficiency is crucial for engineering and environmental science.

η = (Useful Output / Total Input) × 100%
This can be expressed in three equivalent ways:
Energy form: η = (E_useful / E_input) × 100%
Power form: η = (P_useful / P_input) × 100%
Work form: η = (W_useful / W_input) × 100%
EXAMPLE 4.4Combined Efficiency Problem
Given: A crane motor uses 50,000 J of electrical energy to lift a 400 kg load to a height of 8 m. (g = 10 m/s²)
Find: (a) The useful work done, (b) The efficiency of the crane.
Solution
Step 1
Calculate the useful work done (work done against gravity):
W_useful = mgh = 400 × 10 × 8 = 32,000 J
Step 2
Total input energy: E_input = 50,000 J
Step 3
Apply efficiency formula:
η = (W_useful ÷ E_input) × 100%
Step 4
Substitute:
η = (32,000 ÷ 50,000) × 100%
Step 5
Calculate:
η = 0.64 × 100% = 64%
∴ Useful work = 32,000 J, Efficiency = 64%
  • Efficiency links work, energy, and power: η = (Output/Input) × 100%
  • Wasted energy always appears as heat (due to friction, air resistance, electrical resistance).
  • The higher the efficiency, the less energy is wasted per unit of useful work done.
  • No real machine has 100% efficiency — this would violate the laws of thermodynamics.
  • Improving efficiency = reducing energy losses = reducing wasted power = saving resources.

∴ Energy lost to friction = 110 Joules

∴ Input energy required = 150,000 J (150 kJ)
Explain in your own words why work, energy, and power always appear together in physics.
Work is the mechanism by which energy is transferred — it is energy in transit due to a force.
Energy is the capacity to do work — you cannot define work without energy, nor energy without work (they share the same unit, the Joule).
Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred — it answers "how fast?" for any work or energy process.
Together, they completely describe any physical process: what was transferred (energy), how it was transferred (work), and how quickly (power).
∴ They always appear together because they each describe a different dimension of the same physical event.
