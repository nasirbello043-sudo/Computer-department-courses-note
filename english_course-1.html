Chapter 01

Sound Patterns in English Language: Phonetics & Phonology

🔊
Phonetics is the scientific study of the physical properties of speech sounds — how they are produced (articulatory phonetics), transmitted (acoustic phonetics), and perceived (auditory phonetics). Phonology, by contrast, is the study of the sound systems of languages — how sounds function and contrast within a given language to create meaning.

1.1 What is Phonetics?

Phonetics deals with the concrete physical realization of sounds, regardless of their linguistic function. It concerns itself with three branches: articulatory phonetics (how speech sounds are produced by the vocal tract), acoustic phonetics (the physical properties of sound waves), and auditory phonetics (how sounds are perceived by the listener's ear and brain).

Every human language uses a subset of the possible sounds producible by the human vocal tract. The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) was developed to provide a standardized notation for all possible sounds.

1.2 What is Phonology?

Phonology is the abstract, cognitive level of sound study. While phonetics asks "what is this sound physically?", phonology asks "what role does this sound play in the language?" Phonology studies phonemes — the smallest units of sound that can change meaning in a language. For example, the sounds /p/ and /b/ are distinct phonemes in English because they differentiate minimal pairs like pat and bat.

1.3 Phonemes and Allophones

A phoneme is an abstract unit of sound that distinguishes meaning. An allophone is a variant pronunciation of a phoneme that does not change meaning. For example, the phoneme /p/ in English has two allophones: the aspirated [pʰ] in pot and the unaspirated [p] in spot. Native speakers perceive both as the same sound.

1.5 English Diphthongs — Click to Pronounce

Diphthongs are vowel sounds that glide from one position to another within a single syllable. English has 8 standard diphthongs. They are classified into closing diphthongs (ending in /ɪ/ or /ʊ/) and centring diphthongs (ending in /ə/).

1.6 English Consonants — Click to Pronounce

English consonants are classified by three features: place of articulation (where in the vocal tract the sound is made), manner of articulation (how airflow is modified), and voicing (whether the vocal cords vibrate).

1.7 Suprasegmental Features

Beyond individual sounds, phonology also studies suprasegmental features — aspects of speech that span more than one segment (sound):

  • Stress: The relative emphasis placed on syllables. E.g., REcord (noun) vs reCORD (verb).
  • Intonation: The rise and fall of pitch across an utterance, conveying meaning and attitude.
  • Tone: In tonal languages, pitch distinguishes lexical meaning. In English, it operates at discourse level.
  • Rhythm: English is a stress-timed language — stressed syllables occur at roughly equal intervals.

Chapter 02

English Word Classes: Parts of Speech

📚
Word classes (also called parts of speech or grammatical categories) are categories of words that share similar grammatical properties, functions within a sentence, and distributional patterns. English word classes are divided into lexical (open) classes and grammatical (closed) classes.

2.1 Lexical Classes (Open Class Words)

Lexical words carry the main semantic content of a sentence. They are called "open" because new words are constantly added to these categories. The four major open word classes in English are:

  • Nouns: Words that denote entities, objects, concepts, or ideas. They function as subjects, objects, or complements in sentences. They can be countable/uncountable, concrete/abstract, proper/common. E.g., university, freedom, professor, Lagos.
  • Verbs: Words that express actions, states, or events. They carry tense, aspect, and mood information. E.g., run, analyze, become, possess.
  • Adjectives: Words that modify nouns, providing descriptive or qualifying information. They can be attributive (before a noun: intelligent student) or predicative (after a copula: the student is intelligent).
  • Adverbs: Words that modify verbs, adjectives, other adverbs, or whole clauses, providing information about manner, time, place, degree, or frequency. E.g., quickly, very, always, unfortunately.

2.2 Grammatical Classes (Closed Class Words)

Grammatical words serve structural/functional roles rather than carrying primary lexical meaning. New words are rarely added to these categories.

ClassFunctionExamples

2.3 Forms of Words (Morphology)

Words change their form to express grammatical distinctions. This is studied in morphology. Key concepts include:

  • Inflection: Adding endings to modify grammatical features without changing word class. E.g., walk → walks, walked, walking (verb inflection); book → books (noun plural).
  • Derivation: Adding prefixes or suffixes to create new words, often changing word class. E.g., happy (adj) → happiness (noun); teach (verb) → teacher (noun).
  • Compounding: Combining two or more free morphemes to form a new word. E.g., blackboard, sunflower, textbook.

2.4 Functions of Words in Sentences

Every word in a sentence performs a grammatical function. A word's function is its role in a particular sentence — this is distinct from its class. For instance, a noun can function as subject, object, complement, or appositive.

FunctionDescriptionExample

2.5 Collocation

Collocation refers to the habitual co-occurrence of words — the tendency for certain words to appear together more frequently than by chance. Mastery of collocations is a hallmark of advanced English proficiency.

  • Verb-noun collocations: commit a crime, make a decision, take an opportunity
  • Adjective-noun collocations: heavy rain, strong argument, rich tradition
  • Adverb-adjective collocations: deeply concerned, highly significant, perfectly clear

Which process creates the word denationalization from nation?
Correct: C. The word undergoes multiple derivational steps: nation → national → nationalize → nationalization → denationalization. Each step adds a derivational affix changing the word class or meaning.

Words belonging to open word classes are called __________ words because they carry the primary meaning of a sentence.

Q3 True / False
The word class (category) of a word and its grammatical function in a sentence are always identical concepts.
False. Word class is a category (e.g., noun), while function is the role in a specific sentence (e.g., subject, object). The same word can have different functions in different sentences.

In the sentence "She gave the student the book," what grammatical function does "the student" perform?
Correct: B. The indirect object is the beneficiary of the action. "The student" receives the book (direct object). The ditransitive verb "gave" takes both objects.
Q5 Fill in the Blank
The habitual tendency of specific words to appear together — such as make a decision rather than do a decision — is called __________.
Answer: collocation. Collocations are statistically significant word pairings that native speakers use habitually, and mastery of them distinguishes advanced from intermediate learners.
Q6 Short Identification
Distinguish between inflection and derivation in morphology. Provide two examples of each.
Model Answer: Inflection modifies a word's grammatical form without changing its class or core meaning: walk→walked (past tense), book→books (plural). Derivation creates a new word, often changing its class: teach (v)→teacher (n); happy (adj)→happiness (n). Inflection is grammatically conditioned; derivation is lexically creative.

Chapter 03

English Sentences: Structural and Functional Analysis

📐
A sentence is the largest grammatical unit in syntax. It is a complete, independent unit of expression conveying a proposition or speech act. Sentences can be analyzed from two perspectives: their structural form (what they look like grammatically) and their functional purpose (what communicative act they perform).

3.1 Structural Classification of Sentences

Simple Sentences

A simple sentence contains one independent clause — a subject and predicate that together express a complete thought. Despite being structurally simple, simple sentences can contain complex phrases.

Simple SentenceThe diligent student completed the research paper.

Compound Sentences

A compound sentence joins two or more independent clauses using a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) or a semicolon. Each clause could stand alone as a sentence.

Compound SentenceThe professor explained the theory, but the students remained confused.

Complex Sentences

A complex sentence contains one independent clause and at least one dependent (subordinate) clause. The subordinate clause is introduced by a subordinating conjunction (because, although, when, since, if, etc.) or a relative pronoun (who, which, that).

Complex SentenceAlthough the examination was difficult, most students who had studied consistently performed well.

Compound-Complex Sentences

The most structurally elaborate type, containing at least two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

Compound-ComplexWhen the results were announced, the lecturer smiled broadly, and the students erupted in celebration.

3.2 Functional Classification of Sentences

TypeFunctionExample
DeclarativeStates a fact or propositionLanguage is a system of arbitrary signs.
InterrogativeAsks a questionHave you completed the assignment?
ImperativeGives a command or instructionAnalyze the text carefully.
ExclamatoryExpresses strong emotionWhat a remarkable discovery this is!

3.3 Sentence Elements: A Deep Analysis

Every sentence can be broken down into constituent elements. Transformational-generative grammar (Chomsky) proposes that every sentence has both a deep structure (underlying semantic representation) and a surface structure (actual spoken/written form). Passive transformations, for instance, change surface but not deep structure.

  • Noun Phrase (NP): Determiner + (Adjective) + Noun + (Prepositional phrase)
  • Verb Phrase (VP): (Auxiliary) + Main Verb + (Complement/Object)
  • Prepositional Phrase (PP): Preposition + NP
  • Adjective Phrase (AdjP): (Intensifier) + Adjective + (Complement)

3.4 Clause Types

Clauses are the building blocks of sentences. They contain a subject and predicate. Independent clauses can stand alone. Dependent clauses serve noun, adjective, or adverb roles within larger sentences.

  • Noun clause: That she passed the exam surprised everyone. [subject function]
  • Adjective/Relative clause: The book that she recommended was outstanding. [modifies noun]
  • Adverbial clause: He revised thoroughly because the exam was approaching. [modifies verb]

Chapter 04

Grammar and Usage: Tenses, Mood, Modality, Concord & Aspects

⚙️
Grammar is the system of rules governing the structure of a language. Usage refers to how these rules are applied in real communicative contexts. The major grammatical categories of the English verb system include tense, aspect, mood, modality, and concord (agreement).

4.1 Tense

Tense is a grammatical category that locates events and states in time, primarily distinguishing past from present. English has two morphological tenses: past (marked by -ed or irregular forms) and present (unmarked or marked by -s for 3rd person singular). Future is expressed through modals or going to constructions.

4.2 Aspect

Aspect expresses how an event unfolds or extends in time, independent of tense. English has two grammatical aspects:

  • Perfective aspect (marked by have + past participle): views the event as a complete whole with current relevance — She has finished the report.
  • Progressive/Continuous aspect (marked by be + present participle): views the event as ongoing and unfolding — She is writing the report.
  • These can be combined: She has been writing for hours. (Perfect Progressive)

4.3 Mood

Mood expresses the speaker's attitude toward the content of the utterance — specifically, the speaker's assessment of reality, possibility, necessity, or desirability.

  • Indicative mood: States facts or asks questions about reality. The student passed the examination.
  • Imperative mood: Issues commands or requests. Submit your assignment by Friday.
  • Subjunctive mood: Expresses hypothetical, counterfactual, or desired states. If I were the professor, I would restructure the curriculum. / It is essential that every student attend.

4.4 Modality

Modality is expressed primarily through modal auxiliary verbs and encodes epistemic (knowledge/belief-based) or deontic (obligation/permission-based) meanings.

ModalEpistemic UseDeontic Use
mustShe must be the lecturer. (deduction)You must submit on time. (obligation)

may / mightIt might rain. (possibility)You may enter now. (permission)

4.5 Concord (Subject-Verb Agreement)

Concord (or agreement) refers to the grammatical relationship whereby a verb must agree with its subject in number and person. Violations of concord are among the most common grammatical errors in academic writing.

  • Basic rule: Singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs. The data show (not shows) — because "data" is technically plural.
  • Collective nouns: In American English, collective nouns (committee, team) take singular verbs. In British English, plural is acceptable.
  • Intervening phrases: The quality of the results is impressive — the verb agrees with "quality," not "results."
  • Neither…nor / Either…or: The verb agrees with the nearest subject. Neither the students nor the professor was ready.

GRAMMAR & USAGE · 6 QUESTIONS
Q1 Multiple Choice
Which tense/aspect combination is used in "By the time she arrives, he will have submitted the report"?
Correct: B. Future Perfect (will have + past participle) expresses an action that will be completed before a specified future point.
Q2 Fill in the Blank
The sentence "It is essential that every student __________ [attend/attends] all lectures" uses the __________ mood.
Answer: attend / subjunctive. The mandative subjunctive uses the base form of the verb (attend, not attends) in "that" clauses following verbs of recommendation, requirement, or necessity.
Q3 Multiple Choice
"You must submit your thesis" — which type of modality does must express here?
Correct: B. Deontic modality expresses obligation, permission, or prohibition. Here "must" imposes an obligation on the addressee.
Q4 True / False
In the sentence "Neither the dean nor the lecturers was present," the verb form "was" is grammatically correct.
False. With neither…nor, the verb agrees with the nearest subject. "The lecturers" (plural) is nearest to the verb, so the correct form is "were present."
Q5 Fill in the Blank
The progressive aspect marks a situation as __________ and ongoing, while the perfective aspect presents an event as a __________ whole.
Answer: unfolding / complete. Progressive = internal, unfinished perspective. Perfective = external, completed perspective with present relevance.
Q6 Short Identification
What is the difference between epistemic and deontic modality? Provide one sentence example for each using the modal verb may.
Model Answer: Epistemic modality expresses the speaker's degree of certainty or knowledge about a proposition: "She may be in the library" (= possibly she is). Deontic modality expresses permission, obligation, or prohibition: "You may submit your paper tomorrow" (= I give you permission). The same modal verb can express either meaning depending on context.
