Part 1 · Chapter 01

Electrostatics (Electric Charge)

Everything downstream in this book — fields, potential, capacitance, even current — is built on one idea: charge. This chapter is where that idea gets its foundation.

What electricity isTypes of charge+ / − chargesConductors & insulators Conservation of chargeQuantizationFrictionConductionInduction PropertiesApplicationsMisconceptions

1.1 What Electricity Actually Is

Start from matter, not from wires. Every atom is a tiny solar system: a nucleus of protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral) sits at the centre, while electrons (negative) orbit around it in a cloud. In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons, so the positive and negative charge cancel out exactly — the atom shows no net charge to the outside world.

Electricity is simply the physics of what happens when that balance is disturbed. Electrons are far lighter than protons and sit at the outer edge of the atom, so they are the ones that move — protons stay locked in the nucleus. Whenever electrons are transferred from one object to another, or made to flow continuously through a wire, we call the resulting phenomenon "electricity." Static electricity is charge sitting still on a surface; current electricity is charge in organised motion. This chapter deals with the first kind — the platform everything else stands on.

Intuition Think of protons as furniture bolted to the floor and electrons as people in the room. You never move the furniture to change how crowded a room feels — you move the people. Every charging process in this chapter is really just "moving electrons," never "moving protons."

1.2 Types of Charge — Positive and Negative

Benjamin Franklin, working before anyone knew what an electron was, arbitrarily labelled the two kinds of charge he observed as positive (+) and negative (−). It turned out (by chance) that the charge on the electron is what we call negative, and the charge on the proton is what we call positive. The labelling is a historical accident, but the physics behind it is exact:

  • Positive charge — carried by protons; associated with a deficit of electrons on an object.
  • Negative charge — carried by electrons; associated with a surplus of electrons on an object.

The single most important rule in electrostatics follows directly from this:

Core Law Like charges repel. Unlike charges attract. Two positives push apart, two negatives push apart, one positive and one negative pull together.

An object is described as neutral when its protons and electrons are equal in number, positively charged when it has lost electrons (protons now outnumber electrons), and negatively charged when it has gained electrons (electrons now outnumber protons). Notice the phrasing: a positively charged object didn't "gain positive charge" — it lost negative charge. Almost every charging process in this chapter is an electron transfer, and reading it that way will save you from a whole category of exam mistakes.

1.3 Conductors and Insulators

Materials differ enormously in how easily their electrons can move, and that single property — electron mobility — is what separates a conductor from an insulator.

PropertyConductorInsulator
Outer electronsLoosely bound, "free electrons" roam through the materialTightly bound to their atoms
Charge behaviourCharge spreads instantly across the whole surfaceCharge stays exactly where it was placed
ExamplesCopper, silver, aluminium, graphite, salt water, the human bodyRubber, glass, dry wood, plastic, air (mostly)
Everyday useWires, plugs, circuit tracksWire coating, handles, gloves

There is a third, in-between category — semiconductors (silicon, germanium) — whose conductivity can be switched on or off by temperature, light, or a small added impurity. They're covered properly in Chapter 7, but it's worth knowing now that "conductor vs insulator" is really a spectrum, not two isolated boxes.

1.4 Conservation of Charge

Charge cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred from one object to another. If you rub a balloon on your hair, the balloon doesn't manufacture new negative charge out of nowhere; it simply pulls electrons away from your hair. Count every charge before the process and every charge after, and the total is identical.

Written as a law: for any isolated system, Q_total(before) = Q_total(after). This is one of the deepest conservation laws in physics — no experiment has ever found an exception to it, and it underlies Kirchhoff's Current Law that you'll meet in Chapter 11.

1.5 Quantization of Charge

Charge doesn't come in any amount you like — it comes in fixed packets. The smallest possible unit of free charge is the charge on a single electron (or proton), denoted e:

Key Constant
e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ Celementary charge (magnitude on one electron or proton)
Q = n eany observable charge Q is a whole-number multiple n of e

This is what "quantization" means: charge is discrete, like coins, not continuous, like water. You can have 3e or 4e of charge on an object, but never 3.5e — there's no such thing as a fraction of an electron sitting free on a surface. (Quarks inside protons and neutrons carry fractional charge, but they're never found alone — this doesn't break the rule for any charge you can isolate.)

Worked Example

Q: A charged sphere carries −6.4 × 10⁻¹⁸ C. How many excess electrons does it have?

Solution: Number of electrons $n = \dfrac{|Q|}{e} = \dfrac{6.4\times10^{-18}}{1.6\times10^{-19}} = 40$.

The sphere has 40 more electrons than protons.

1.6 Charging by Friction

When two different insulating materials are rubbed together, one material's atoms hold their outer electrons more tightly than the other's. Electrons transfer from the material that holds them more weakly to the material that holds them more strongly. After separation, one object is left with a surplus of electrons (negative) and the other with a deficit (positive).

Classic example: rub a glass rod with silk. Silk holds electrons more tightly than glass, so electrons move from glass → silk. The glass rod becomes positive; the silk becomes negative. Rub a rubber rod with fur, and it's the other way round — fur loses electrons to rubber, so the rod becomes negative and the fur positive.

Memory Trick "Friction is a fight over electrons, and only one side wins." Whichever material grips its electrons tighter walks away negative; the loser walks away positive.

1.7 Charging by Conduction (Contact)

Touch a charged conductor to a neutral (or oppositely-signed) conductor, and charge flows across the contact point until the two objects share the same electric potential. Both objects end up charged with the same sign as the originally-charged one (if the second object started neutral).

For two identical conducting spheres, contact charging splits the total charge equally between them:

Conduction Between Identical Spheres
Q₁' = Q₂' = (Q₁ + Q₂) / 2charge shared equally after contact (identical spheres)

1.8 Charging by Induction

Induction charges an object without any contact at all, and — this is the detail students most often miss — the induced charge is opposite in sign to the charge that caused it. The steps:

  1. Bring a charged rod (say, negative) close to a neutral conductor, but don't touch it. Free electrons in the conductor are repelled and pushed to the far side.
  2. The near side of the conductor is now short of electrons (positive); the far side has a surplus (negative).
  3. While the rod is still held close, ground the conductor (touch it with a wire to the earth, or your finger). Electrons drain away to ground, since they're being repelled by the rod and have somewhere to escape to.
  4. Remove the ground connection first, then remove the rod. The conductor is left with a net deficit of electrons — it is now positively charged, opposite to the negative rod that induced it.
Common Exam Trap The order of steps 3 and 4 matters. If you remove the rod before breaking the ground connection, all the displaced electrons flow back and the object ends up neutral again. Ground wire off first, then the rod.

1.9 Properties of Electric Charge — Summary

  • Additivity: total charge on a system is the algebraic (signed) sum of all individual charges.
  • Conservation: charge is never created or destroyed, only transferred (Section 1.4).
  • Quantization: charge always exists as a whole-number multiple of e (Section 1.5).
  • Invariance: the charge on a particle does not change with its speed — unlike mass, charge is the same whether a particle is at rest or moving near the speed of light.
  • Two-valued: unlike mass (which is only positive), charge can be positive, negative, or zero.

1.10 Real-Life Applications

  • Photocopiers & laser printers — a drum is charged by induction/friction principles, toner (oppositely charged) sticks only where the drum holds charge.
  • Electrostatic precipitators — charge smoke/dust particles so they're attracted to a collecting plate, cleaning industrial exhaust.
  • Fuel truck grounding chains — dangling a metal chain lets accumulated static charge drain to the ground continuously, preventing a spark near flammable vapour.
  • Lightning — friction between rising and falling ice particles in a storm cloud separates huge amounts of charge; when the potential difference is big enough, it discharges as lightning.
  • Static cling & hair-raising after rubbing a balloon — direct, everyday demonstration of Section 1.6.

1.11 Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 "Charging creates charge." — No process in this chapter creates charge; every one of them (friction, conduction, induction) only moves electrons that already existed.
Misconception 2 "A positively charged object has extra positive particles on it." — Protons are fixed inside the nucleus and never move between objects at these energies. A positive object simply has fewer electrons than protons — it lost negative charge, it did not gain positive charge.
Misconception 3 "Induction requires touching." — Induction is defined by the absence of contact between the inducing rod and the object being charged; only the temporary grounding wire touches it.
Misconception 4 "Insulators can't be charged." — Insulators absolutely can be charged (that's exactly what happens in friction charging) — what they can't do is let that charge move freely once it's there.

Chapter Summary

Charge is a fundamental, conserved, quantized property carried by protons (+) and electrons (−). Conductors let charge move freely through them; insulators don't. Objects can be charged by friction (electron transfer through rubbing), conduction (electron transfer through contact), or induction (charge separation without contact, followed by grounding) — and in every single case, what's actually moving is electrons, never protons.

Part 1 · Chapter 02

Coulomb's Law & Superposition

Chapter 1 told you charges push and pull each other. This chapter puts a number on that push — and shows you what to do when there's more than two charges in the room.

HistoryCoulomb's LawInverse square lawMultiple charges SuperpositionVector additionSolved examplesGraphs

2.1 A Little History

In 1785, French physicist Charles-Augustin de Coulomb used a delicate torsion balance — a horizontal bar suspended by a thin fibre that twists in proportion to applied force — to measure the force between two small charged spheres. By varying the distance and the amount of charge, he found a strikingly clean pattern: the force depended on the product of the two charges and fell off with the square of the distance between them. That relationship now carries his name.

2.2 Coulomb's Law

For two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$, the magnitude of the electric force between them is:

Coulomb's Law
$F = k\dfrac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$k = Coulomb's constant
$k = 8.99\times10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$often rounded to 9 × 10⁹
$k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/N·m² (permittivity of free space)

The direction of the force lies along the straight line joining the two charges: repulsive (pushing apart) if the charges have the same sign, attractive (pulling together) if opposite. Coulomb's law and Newton's law of gravitation ($F = Gm_1m_2/r^2$) have the exact same mathematical shape — both are inverse-square laws — but the electric force is enormously stronger: the electric force between two protons is about 10³⁶ times their gravitational attraction.

2.3 The Inverse-Square Law

"Inverse square" means that if you double the distance between two charges, the force drops to a quarter of its original value; triple the distance, and the force drops to a ninth. This isn't unique to electricity — gravity, light intensity, and sound intensity all fall off the same way, because in each case the effect is spreading out over the surface of an expanding sphere (surface area ∝ r²).

Quick Check $F \propto 1/r^2$. If r → 2r, then F → F/4. If r → r/2, then F → 4F. Memorize this ratio pattern — it's tested constantly.

2.4 Force Between Multiple Charges & the Superposition Principle

Coulomb's law as written only handles two charges. The moment you have three or more, you need the superposition principle: the net force on any one charge is the vector sum of the individual Coulomb forces exerted on it by every other charge, calculated one pair at a time, as if none of the other charges existed.

Superposition
$\vec F_{net} = \vec F_{1} + \vec F_{2} + \vec F_{3} + \dots$vector sum of every pairwise force

2.5 Vector Addition in Practice

Because force is a vector, you cannot just add magnitudes when the charges aren't arranged in a straight line. The standard method: resolve every individual force into x- and y-components, sum the components separately, then recombine.

Component Method
$F_x = \sum F_i\cos\theta_i,\quad F_y=\sum F_i\sin\theta_i$sum components first
$F_{net}=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}$magnitude of resultant
$\theta=\tan^{-1}(F_y/F_x)$direction of resultant
Solved Example 1 — Straight Line

Q: Two point charges, $q_1=+2\,\mu C$ and $q_2=+3\,\mu C$, are 0.30 m apart. Find the force between them.

Solution: $F = k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2} = (8.99\times10^9)\dfrac{(2\times10^{-6})(3\times10^{-6})}{(0.30)^2} \approx 0.60\ \text{N}$, repulsive (both positive).

Solved Example 2 — Superposition on a Line

Q: Charges $q_1=+4\,\mu C$ and $q_2=-4\,\mu C$ sit at x = 0 and x = 0.4 m. Find the net force on a third charge $q_3=+1\,\mu C$ placed at x = 0.2 m (exact midpoint).

Solution: Distance to each is 0.2 m. $F_1 = k\dfrac{(4\times10^{-6})(1\times10^{-6})}{(0.2)^2}=0.90\ \text{N}$ (repulsive, pushes q₃ toward +x, away from q₁). $F_2$ has the same magnitude 0.90 N but is attractive, pulling q₃ toward q₂ (also +x direction). Both forces point the same way here, so $F_{net}=0.90+0.90=1.80\ \text{N}$ in the +x direction.

Solved Example 3 — Vector (Right-Angle) Case

Q: Charge $q_3=+2\,\mu C$ sits at the corner of a right angle, with $q_1=+3\,\mu C$ 0.3 m directly to its west and $q_2=+3\,\mu C$ 0.3 m directly to its south. Find the net force on $q_3$.

Solution: Each force: $F=k\dfrac{(3\times10^{-6})(2\times10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2}=0.60\ \text{N}$. $F_1$ points east (away from q₁), $F_2$ points north (away from q₂) — they're perpendicular. $F_{net}=\sqrt{0.60^2+0.60^2}=0.85\ \text{N}$, directed 45° between east and north.

2.6 Reading Force-vs-Distance Graphs

A graph of F against r for fixed charges is a curve that drops steeply at first and flattens out, following $F\propto 1/r^2$ — never a straight line. If you instead plot F against $1/r^2$, you get a straight line through the origin, with slope $k q_1 q_2$. Examiners often use this second version to test whether you understand the inverse-square relationship rather than just memorizing the formula.

$k=8.99\times10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$Coulomb's constant
$k=1/4\pi\varepsilon_0$ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²/N·m²
$\vec F_{net}=\sum \vec F_i$superposition principle

Electric Field & Electric Potential

Instead of asking "what force does charge A exert on charge B," this chapter asks a more powerful question: "what does charge A do to the space around it?" That's the electric field — and its energy cousin, electric potential.

Electric fieldField intensityField linesUniform field Point charge fieldElectric potentialPotential differenceEquipotential surfaces E and V relationWork done

3.1 What Is an Electric Field?

Every charged object modifies the space around it, so that any other charge placed nearby feels a force — even before it arrives. That modified region of space is the electric field. Formally, the electric field at a point is defined as the force per unit positive charge that a small "test charge" would feel if placed at that point:

Definition
$\vec E = \dfrac{\vec F}{q_0}$q₀ = small positive test charge, units: N/C or V/m

The field is a vector — it has both a magnitude and a direction at every point in space, and by convention it points in the direction a small positive test charge would be pushed.

3.2 Field Intensity Due to a Point Charge

Combining the field definition with Coulomb's law gives the field a distance r from a point charge Q:

Point Charge Field
$E = k\dfrac{|Q|}{r^2}$magnitude, points radially outward from +Q, inward toward −Q

3.3 Field Lines

Field lines are an imaginary drawing device — never physically real — used to visualize the field's direction and relative strength. Rules for drawing them:

  • Lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges (or run off to infinity).
  • The tangent to a line at any point gives the field's direction there.
  • Lines are drawn denser where the field is stronger, sparser where it's weaker.
  • Field lines never cross — if they did, the field would have two directions at once at that point, which is meaningless.

3.4 Uniform Electric Field

Between two large, oppositely charged parallel plates, the field lines are straight, parallel, and evenly spaced — the field has the same magnitude and direction everywhere between the plates (ignoring edge effects). This uniform field is a standard idealization used throughout electrostatics, especially for capacitors (Chapter 4):

Uniform Field (Parallel Plates)
$E = V/d$V = potential difference between plates, d = plate separation

3.5 Electric Potential

Electric potential is the electric "height" of a point in space — the potential energy per unit charge that a charge would have if placed there. Just like gravitational potential energy depends on height above the ground, electric potential energy depends on position within an electric field.

Electric Potential
$V = \dfrac{U}{q_0} = k\dfrac{Q}{r}$potential due to a point charge Q, at distance r; unit: volt (V)

Unlike the field, potential is a scalar — it has a magnitude and a sign, but no direction. This makes it much easier to add contributions from multiple charges: just add the numbers algebraically (with sign), no vector components needed.

3.6 Potential Difference

What matters in circuits and most electrostatics problems isn't the absolute potential at a point (which depends on an arbitrary reference, usually taken as zero at infinity) but the potential difference between two points — commonly called voltage:

Potential Difference
$\Delta V = V_B - V_A$work done per unit charge moving a test charge from A to B
$W = q\,\Delta V$work done by an external agent moving charge q through ΔV

3.7 Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface is a surface on which every point has the same electric potential. Two key geometric facts, both provable from the definitions above: (1) no work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface, since ΔV = 0 there; (2) field lines always cross equipotential surfaces at right angles. For a single point charge, the equipotentials are concentric spheres centred on the charge; for a uniform field between parallel plates, they are flat planes parallel to the plates.

3.8 Relation Between E and V

Field and potential are two views of the same underlying physics — the field is the negative rate of change (gradient) of potential with position:

E–V Relationship
$E = -\dfrac{dV}{dr}$field is the (negative) spatial rate of change of potential
$E=V/d$uniform-field special case (Section 3.4)

In plain language: the field points from high potential to low potential, and its strength tells you how quickly the potential is changing as you move through space. Where equipotential lines are packed close together, the field is strong; where they're spread out, the field is weak.

3.9 Work Done in an Electric Field

The work needed to move a charge between two points depends only on the potential difference and the charge moved — not on the path taken (the electric force is conservative, just like gravity):

Work-Energy
$W = q(V_B - V_A)$path-independent
$W = \Delta KE$if no other forces act, all work goes into kinetic energy
Numerical Example 1

Q: Find the electric field 0.5 m from a +6 μC point charge.

Solution: $E=k Q/r^2=(8.99\times10^9)(6\times10^{-6})/(0.5)^2\approx2.16\times10^5\ \text{N/C}$, directed radially outward.

Numerical Example 2

Q: Parallel plates 2 cm apart are held at a potential difference of 400 V. Find the field between them, and the force on an electron placed inside.

Solution: $E=V/d=400/0.02=2\times10^4\ \text{N/C}$. Force on electron: $F=eE=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2\times10^4)=3.2\times10^{-15}\ \text{N}$, directed opposite to E (electron is negative).

Numerical Example 3

Q: How much work is done moving a +2 μC charge from a point at 100 V to a point at 250 V?

Solution: $W=q\Delta V=(2\times10^{-6})(250-100)=3\times10^{-4}\ \text{J}$.

Capacitance

A capacitor is nothing more than two conductors, kept apart, storing equal and opposite charge. Simple as that sounds, capacitors show up in every phone charger, camera flash, and RAM chip you own.

CapacitorsParallel plate capacitorDielectricsEnergy stored Series combinationParallel combinationApplicationsWorked examples

4.1 What Is a Capacitor?

A capacitor is a device made of two conductors (called "plates," whatever their actual shape) separated by an insulating gap. When a potential difference V is applied across the plates, one plate accumulates charge +Q and the other −Q. The ratio of stored charge to applied voltage is a fixed property of the device called its capacitance:

Definition of Capacitance
$C = Q/V$unit: farad (F) = coulomb/volt

A farad is an enormous unit for everyday devices — practical capacitors are usually measured in microfarads (μF, 10⁻⁶), nanofarads (nF, 10⁻⁹), or picofarads (pF, 10⁻¹²).

4.2 The Parallel Plate Capacitor

The simplest and most-studied design: two flat conducting plates of area A, separated by a small distance d, with vacuum or air between them.

Parallel Plate Capacitance
$C=\varepsilon_0\dfrac{A}{d}$ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² F/m, vacuum/air gap

Three levers control capacitance: increasing plate area A increases C (more room to store charge); increasing separation d decreases C (weaker field coupling between the plates); and — as the next section shows — filling the gap with an insulating material increases C.

4.3 Dielectrics

A dielectric is an insulating material (glass, mica, plastic, ceramic) inserted between the plates. The molecules of the dielectric become slightly polarized by the field, producing a small internal field that opposes the plates' field, weakening the net field for the same charge — which means more charge can be stored for the same voltage.

Capacitor with Dielectric
$C = \kappa\varepsilon_0\dfrac{A}{d}$κ (kappa) = dielectric constant, always > 1
κ(air)≈1, κ(paper)≈3.7, κ(glass)≈5–10, κ(mica)≈5–7typical dielectric constants

4.4 Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Charging a capacitor takes work, because each new bit of charge added has to fight the repulsion of the charge already there. That work is stored as electrical potential energy in the field between the plates:

Energy Stored
$U=\tfrac12 QV = \tfrac12 CV^2 = \dfrac{Q^2}{2C}$three equivalent forms — use whichever variables you know

4.5 Capacitors in Series

In series, capacitors share the same charge Q (charge has nowhere else to go), but voltage splits across them:

Series Combination
$\dfrac{1}{C_{eq}}=\dfrac{1}{C_1}+\dfrac{1}{C_2}+\dots$reciprocals add — equivalent C is smaller than the smallest individual C

4.6 Capacitors in Parallel

In parallel, capacitors share the same voltage V (both ends tied to the same two nodes), but charge splits across them according to each one's capacitance:

Parallel Combination
$C_{eq}=C_1+C_2+\dots$capacitances add directly — equivalent C is larger than the largest individual C
Memory Trick Capacitors and resistors are mirror images of each other: series capacitors combine like parallel resistors (reciprocals add), and parallel capacitors combine like series resistors (direct sum). You'll see the resistor version in Chapter 10.

4.7 Applications

  • Camera flash — charges slowly from a battery, then dumps its stored energy almost instantly through the flash bulb.
  • Smoothing/filtering in power supplies — capacitors smooth out ripples in rectified AC, delivering steadier DC.
  • RAM (DRAM) — each bit of computer memory is stored as charge on a tiny capacitor.
  • Touchscreens — capacitive touchscreens detect your finger by the small change in capacitance it causes.
  • Timing circuits — RC (resistor-capacitor) circuits set the timing for blinkers, oscillators, and debounce circuits.
Worked Example 1

Q: A parallel plate capacitor has plates of area 0.02 m² separated by 1 mm of air. Find its capacitance.

Solution: $C=\varepsilon_0 A/d = (8.85\times10^{-12})(0.02)/(0.001) = 1.77\times10^{-10}\ \text{F} = 177\ \text{pF}$.

Worked Example 2

Q: The same capacitor is now filled with a dielectric of κ = 4. Find the new capacitance, and the energy stored at 12 V.

Solution: $C' = \kappa C = 4\times177\ \text{pF}=708\ \text{pF}$. Energy: $U=\tfrac12 C'V^2=\tfrac12(708\times10^{-12})(12)^2\approx5.1\times10^{-8}\ \text{J}$.

Worked Example 3

Q: Two capacitors, 2 μF and 6 μF, are connected (a) in series and (b) in parallel. Find the equivalent capacitance in each case.

Solution (a): $1/C_{eq}=1/2+1/6=2/3\Rightarrow C_{eq}=1.5\,\mu F$. (b): $C_{eq}=2+6=8\,\mu F$.

Electric Dipoles

Most molecules — including water — aren't net-charged, yet they still feel and create electric effects. The reason is the dipole: two equal, opposite charges separated by a small distance.

DefinitionDipole momentTorquePotential energy Dipole in a fieldField due to dipoleApplications

5.1 Definition

An electric dipole is a pair of equal and opposite point charges, +q and −q, separated by a small fixed distance $2a$ (or often written as d). The system is net-neutral overall (+q and −q cancel), but because the charges are offset in space, it still produces and responds to electric fields in ways a truly neutral, symmetric object could not.

5.2 Dipole Moment

The dipole moment $\vec p$ is a vector that captures both "how strong" and "which way" the dipole is:

Dipole Moment
$\vec p = q\,\vec d$magnitude p = q·d; direction from −q to +q; unit: C·m

5.3 Torque on a Dipole in an External Field

Place a dipole in a uniform external field $\vec E$ and the two charges feel equal and opposite forces, $qE$ and $-qE$ — since the forces are equal and opposite but act at different points, there's zero net force but a non-zero net torque, which tries to rotate the dipole to align $\vec p$ with $\vec E$:

Torque
$\vec\tau = \vec p \times \vec E$magnitude: $\tau = pE\sin\theta$, θ = angle between p and E

Torque is maximum when the dipole is perpendicular to the field (θ = 90°) and zero when it's already aligned (θ = 0°) or anti-aligned (θ = 180°).

5.4 Potential Energy of a Dipole in a Field

Potential Energy
$U = -\vec p\cdot\vec E = -pE\cos\theta$minimum (most stable) at θ=0°, maximum (least stable) at θ=180°

This is exactly why polar molecules like water line up with an applied field (and why microwave ovens work — the oscillating field constantly flips direction, forcing water molecules to keep re-rotating, and that molecular friction generates heat).

5.5 Field Due to a Dipole

Unlike a single point charge (field ∝ 1/r²), a dipole's field falls off faster — as 1/r³ — because the +q and −q contributions almost, but not quite, cancel at a distance.

Dipole Field (idealized, far from the dipole)
$E_{axial} = \dfrac{2kp}{r^3}$along the dipole's axis, extended line through both charges
$E_{equatorial} = \dfrac{kp}{r^3}$along the perpendicular bisector of the dipole

Note the axial field is exactly twice the equatorial field at the same distance r, and both directions are opposite: axial field points the same way as $\vec p$, equatorial field points opposite to $\vec p$.

5.6 Applications

  • Polar molecules — water (H₂O) is a permanent dipole, which is why it's such an effective solvent for ionic compounds and why it interacts so strongly with electric fields.
  • Microwave heating — rapidly oscillating microwave fields torque water dipoles back and forth, generating heat through molecular friction.
  • Dielectric polarization — the microscopic explanation for why dielectrics increase capacitance (Chapter 4): the material's molecules act as tiny induced dipoles that partially cancel the applied field.
  • Antenna theory — the simplest radio antenna, the "dipole antenna," is literally two charged rod segments driven out of phase, radiating exactly the pattern described here.
Solved Example

Q: A dipole with p = 4 × 10⁻⁹ C·m sits at 30° to a uniform field of 5 × 10⁴ N/C. Find the torque, and the potential energy.

Solution: $\tau = pE\sin\theta = (4\times10^{-9})(5\times10^4)\sin30° = 1\times10^{-4}\ \text{N·m}$. $U=-pE\cos\theta=-(4\times10^{-9})(5\times10^4)\cos30°\approx-1.73\times10^{-4}\ \text{J}$.

Energy in Electric Field

Chapter 4 showed a capacitor stores energy. This chapter zooms in further: that energy doesn't live "on the plates" — it lives in the field itself, spread through the space between them.

Electric potential energyEnergy densityEnergy storageField energyApplications

6.1 Electric Potential Energy — Recap and Extension

Recall from Chapter 3 that moving a charge q through a potential difference ΔV requires work $W = q\Delta V$, and that work becomes stored electric potential energy if no other force intervenes. For a system of point charges assembled from infinite separation, the total potential energy is the sum of the potential energy of every pair:

System of Two Point Charges
$U = k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r}$work needed to bring q₂ from infinity to distance r from q₁

6.2 Where Does the Energy "Live"?

Two equivalent ways to think about a charged capacitor's energy: (1) the "circuit" view — energy was supplied by the battery and is stored as charge separation on the plates; (2) the "field" view — energy is stored throughout the volume of space where the electric field exists, at a certain amount of energy per cubic metre. Both views give the same total energy, but the field view is the more fundamental one, because it also correctly describes energy stored in electromagnetic waves travelling through empty space, far from any capacitor plates at all.

6.3 Energy Density

The energy density u is the energy stored per unit volume of a region containing an electric field:

Energy Density
$u=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0 E^2$vacuum/air, units: J/m³
$u=\tfrac12\kappa\varepsilon_0 E^2$with a dielectric of constant κ present

Notice this depends only on the local field strength E, not on the shape of whatever conductors created that field — this is what makes the "energy lives in the field" view so powerful: you can compute total stored energy by integrating u over all of space, capacitor or no capacitor.

6.4 Deriving Capacitor Energy from Energy Density

For a parallel plate capacitor with field $E=V/d$ filling volume $\text{Volume}=A\cdot d$ between the plates:

Cross-Check with Chapter 4
$U=u\times(\text{Volume})=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0E^2(Ad)=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0\dfrac{V^2}{d^2}(Ad)=\tfrac12\left(\varepsilon_0\dfrac Ad\right)V^2=\tfrac12CV^2$same formula as Section 4.4 — consistent, as it must be

6.5 Applications of Field Energy

  • Capacitor energy storage — flash circuits, backup power, energy harvesting.
  • Electromagnetic waves — light, radio, and all EM radiation carry energy through empty space using exactly this field-energy-density concept (paired with a magnetic-field energy density term, covered in Part 2).
  • High-voltage insulation design — engineers must keep field strength E below a material's breakdown value, since energy density (and the risk of dielectric breakdown/sparking) rises with E².
  • Supercapacitors — engineered for very high surface area A and very thin effective d, maximizing both capacitance and total stored field energy.
Solved Example 1

Q: Find the energy density in a region where E = 2 × 10⁵ N/C (vacuum).

Solution: $u=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0E^2=\tfrac12(8.85\times10^{-12})(2\times10^5)^2\approx0.177\ \text{J/m}^3$.

Solved Example 2

Q: A parallel plate capacitor (A = 0.01 m², d = 2 mm) is charged to E = 10⁵ N/C. Find the total field energy stored.

Solution: Volume $=Ad=0.01\times0.002=2\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}^3$. $u=\tfrac12(8.85\times10^{-12})(10^5)^2\approx0.0443\ \text{J/m}^3$. $U=u\times\text{Volume}=0.0443\times2\times10^{-5}\approx8.85\times10^{-7}\ \text{J}$.

Conductors & Insulators

Chapter 1 introduced conductors and insulators at a glance. Here we go deeper — into semiconductors, free electrons, shielding, and the basic Gaussian reasoning that explains why charge on a conductor always sits on its outer surface.

ConductorsInsulatorsSemiconductorsFree electrons Electrostatic shieldingGauss concepts (basic)Applications

7.1 Conductors, Revisited

A conductor's outer (valence) electrons are only weakly bound to their parent atoms. In a metal, these electrons detach entirely and roam through the whole lattice as a shared "sea" of free electrons — this is why metals conduct so well, and also why they're shiny and malleable (the free-electron sea also explains those properties, though that's more chemistry than this course needs).

In electrostatic equilibrium (no charge is moving), a conductor has three defining properties, all of which follow logically from "electrons are free to move until forces balance":

  • The electric field inside a conductor is exactly zero. If it weren't, free electrons would keep moving in response to it — which contradicts the assumption of equilibrium.
  • Any net charge on an isolated conductor resides entirely on its outer surface, never in its interior.
  • The surface of a conductor is always an equipotential surface (in fact the whole conductor, inside and out, is at one single potential) — if it weren't, charge would flow along the surface until it was.

7.2 Insulators, Revisited

In an insulator, electrons are tightly bound to individual atoms or molecules and cannot roam freely. Any charge placed on an insulator stays exactly where it was placed — it cannot redistribute across the surface, and internal fields are not automatically cancelled the way they are in a conductor. This is why you can charge one spot of a balloon by rubbing and it stays localized there, unlike a metal sphere.

7.3 Semiconductors

Semiconductors — silicon and germanium are the classic examples — sit between conductors and insulators. At absolute zero they behave like insulators (no free carriers), but at room temperature a small number of electrons gain enough thermal energy to break free, leaving behind "holes" (missing electrons that behave like mobile positive charge carriers). Conductivity of a semiconductor can be dramatically increased by:

  • Raising temperature — more thermal energy frees more electrons (opposite of metals, whose conductivity drops slightly as temperature rises).
  • Doping — deliberately adding trace impurities that donate extra free electrons (n-type) or extra holes (p-type).
  • Light exposure — photons can also excite electrons free of their atoms (photoconductivity).

This tunability is exactly why semiconductors, not simple conductors, are the material behind transistors, diodes, and every modern computer chip.

7.4 Free Electrons — the Common Thread

Whether a material conducts, insulates, or does something in between comes down entirely to how many charge carriers are free to move and how easily. Metals: enormous numbers of free electrons, always mobile. Insulators: essentially zero free carriers. Semiconductors: a small, controllable number of free carriers. This single idea — carrier density and mobility — is the organizing principle behind the entire conductor/insulator/semiconductor spectrum.

7.5 Electrostatic Shielding

Because the field inside a conductor in equilibrium is always zero (Section 7.1), a hollow conductor shields its interior from any external electric field — no matter how strong the outside field is, the field inside the hollow cavity remains zero, as long as there's no charge inside the cavity itself. This is called a Faraday cage.

Everyday Faraday Cages A car during a lightning strike (the metal body conducts the charge around the outside, protecting occupants inside), a microwave oven's metal mesh door (keeps microwaves inside, doesn't let them leak out to you), and elevators or metal-heavy buildings weakening cell signal are all real-world Faraday cage effects.

7.6 Basic Gauss Concepts

A fuller treatment of Gauss's Law comes in Part 2 (Chapter 13, alongside Maxwell's Equations), but the basic idea is worth previewing here because it directly explains conductor behaviour. Gauss's Law relates the electric flux through any closed surface to the charge enclosed by that surface:

Gauss's Law (Preview)
$\Phi_E = \oint \vec E\cdot d\vec A = \dfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$flux through a closed surface ∝ enclosed charge

Draw an imaginary closed surface just inside the material of a charged conductor. Since $\vec E=0$ everywhere inside a conductor at equilibrium, the flux through this surface is zero — which by Gauss's Law means the enclosed charge must be zero too. Every bit of the conductor's net charge is therefore forced out onto the outer surface. This one argument is the rigorous proof behind the bullet points in Section 7.1.

7.7 Applications

  • Faraday cages — shielding sensitive electronics, MRI rooms, and lightning-safe vehicle bodies.
  • Doped semiconductors — every diode, transistor, and integrated circuit relies on controlled n-type/p-type doping.
  • High-voltage equipment casings — grounded conductive enclosures keep dangerous fields away from operators, exploiting the zero-field-inside property.
  • Coaxial cable shielding — the outer braided conductor in coax cable shields the inner signal wire from external electrical noise.

DC Circuits

Static charge is where the book started; from here on, charge is moving. This chapter introduces the vocabulary of circuits: current, voltage, resistance, power, EMF, and the instruments used to measure them.

Electric currentCurrent densityVoltageResistance PowerEnergyInternal resistanceEMFBatteriesMeasuring instruments

8.1 Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of charge through a conductor:

Current
$I = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$unit: ampere (A) = coulomb/second

By historical convention, current is defined as flowing in the direction positive charge would move — from + to − through the external circuit — even though in a metal wire it's actually negative electrons drifting the opposite way. This "conventional current" convention is used consistently throughout circuit analysis and never causes a wrong numeric answer as long as you apply it consistently.

8.2 Current Density

Current density J describes how concentrated the current is within a conductor's cross-section — useful when comparing thick and thin wires carrying the same current, or when field strength inside the conductor matters:

Current Density
$J = I/A$unit: A/m²; A = cross-sectional area of the conductor
$J = nqv_d$n = charge carrier density, q = carrier charge, v_d = drift velocity

8.3 Voltage

Voltage is exactly the potential difference from Chapter 3 — the energy per unit charge available to push current through a circuit element. In circuit diagrams, it's the "pressure" that drives charge flow, analogous to pressure difference driving water flow through a pipe.

8.4 Resistance

Resistance is a measure of how much a material opposes the flow of current — the electrical analogue of friction in a water pipe. Higher resistance means more voltage is needed to push the same current through.

Resistance (preview — Ohm's Law is Chapter 9)
$R = V/I$unit: ohm (Ω)

8.5 Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted (to heat, light, motion, etc.):

Power
$P = IV$general form, unit: watt (W)
$P = I^2R$equivalent, using Ohm's law V = IR
$P = V^2/R$equivalent, another substitution of Ohm's law

8.6 Energy

Electrical energy consumed over a time t is simply power multiplied by time:

Energy
$W = Pt = IVt$unit: joule (J); utility bills use kilowatt-hours (kWh) instead

8.7 EMF (Electromotive Force)

EMF (ε) is the total energy per unit charge supplied by a source (battery, generator) — not a force at all despite the name, but a potential difference measured with no current flowing (open-circuit voltage). Once current flows, the terminal voltage delivered to the external circuit is always slightly less than the EMF, because some energy is lost driving current through the source's own internal resistance.

8.8 Internal Resistance

Every real battery has some internal resistance r, due to the chemistry and materials inside it. This changes the relationship between EMF and the voltage actually delivered to a circuit:

Internal Resistance
$\varepsilon = IR + Ir = I(R+r)$ε = EMF, R = external (load) resistance, r = internal resistance
$V_{terminal} = \varepsilon - Ir$voltage actually available to the external circuit

This is why a battery's voltage sags under heavy load (large I) — more voltage is lost internally as Ir, leaving less for the circuit.

8.9 Batteries

A battery is a chemical energy source that maintains a potential difference between its terminals through electrochemical reactions. Batteries can be arranged in series (EMFs add, increasing total voltage) or parallel (EMFs stay the same, but total available current capacity increases, and effective internal resistance decreases).

Q: A battery with EMF 12 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω is connected to a 5.5 Ω resistor. Find the current and the terminal voltage.

Part 1 · Chapter 09

Ohm's Law

One of the most-quoted, most-misused equations in physics. This chapter nails down exactly what Ohm's law says, what it doesn't say, and what happens to resistance when things heat up.

Ohm's LawNon-ohmic devicesResistivityConductivity Temperature effectPower calculationsNumerical examples

9.1 Statement of Ohm's Law

For many conductors, at constant temperature, the current through them is directly proportional to the voltage applied across them:

Ohm's Law
$V = IR$R = resistance, constant for an "ohmic" material at fixed temperature

Crucially, Ohm's law is not a universal law of nature the way Coulomb's law or the conservation of charge are — it's an empirical (observed) approximation that holds for some materials under some conditions, and fails for others. Metals at constant temperature are good "ohmic" conductors; many other components are not.

9.2 Non-Ohmic Devices

A device is called non-ohmic if its V–I graph is not a straight line through the origin — meaning its resistance changes depending on the voltage or current applied.

  • Diodes — conduct easily in one direction, almost not at all in the other; wildly non-linear V–I curve.
  • Filament (incandescent) light bulbs — resistance rises noticeably as the filament heats up at higher current.
  • Thermistors — resistance changes sharply with temperature by design.
  • Transistors — highly non-linear, voltage-controlled resistance behaviour.
How to Tell From a Graph Ohmic device: V vs I is a straight line through the origin, constant slope = R. Non-ohmic device: the line curves, or the slope changes at different points — resistance is not constant.

9.3 Resistivity

Resistance R depends on the specific object's shape (length, cross-section) as well as the material. Resistivity ρ strips away the geometry and gives a pure material property:

Resistivity
$R=\rho\dfrac{L}{A}$L = length, A = cross-sectional area, ρ = resistivity (Ω·m)

Longer wires have more resistance (more material to push through); thicker wires have less resistance (more parallel "lanes" for current to flow).

9.4 Conductivity

Conductivity σ is simply the reciprocal of resistivity — a measure of how well a material conducts, rather than how much it resists:

Conductivity
$\sigma = 1/\rho$unit: siemens per metre (S/m)

9.5 Temperature Effect on Resistance

For most metals, resistivity increases roughly linearly with temperature — atoms vibrate more at higher temperature, colliding more often with the moving electrons and impeding their flow:

Temperature Dependence
$\rho_T = \rho_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]$α = temperature coefficient of resistivity

Semiconductors go the opposite way (Chapter 7): their resistivity decreases as temperature rises, since more thermal energy frees more charge carriers.

9.6 Power Calculations Under Ohm's Law

Combining $P=IV$ from Chapter 8 with $V=IR$ gives two more useful forms — already listed in Chapter 8's formula sheet, but worth restating here since they only strictly apply where Ohm's law (R constant) actually holds:

Power
$P=I^2R$use when I and R are known
$P=V^2/R$use when V and R are known
Numerical Example 1

Q: A 12 V battery drives 3 A through a resistor. Find the resistance and power dissipated.

Solution: $R=V/I=12/3=4\ \Omega$. $P=IV=3\times12=36\ \text{W}$.

Numerical Example 2

Q: A copper wire (ρ = 1.68×10⁻⁸ Ω·m) is 2 m long with cross-sectional area 1×10⁻⁶ m². Find its resistance.

Solution: $R=\rho L/A=(1.68\times10^{-8})(2)/(1\times10^{-6})=0.0336\ \Omega$.

Numerical Example 3

Q: A resistor has ρ₀ corresponding to R₀ = 10 Ω at 20°C, with α = 0.004 /°C. Find its resistance at 80°C.

Solution: $R=R_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]=10[1+0.004(60)]=10(1.24)=12.4\ \Omega$.

Resistor Combination

Real circuits are rarely a single resistor. This chapter builds the toolkit for reducing any network of series and parallel resistors down to one equivalent number — and for splitting voltage or current exactly where you want it.

SeriesParallelMixed circuitsVoltage dividerCurrent dividerEquivalent resistancePractical examples

10.1 Series Combination

Resistors in series are connected end-to-end, so the same current I flows through every one of them (there's no other path for it to take), while the total voltage splits across each resistor in proportion to its resistance.

Series
$R_{eq}=R_1+R_2+R_3+\dots$resistances add directly
$I$ same through allonly one current path
$V=V_1+V_2+V_3+\dots$voltages add up to the total

10.2 Parallel Combination

Resistors in parallel are connected across the same two nodes, so every resistor sees the same voltage V, while the total current splits between the branches — more current flows through the branch with lower resistance.

Parallel
$\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}}=\dfrac1{R_1}+\dfrac1{R_2}+\dots$reciprocals add
$V$ same across allsame two shared nodes
$I=I_1+I_2+I_3+\dots$currents add up to the total
$R_{eq}=\dfrac{R_1R_2}{R_1+R_2}$quick shortcut for exactly two resistors

The equivalent resistance of a parallel combination is always smaller than the smallest individual resistor — adding more parallel paths always makes it easier for current to flow overall.

10.3 Mixed (Series-Parallel) Circuits

Most real circuits combine both patterns. The standard method: identify the smallest self-contained series or parallel group, collapse it to a single equivalent resistor, redraw the simplified circuit, and repeat until only one resistor remains.

Reduction Strategy Work from the "leaves" of the circuit inward. Find resistors that are purely in series or purely in parallel with each other (not mixed with anything else yet), combine them first, then treat that combination as a single new resistor and repeat.

10.4 Voltage Divider

Two resistors in series across a voltage source split that voltage in proportion to their resistances — this is the basis of the voltage divider, used constantly to generate a smaller reference voltage from a larger supply:

Voltage Divider
$V_1=V\times\dfrac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}$voltage across R₁
$V_2=V\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}$voltage across R₂

10.5 Current Divider

Two resistors in parallel split the incoming total current in inverse proportion to their resistances (more current takes the path of least resistance):

Current Divider (two branches)
$I_1=I\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}$current through R₁ (note: uses the OTHER resistor on top)
$I_2=I\times\dfrac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}$current through R₂
Common Mix-Up In the current divider formula, the resistor value in the numerator belongs to the OTHER branch, not the one you're solving for. This is the opposite pattern from the voltage divider, and it's the single most common sign error in this topic.
Practical Example 1 — Series

Q: Three resistors, 2 Ω, 3 Ω, 5 Ω, are in series across a 20 V source. Find total resistance and current.

Solution: $R_{eq}=2+3+5=10\ \Omega$. $I=V/R_{eq}=20/10=2\ \text{A}$ (same through all three).

Practical Example 2 — Parallel

Q: Two resistors, 6 Ω and 3 Ω, are in parallel across a 12 V source. Find the equivalent resistance and the current through each branch.

Solution: $R_{eq}=\dfrac{6\times3}{6+3}=2\ \Omega$. $I_6 = 12/6=2\ \text{A}$, $I_3=12/3=4\ \text{A}$; total current from source = 6 A, consistent with $I=V/R_{eq}=12/2=6\ \text{A}$.

Practical Example 3 — Mixed Circuit

Q: A 4 Ω resistor is in series with a parallel pair of 6 Ω and 12 Ω, across a 24 V source. Find the total current from the source.

Solution: Parallel pair: $R_p=\dfrac{6\times12}{6+12}=4\ \Omega$. Total: $R_{eq}=4+4=8\ \Omega$. $I=24/8=3\ \text{A}$.

Part 1 · Chapter 11

DC Circuit Analysis

The capstone of Part 1. Series and parallel reduction (Chapter 10) breaks down once a circuit has multiple loops or multiple sources — this chapter gives you the systematic tools that handle any circuit, no matter how tangled.

Kirchhoff's Current LawKirchhoff's Voltage LawMesh analysisNode analysis Wheatstone bridgeMaximum power theorem

11.1 Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

KCL is nothing more than conservation of charge (Chapter 1) applied to a junction: charge can't pile up or vanish at a node, so whatever current flows in must flow back out.

Kirchhoff's Current Law
$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$at any junction (node)

11.2 Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

KVL is conservation of energy applied to a closed loop: if you walk all the way around any closed loop in a circuit and add up every voltage rise and drop, you must return to your starting potential — so the total must be zero.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law
$\sum V = 0$sum of all EMFs and IR drops around any closed loop
Sign Convention Pick a travel direction around the loop. Crossing a resistor in the direction of assumed current: voltage DROP (−IR). Crossing a battery from − to +: voltage RISE (+ε). Crossing a battery from + to −: voltage DROP (−ε). Stay consistent and the algebra takes care of the rest.

11.3 Mesh (Loop) Analysis

Mesh analysis assigns an unknown loop current to each independent loop in the circuit, then writes one KVL equation per loop. Solving the resulting system of simultaneous equations gives every loop current at once, from which every branch current and voltage can be found.

  1. Identify every independent loop (mesh) in the circuit and assign each a circulating current ($I_1, I_2, \dots$), usually all clockwise for consistency.
  2. Write a KVL equation for each mesh, in terms of the mesh currents (a resistor shared between two meshes carries the difference of their two mesh currents).
  3. Solve the simultaneous equations for all mesh currents.
  4. Convert mesh currents into actual branch currents as needed.

11.4 Node (Nodal) Analysis

Node analysis is the KCL-based counterpart: pick one node as a reference ("ground," defined as 0 V), assign unknown voltages to every other node, and write one KCL equation per unknown node using Ohm's law to express each branch current in terms of node voltages.

  1. Choose a reference (ground) node.
  2. Label the unknown voltage at every other node.
  3. At each unknown node, write KCL: sum of currents leaving = 0 (each current expressed via Ohm's law using the node voltages).
  4. Solve the simultaneous equations for all node voltages.
Choosing a Method Mesh analysis is usually faster for circuits with few loops and many nodes; node analysis is usually faster for circuits with many loops but few nodes. Both always give the same final answer — pick whichever produces fewer simultaneous equations for a given circuit.

11.5 The Wheatstone Bridge

A Wheatstone bridge is a diamond-shaped network of four resistors with a galvanometer bridging the middle, classically used to measure an unknown resistance with high precision. The bridge is "balanced" when no current flows through the galvanometer branch — which happens at one specific ratio of the four resistors:

Balance Condition
$\dfrac{R_1}{R_2}=\dfrac{R_3}{R_4}$equivalently: R₁R₄ = R₂R₃, at balance (zero galvanometer current)

In practice, three of the four resistances are precisely known (often one is an adjustable standard resistor), and the fourth (unknown) resistance is found by adjusting until the galvanometer reads zero, then solving the balance equation.

11.6 Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

Given a source with fixed internal resistance r, the load resistance R that extracts the maximum possible power from that source is exactly:

Maximum Power Transfer
$R_{load}=r_{internal}$condition for maximum power delivered to the load
$P_{max}=\dfrac{\varepsilon^2}{4r}$the maximum power itself, at that matched condition

Note this is a theorem about maximizing power delivered to the load, not about maximizing efficiency — at R = r exactly, only 50% of the total power generated actually reaches the load (the other 50% is lost heating the source's own internal resistance). This trade-off matters in audio amplifier design, antenna matching, and battery-powered devices, where sometimes efficiency is prioritized over raw power transfer.

Solved Example 1 — KVL, Single Loop

Q: A single loop has a 10 V battery, a 2 Ω resistor, and a 3 Ω resistor in series. Find the current.

Solution: KVL: $10 - I(2) - I(3) = 0 \Rightarrow 10 = 5I \Rightarrow I = 2\ \text{A}$.

Solved Example 2 — Mesh Analysis, Two Loops

Q: A circuit has two meshes sharing a 4 Ω resistor. Mesh 1 has a 12 V source and 2 Ω resistor; Mesh 2 has a 6 V source and 3 Ω resistor, both circulating clockwise. Set up (don't fully solve) the mesh equations.

Solution: Mesh 1: $12 - 2I_1 - 4(I_1-I_2) = 0$. Mesh 2: $-6-3I_2-4(I_2-I_1)=0$. These simplify to $6I_1-4I_2=12$ and $-4I_1+7I_2=6$, solvable simultaneously for $I_1$ and $I_2$.

Solved Example 3 — Wheatstone Bridge

Q: A Wheatstone bridge has R₁=10 Ω, R₂=20 Ω, R₃=15 Ω, and unknown R₄, balanced with zero galvanometer current. Find R₄.

Solution: $R_1/R_2=R_3/R_4\Rightarrow 10/20=15/R_4\Rightarrow R_4=15\times20/10=30\ \Omega$.

Solved Example 4 — Maximum Power

Q: A source has EMF 20 V and internal resistance 5 Ω. Find the load resistance for maximum power transfer, and the maximum power delivered.

Solution: $R_{load}=r=5\ \Omega$. $P_{max}=\varepsilon^2/4r = 400/20=20\ \text{W}$.
