1.1 Definition of a Function

A function is a rule that assigns to every input value exactly one output value. If $f$ is a function, and $x$ is a valid input, then $f(x)$ denotes the single output value that $f$ produces for that $x$. The key word is "exactly one" — a rule that gives one input two different outputs is not a function.

Vertical Line Test On a graph, if you can draw any vertical line that crosses the curve more than once, the curve is NOT a function of x — because that would mean one x-value has two different y-outputs.

1.2 Real Numbers and Intervals

The real numbers ℝ include all rational numbers (fractions of integers) and all irrational numbers (like $\sqrt2$ and $\pi$) — essentially every point on the number line. An interval is a connected stretch of the real line, written with brackets that show whether the endpoints are included:

1.3 Domain and Range

The domain of a function is the complete set of allowed input values (x-values) for which the function produces a valid, real output. The range is the complete set of output values (y-values) the function actually produces.

Three things routinely restrict a domain, and every domain question in this course reduces to checking for these:

  • Division by zero — any x that makes a denominator zero must be excluded.
  • Square roots (or any even root) of a negative number — the expression under the root must be $\ge 0$.
  • Logarithms of zero or a negative number — the argument of a log must be $>0$ (this becomes relevant once logarithmic functions appear later in the course).

Functions are also classified by symmetry: an even function satisfies $f(-x)=f(x)$ (symmetric about the y-axis, like $x^2$); an odd function satisfies $f(-x)=-f(x)$ (symmetric about the origin, like $x^3$). Most functions are neither.

1.5 Function Notation

$f(x)$ is read "f of x," and means "the output of function f when the input is x." It does NOT mean f multiplied by x. Substituting a specific value: $f(3)$ means replace every x in the formula with 3 and simplify.

1.6 Graphs of Functions

The graph of $y=f(x)$ is the set of all points $(x,f(x))$ plotted on the coordinate plane. Reading a graph well means being able to find $f(a)$ by locating $x=a$ and reading off the height of the curve there, and being able to solve $f(x)=b$ by locating the height $y=b$ and reading off the x-value(s) where the curve reaches it.

1.7 Composition of Functions

The composition $(f\circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ means: first apply g to x, then apply f to whatever g produced. Order matters enormously — $f(g(x))$ and $g(f(x))$ are generally different functions.

Composition
$(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))$apply g first, then f
Domain of $f\circ g$all x in the domain of g, such that g(x) is in the domain of f

1.8 Inverse Functions

The inverse $f^{-1}$ of a function f "undoes" f: if $f(a)=b$, then $f^{-1}(b)=a$. An inverse exists only if f is one-to-one (passes the horizontal line test — each output comes from only one input).

Finding an Inverse — Standard Method

Check: $f(f^{-1}(x))=x$ and $f^{-1}(f(x))=x$ must both hold for a correct inverse.

1.10 Real-Life Applications

  • Cost functions in economics: $C(x)$ = cost to produce x units.
  • Population growth models expressed as a function of time.
  • Conversion functions (e.g. Celsius to Fahrenheit, currency exchange).
  • Physics: position, velocity, and acceleration are all functions of time — the foundation for Chapter 2.

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Worked Example 1 — Finding the Domain of a Rational Function

Q: Find the domain of $f(x)=\dfrac{x+1}{x^2-9}$.

Step 1 — Identify what could restrict the domainThis is a rational function (a fraction), so the only restriction is: the denominator cannot equal zero.
Step 2 — Set the denominator equal to zero$x^2-9=0$.
Step 3 — Solve for x$x^2=9 \Rightarrow x=\pm3$. These are the values that must be excluded.
Step 4 — Write the domainThe domain is all real numbers except $x=3$ and $x=-3$: $\{x\in\mathbb R : x\ne\pm3\}$, or in interval notation, $(-\infty,-3)\cup(-3,3)\cup(3,\infty)$.
Worked Example 2 — Domain of a Square Root Function

Q: Find the domain of $f(x)=\sqrt{5-2x}$.

Step 1 — Identify the restrictionThis is a square root, so whatever is under the root must be greater than or equal to zero.
Step 2 — Set up the inequality$5-2x\ge0$.
Step 3 — Solve the inequalitySubtract 5 from both sides: $-2x\ge-5$. Divide both sides by $-2$, and remember to FLIP the inequality sign when dividing by a negative number: $x\le\dfrac52$.
Step 4 — Write the domain$x\le2.5$, or in interval notation, $\left(-\infty,\dfrac52\right]$.
Worked Example 3 — Evaluating a Function

Q: If $f(x)=2x^2-3x+4$, find $f(-2)$.

Step 1 — Write down the formula$f(x)=2x^2-3x+4$.
Step 2 — Replace every x with $-2$$f(-2)=2(-2)^2-3(-2)+4$.
Step 3 — Evaluate the exponent first$(-2)^2=4$, so we now have $2(4)-3(-2)+4$.
Step 4 — Carry out the multiplications$2(4)=8$ and $-3(-2)=6$, giving $8+6+4$.
Step 5 — Add the results$8+6+4=18$. So $f(-2)=18$.
Worked Example 4 — Composition of Functions

Q: Given $f(x)=x^2+1$ and $g(x)=2x-3$, find $(f\circ g)(x)$ and evaluate it at $x=2$.

Step 1 — Understand what's being asked$(f\circ g)(x)$ means $f(g(x))$ — plug the ENTIRE function g(x) into f wherever f has an x.
Step 2 — Write out g(x) to substitute$g(x)=2x-3$.
Step 3 — Substitute g(x) into f(x)$f(x)=x^2+1$ becomes $f(g(x))=(2x-3)^2+1$.
Step 4 — Expand $(2x-3)^2$$(2x-3)^2=(2x-3)(2x-3)=4x^2-6x-6x+9=4x^2-12x+9$.
Step 5 — Add the remaining +1$(f\circ g)(x)=4x^2-12x+9+1=4x^2-12x+10$.
Step 6 — Evaluate at x = 2$(f\circ g)(2)=4(2)^2-12(2)+10=4(4)-24+10=16-24+10=2$.
Worked Example 5 — Finding an Inverse Function

Q: Find the inverse of $f(x)=\dfrac{3x-2}{5}$.

Step 1 — Replace f(x) with y$y=\dfrac{3x-2}{5}$.
Step 2 — Swap x and yThis reflects the input/output roles: $x=\dfrac{3y-2}{5}$.
Step 3 — Clear the fractionMultiply both sides by 5: $5x=3y-2$.
Step 4 — Isolate the y-termAdd 2 to both sides: $5x+2=3y$.
Step 5 — Solve for yDivide both sides by 3: $y=\dfrac{5x+2}{3}$.
Step 6 — Write the final answer$f^{-1}(x)=\dfrac{5x+2}{3}$.
Step 7 — Verify (optional but recommended)Check $f(f^{-1}(x))$: $f\left(\dfrac{5x+2}{3}\right)=\dfrac{3\left(\frac{5x+2}{3}\right)-2}{5}=\dfrac{(5x+2)-2}{5}=\dfrac{5x}{5}=x$. ✓ Confirmed correct.
Worked Example 6 — Testing Even, Odd, or Neither

Q: Determine whether $f(x)=x^3-4x$ is even, odd, or neither.

Step 1 — Recall the testsEven: $f(-x)=f(x)$. Odd: $f(-x)=-f(x)$.
Step 2 — Compute f(−x)Replace every x with $-x$: $f(-x)=(-x)^3-4(-x)$.
Step 3 — Simplify each term$(-x)^3=-x^3$ and $-4(-x)=4x$. So $f(-x)=-x^3+4x$.
Step 4 — Compare to f(x) and to −f(x)$f(x)=x^3-4x$. Is $f(-x)=f(x)$? No, since $-x^3+4x\ne x^3-4x$ in general. Now check $-f(x)=-(x^3-4x)=-x^3+4x$.
Step 5 — Conclude$f(-x)=-x^3+4x=-f(x)$ — this matches exactly, so $f(x)$ is an odd function.
Worked Example 7 — Applying a Graph Transformation

Q: Starting from $f(x)=x^2$, describe the transformation that produces $g(x)=-2(x+3)^2+5$, and find the new vertex.

Step 1 — Identify the base function$f(x)=x^2$, the standard upward parabola with vertex at (0,0).
Step 2 — Read the horizontal shiftThe $(x+3)$ inside the square means a shift LEFT by 3 units (recall: $f(x+k)$ shifts left).
Step 3 — Read the vertical stretch and reflectionThe coefficient $-2$ in front does two things: the "2" stretches the graph vertically (makes it narrower), and the negative sign reflects it over the x-axis (flips it upside-down, opening downward).
Step 4 — Read the vertical shiftThe "+5" at the end shifts the whole graph UP by 5 units.
Step 5 — Combine to find the new vertexStarting vertex (0,0): shift left 3 → (−3, 0); shift up 5 → (−3, 5). The reflection and stretch don't move the vertex itself, only reshape the curve around it.
Step 6 — State the final answer$g(x)$ is $f(x)=x^2$ shifted left 3, reflected over the x-axis, vertically stretched by factor 2, and shifted up 5. New vertex: $(-3,5)$, opening downward.

The Derivative as a Limit

This is where calculus actually begins. Every technique in Chapter 3 is really just a shortcut for the single idea built here: the derivative as a limit of a difference quotient.

Chapter Objectives

  • Evaluate limits as x → ∞ for rational functions.
  • Determine whether a function is continuous at a point using the three-part definition.
  • State and apply the formal (limit) definition of the derivative.

2.1 Limits

The limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$, written $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$, is the value that $f(x)$ gets closer and closer to as $x$ gets closer and closer to $a$ — regardless of whether $f$ is actually defined at $a$ itself. This distinction (what happens NEAR a point, versus what happens AT the point) is the entire reason limits are useful.

Three Standard Techniques (1) Direct substitution — try plugging in x=a first; if it gives a real number, you're done. (2) Factor and cancel — if substitution gives 0/0, factor numerator and denominator and cancel the common factor. (3) Rationalize — if a square root is causing the 0/0 form, multiply by the conjugate.

2.2 Continuity

A function $f$ is continuous at $x=a$ if all three of the following hold:

  1. $f(a)$ is defined (the function actually has a value at a).
  2. $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ exists (the function approaches a single value from both sides).
  3. $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)$ (the limit matches the actual function value).

If any one of these three fails, the function is discontinuous at that point — common types are removable (a hole), jump (piecewise mismatch), or infinite (vertical asymptote) discontinuities.

2.3 Definition of the Derivative

The derivative of $f$ at $x$, written $f'(x)$, is defined as the limit of the difference quotient as the interval shrinks to zero:

Formal Definition
$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$the derivative "from first principles"

2.4 The Difference Quotient

Before taking the limit, $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ is called the difference quotient — it computes the average rate of change (slope of a secant line) between two points on the curve, $x$ and $x+h$. As $h\to0$, those two points merge into one, and the secant line's slope becomes the tangent line's slope: the derivative.

2.5 Rate of Change

The derivative $f'(x)$ measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ at the point $x$ — how fast the output is changing per unit change in input, at that exact instant. This is the single most important interpretation of the derivative and the one that makes it useful across every branch of science: it can mean speed, growth rate, marginal cost, or infection rate, depending on what $f$ represents.

2.6 Tangent Line

Once you know $f'(a)$, the slope of the tangent line to $y=f(x)$ at the point $(a,f(a))$, you can write the tangent line's equation using point-slope form:

Tangent Line Equation
$y-f(a)=f'(a)(x-a)$point-slope form, using the derivative as the slope

2.7 Velocity and Acceleration

If $s(t)$ describes an object's position at time $t$, then its velocity is the derivative of position, and its acceleration is the derivative of velocity (equivalently, the second derivative of position):

Motion
$v(t)=s'(t)$velocity = rate of change of position
$a(t)=v'(t)=s''(t)$acceleration = rate of change of velocity

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Worked Example 1 — Direct Substitution

Q: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$.

Step 1 — Try direct substitution firstPlug $x=2$ directly into the expression: $\dfrac{2^2-4}{2-2}=\dfrac{0}{0}$. This is an "indeterminate form" — it doesn't tell us the answer, it tells us we need another method.
Step 2 — Factor the numerator$x^2-4$ is a difference of squares: $x^2-4=(x-2)(x+2)$.
Step 3 — Rewrite the limit with the factored form$\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}$.
Step 4 — Cancel the common factor $(x-2)$Since $x\to2$ means $x$ gets close to but never equals 2, $x-2\ne0$, so cancelling is valid: $\lim_{x\to2}(x+2)$.
Step 5 — Now substitute directly$2+2=4$. So the limit is $4$.
Worked Example 2 — Limit at Infinity

Q: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{5x^2+3x}{x}$.

Step 1 — Simplify the expression algebraically firstSplit the fraction term by term: $\dfrac{5x^2+3x}{x}=\dfrac{5x^2}{x}+\dfrac{3x}{x}=5x+3$.
Step 2 — Take the limit of the simplified expression$\lim_{x\to\infty}(5x+3)$.
Step 3 — Reason about the behavior as x grows without boundAs $x\to\infty$, the term $5x$ grows without bound (gets arbitrarily large), and adding 3 doesn't change that behavior.
Step 4 — State the conclusionThe limit does not approach a finite number — it diverges to $+\infty$. We write $\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{5x^2+3x}{x}=\infty$ (the limit does not exist as a finite value).
Worked Example 3 — Direct Substitution (Polynomial)

Q: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to3}(x^2-2x-3)$.

Step 1 — Recognize this is a polynomialPolynomials are continuous everywhere, so the limit as $x\to a$ always equals direct substitution — no special technique is needed.
Step 2 — Substitute x = 3$(3)^2-2(3)-3$.
Step 3 — Evaluate the exponent$3^2=9$, giving $9-2(3)-3$.
Step 4 — Carry out the multiplication$2(3)=6$, giving $9-6-3$.
Step 5 — Combine the numbers$9-6-3=0$. So the limit is $0$.
Worked Example 4 — Factoring a Quadratic Denominator

Q: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}$.

Step 1 — Try direct substitution$\dfrac{1^2-1}{1-1}=\dfrac{0}{0}$, indeterminate — need another technique.
Step 2 — Factor the numerator as a difference of squares$x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$.
Step 3 — Rewrite the limit$\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1}$.
Step 4 — Cancel the common factor$\lim_{x\to1}(x+1)$.
Step 5 — Substitute x = 1$1+1=2$. So the limit is $2$.
NoteThis is a good comparison to a similar-looking expression, $\dfrac{x^2+1}{x-1}$, which does NOT simplify this way — its numerator never factors to cancel with $(x-1)$, so that limit is genuinely undefined (the function blows up near x=1). Always check whether the numerator actually factors before assuming a hole exists.
Worked Example 5 — Testing Continuity

Q: Is $f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x\ne2\\5&x=2\end{cases}$ continuous at $x=2$?

Step 1 — Check condition 1: is f(2) defined?Yes, by the piecewise rule, $f(2)=5$.
Step 2 — Check condition 2: does the limit exist as x→2?For $x\ne2$, $f(x)=x^2$, so $\lim_{x\to2}f(x)=\lim_{x\to2}x^2=4$. The limit exists and equals 4 (note: the limit only cares about values NEAR 2, so the special value at exactly $x=2$ doesn't affect this calculation).
Step 3 — Check condition 3: does the limit equal f(2)?The limit is 4, but $f(2)=5$. These do NOT match.
Step 4 — ConcludeSince condition 3 fails, $f$ is discontinuous at $x=2$ — specifically, this is a "removable discontinuity" (a hole in the graph at x=2, with a single misplaced point at (2,5)).
Worked Example 6 — Derivative from First Principles

Q: Use the limit definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^2+3x$.

Step 1 — Write down the definition$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$.
Step 2 — Compute f(x+h)Replace every x in $f(x)=x^2+3x$ with $(x+h)$: $f(x+h)=(x+h)^2+3(x+h)$.
Step 3 — Expand $(x+h)^2+3(x+h)$$(x+h)^2=x^2+2xh+h^2$, and $3(x+h)=3x+3h$. So $f(x+h)=x^2+2xh+h^2+3x+3h$.
Step 4 — Subtract f(x)$f(x+h)-f(x)=(x^2+2xh+h^2+3x+3h)-(x^2+3x)$. The $x^2$ terms cancel and the $3x$ terms cancel, leaving $2xh+h^2+3h$.
Step 5 — Divide by h$\dfrac{2xh+h^2+3h}{h}$. Every term in the numerator has a factor of h, so divide each term by h: $2x+h+3$.
Step 6 — Take the limit as h → 0$\lim_{h\to0}(2x+h+3)=2x+0+3=2x+3$.
Step 7 — State the result$f'(x)=2x+3$.
Worked Example 7 — Equation of a Tangent Line

Q: Find the equation of the tangent line to $f(x)=x^2+3x$ (from Example 6) at $x=1$.

Step 1 — Find the point on the curve$f(1)=1^2+3(1)=1+3=4$. The point is $(1,4)$.
Step 2 — Find the slope using the derivative found in Example 6$f'(x)=2x+3$, so $f'(1)=2(1)+3=5$. The slope of the tangent line is 5.
Step 3 — Apply point-slope form$y-f(a)=f'(a)(x-a)$ becomes $y-4=5(x-1)$.
Step 4 — Distribute the right-hand side$y-4=5x-5$.
Step 5 — Solve for yAdd 4 to both sides: $y=5x-5+4=5x-1$.
Step 6 — State the final equationThe tangent line is $y=5x-1$.
Worked Example 8 — Velocity and Acceleration

Q: A particle's position is $s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t$ (metres, t in seconds). Find its velocity and acceleration at $t=2$, and state whether it's speeding up or slowing down.

Step 1 — Find the velocity function$v(t)=s'(t)$. Differentiate term by term (using the power rule, formalized fully in Chapter 3): $s'(t)=3t^2-12t+9$.
Step 2 — Evaluate velocity at t = 2$v(2)=3(2)^2-12(2)+9=3(4)-24+9=12-24+9=-3\ \text{m/s}$.
Step 3 — Find the acceleration function$a(t)=v'(t)$: differentiate $3t^2-12t+9$ term by term: $a(t)=6t-12$.
Step 4 — Evaluate acceleration at t = 2$a(2)=6(2)-12=12-12=0\ \text{m/s}^2$.
Step 5 — Interpret the resultAt $t=2$, velocity is $-3$ m/s (particle moving in the negative direction) and acceleration is exactly $0$ m/s² — at this precise instant the particle's velocity is momentarily not changing (it's at a local extreme of velocity, not of position).

Chapter 03

Techniques of Differentiation

Nobody uses first-principles limits every time they need a derivative — that would be painfully slow. This chapter gives you the shortcut rules, derived once and reused forever.

Constant rulePower ruleProduct ruleQuotient rule Chain ruleImplicit differentiationLogarithmic differentiationHigher-order derivatives
Chapter Objectives
  • Apply the constant and power rules to differentiate polynomial terms instantly.
  • Apply the product rule to differentiate a product of two functions.
  • Apply the quotient rule to differentiate a ratio of two functions.
  • Apply the chain rule to differentiate composite functions.
  • Use implicit differentiation to find dy/dx when y is not isolated.
  • Use logarithmic differentiation for functions with variable exponents or complicated products/quotients.
  • Compute second and higher-order derivatives.

3.1 Constant Rule

Constant Rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[c]=0$the derivative of any constant is zero — a constant never changes, so its rate of change is 0

3.2 Power Rule

Power Rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}$bring the exponent down as a coefficient, then reduce the exponent by 1

Combined with the constant multiple rule ($\frac{d}{dx}[c\cdot f(x)]=c\cdot f'(x)$) and the sum rule ($\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)\pm g(x)]=f'(x)\pm g'(x)$), the power rule lets you differentiate any polynomial term by term instantly.

3.3 Product Rule

Product Rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[u\cdot v]=u'v+uv'$"first times derivative of second, PLUS second times derivative of first"
Common MistakeIt's tempting to just multiply the two derivatives, $u'v'$ — this is wrong. The product rule always has TWO terms added together.

3.4 Quotient Rule

Quotient Rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}\left[\dfrac{u}{v}\right]=\dfrac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$"low d-high minus high d-low, over low squared" — a classic memory chant

Order matters here — unlike the product rule, swapping $u'v$ and $uv'$ gives the wrong (negative) answer, so the subtraction order must be exact.

3.5 Chain Rule

Chain Rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))]=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$"derivative of the outside (leaving inside alone) times derivative of the inside"

The chain rule is needed any time you're differentiating a function-inside-a-function — for example $(3x+1)^5$, $\sin(x^2)$, or $\sqrt{2x-3}$.

3.6 Implicit Differentiation

Some equations, like $x^2+y^2=25$, define $y$ as a function of $x$ without solving for $y$ explicitly. To differentiate, treat $y$ as an unknown function of $x$: differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, applying the chain rule to every $y$-term (since $y$ is really $y(x)$, each $y$ term picks up a $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ factor), then algebraically solve for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

3.7 Logarithmic Differentiation

For functions with a variable in the exponent (like $x^x$), or very complicated products/quotients, taking the natural log of both sides first turns multiplication into addition and exponents into multipliers — often making the derivative dramatically easier to find. Steps: take $\ln$ of both sides, differentiate implicitly, then solve for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (multiplying back through by the original $y$).

3.8 Higher-Order Derivatives

Differentiating $f'(x)$ again gives the second derivative $f''(x)$; differentiating again gives the third, and so on. Notation: $f'(x), f''(x), f'''(x), f^{(4)}(x), \dots$ or equivalently $\dfrac{dy}{dx}, \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}, \dfrac{d^3y}{dx^3},\dots$. As seen in Chapter 2, the second derivative of position is acceleration — higher-order derivatives generally describe how the rate of change is itself changing.

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Worked Example 1 — Power Rule on a Polynomial

Q: Differentiate $f(x)=4x^5-3x^3+7x-9$.

Step 1 — Differentiate each term separately (sum rule)We handle $4x^5$, $-3x^3$, $7x$, and $-9$ one at a time.
Step 2 — Apply the power rule to $4x^5$Bring down the 5, reduce the exponent by 1: $5\times4x^{5-1}=20x^4$.
Step 3 — Apply the power rule to $-3x^3$$3\times(-3)x^{3-1}=-9x^2$.
Step 4 — Apply the power rule to $7x$ (which is $7x^1$)$1\times7x^{1-1}=7x^0=7$.
Step 5 — Apply the constant rule to $-9$The derivative of any constant is 0.
Step 6 — Combine all the pieces$f'(x)=20x^4-9x^2+7+0=20x^4-9x^2+7$.
Worked Example 2 — Product Rule

Q: Differentiate $f(x)=(3x^2+1)(2x-5)$.

Step 1 — Identify u and vLet $u=3x^2+1$ and $v=2x-5$.
Step 2 — Find u′$u'=\dfrac{d}{dx}[3x^2+1]=6x$.
Step 3 — Find v′$v'=\dfrac{d}{dx}[2x-5]=2$.
Step 4 — Apply the product rule formula $u'v+uv'$$f'(x)=(6x)(2x-5)+(3x^2+1)(2)$.
Step 5 — Expand the first term$(6x)(2x-5)=12x^2-30x$.
Step 6 — Expand the second term$(3x^2+1)(2)=6x^2+2$.
Step 7 — Add the two expanded terms together$f'(x)=12x^2-30x+6x^2+2$.
Step 8 — Combine like terms$12x^2+6x^2=18x^2$, so $f'(x)=18x^2-30x+2$.
Worked Example 3 — Quotient Rule

Q: Differentiate $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+1}{3x-2}$.

Step 1 — Identify u and v$u=x^2+1$ (the "high" / numerator), $v=3x-2$ (the "low" / denominator).
Step 2 — Find u′$u'=2x$.
Step 3 — Find v′$v'=3$.
Step 4 — Apply the quotient rule formula $\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$$f'(x)=\dfrac{(2x)(3x-2)-(x^2+1)(3)}{(3x-2)^2}$.
Step 5 — Expand the first product in the numerator$(2x)(3x-2)=6x^2-4x$.
Step 6 — Expand the second product in the numerator$(x^2+1)(3)=3x^2+3$.
Step 7 — Subtract the second expansion from the first$6x^2-4x-(3x^2+3)=6x^2-4x-3x^2-3=3x^2-4x-3$.
Step 8 — Write the final derivative$f'(x)=\dfrac{3x^2-4x-3}{(3x-2)^2}$.
Worked Example 4 — Chain Rule

Q: Differentiate $f(x)=(4x^3-5x)^6$.

Step 1 — Identify the outer and inner functionsOuter function: "raise something to the 6th power." Inner function: $g(x)=4x^3-5x$.
Step 2 — Differentiate the outer function first, leaving the inside untouchedUsing the power rule on the outer layer: $6(4x^3-5x)^5$.
Step 3 — Differentiate the inner function separately$g'(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}[4x^3-5x]=12x^2-5$.
Step 4 — Multiply the outer derivative by the inner derivative$f'(x)=6(4x^3-5x)^5\cdot(12x^2-5)$.
Step 5 — Write the final, simplified form$f'(x)=6(12x^2-5)(4x^3-5x)^5$. (Left in factored form — this is standard practice, since expanding would be extremely messy and gains nothing.)
Worked Example 5 — Implicit Differentiation

Q: Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ given $x^2+y^2=25$.

Step 1 — Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to x$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2]+\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2]=\dfrac{d}{dx}[25]$.
Step 2 — Differentiate $x^2$ using the power rule$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2]=2x$.
Step 3 — Differentiate $y^2$ using the chain rule (since y is a function of x)Treat y as $y(x)$: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2]=2y\cdot\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (power rule on the outside, times the derivative of the inside, which is $\frac{dy}{dx}$).
Step 4 — Differentiate the constant on the right$\dfrac{d}{dx}[25]=0$.
Step 5 — Assemble the differentiated equation$2x+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0$.
Step 6 — Isolate the term containing dy/dxSubtract $2x$ from both sides: $2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-2x$.
Step 7 — Solve for dy/dxDivide both sides by $2y$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{-2x}{2y}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$.
Worked Example 6 — Logarithmic Differentiation

Q: Differentiate $y=x^x$ (for $x>0$).

Step 1 — Recognize why standard rules don't directly applyThe power rule needs a constant exponent, and the exponential rule (Chapter 6 onward) needs a constant base — here BOTH the base and exponent are the variable x, so neither rule alone works.
Step 2 — Take the natural log of both sides$\ln y=\ln(x^x)$.
Step 3 — Use the log power rule to bring the exponent down$\ln(x^x)=x\ln x$, so now: $\ln y=x\ln x$.
Step 4 — Differentiate both sides with respect to xLeft side (chain rule, since y depends on x): $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln y]=\dfrac1y\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. Right side (product rule, since it's $x$ times $\ln x$): $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x\ln x]=(1)\ln x+x\cdot\dfrac1x=\ln x+1$.
Step 5 — Write the differentiated equation$\dfrac1y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\ln x+1$.
Step 6 — Solve for dy/dx by multiplying both sides by y$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y(\ln x+1)$.
Step 7 — Substitute back the original expression for ySince $y=x^x$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x^x(\ln x+1)$.
Worked Example 7 — Combining Product Rule and Chain Rule

Q: Differentiate $f(x)=x^2(3x-1)^4$.

Step 1 — Identify this as a product of two functions$u=x^2$ and $v=(3x-1)^4$ — the product rule is the outer structure here.
Step 2 — Find u′ using the power rule$u'=2x$.
Step 3 — Find v′ using the chain rule (since v is a composite function)Outer derivative: $4(3x-1)^3$. Inner derivative: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[3x-1]=3$. Multiply: $v'=4(3x-1)^3\cdot3=12(3x-1)^3$.
Step 4 — Apply the product rule formula $u'v+uv'$$f'(x)=(2x)(3x-1)^4+(x^2)\cdot12(3x-1)^3$.
Step 5 — Factor out the common factor $(3x-1)^3$Both terms share $(3x-1)^3$ and both share a factor of x: $f'(x)=(3x-1)^3\left[2x(3x-1)+12x^2\right]$.
Step 6 — Simplify inside the brackets$2x(3x-1)=6x^2-2x$, so the bracket becomes $6x^2-2x+12x^2=18x^2-2x$.
Step 7 — Write the final factored derivative$f'(x)=(3x-1)^3(18x^2-2x)$, which can also be written as $2x(3x-1)^3(9x-1)$ after factoring out $2x$ from the bracket.
Worked Example 8 — Higher-Order Derivatives

Q: Find $f''(x)$ for $f(x)=2x^4-9x^3+6x-1$.

Step 1 — Find the first derivative f′(x) term by term$\dfrac{d}{dx}[2x^4]=8x^3$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[-9x^3]=-27x^2$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[6x]=6$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[-1]=0$.

Step 3 — Differentiate f′(x) again to find f″(x), term by term$\dfrac{d}{dx}[8x^3]=24x^2$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[-27x^2]=-54x$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[6]=0$ (constant).
Step 4 — Combine to write the final second derivative$f''(x)=24x^2-54x$.

4.1 Critical Points

A critical point of $f$ is any x-value in the domain where $f'(x)=0$ or $f'(x)$ is undefined. These are the only candidates for local maxima or minima — a smooth function cannot turn from increasing to decreasing (or vice versa) anywhere else.

4.2 Increasing and Decreasing Functions

Sign of f′(x)
$f'(x)>0$ on an intervalf is increasing there
$f'(x)<0$ on an intervalf is decreasing there

To find these intervals: locate all critical points, use them to split the number line into pieces, then test a sample point from each piece in $f'(x)$ to see whether it's positive or negative there.

4.3 First Derivative Test

At a critical point $x=c$: if $f'(x)$ changes from positive to negative there, $f$ has a local maximum at $c$. If $f'(x)$ changes from negative to positive, $f$ has a local minimum. If $f'(x)$ doesn't change sign, $c$ is neither (often a saddle-type flat point).

4.4 Concavity and Inflection Points

Sign of f″(x)
$f''(x)>0$ on an intervalconcave UP (shaped like a cup, ∪)
$f''(x)<0$ on an intervalconcave DOWN (shaped like a cap, ∩)

An inflection point is a point where concavity actually switches (from up to down, or down to up) — found where $f''(x)=0$ or is undefined, AND the sign of $f''(x)$ genuinely changes there (just like critical points, not every candidate turns out to be a true inflection point).

4.5 Second Derivative Test

An alternative to the first derivative test, often faster: at a critical point $x=c$ where $f'(c)=0$, if $f''(c)>0$, $f$ has a local minimum at $c$ (concave up, cup-shaped, bottom of a valley). If $f''(c)<0$, $f$ has a local maximum (concave down, top of a hill). If $f''(c)=0$, the test is inconclusive — fall back to the first derivative test.

4.6 Asymptotes

TypeHow to find it
Vertical asymptotevalues of x where the denominator = 0 (and the numerator doesn't also vanish there)
Horizontal asymptoteevaluate $\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}f(x)$; compare degrees of numerator/denominator for rational functions

4.7 Complete Curve Sketching — the Checklist

  1. Find the domain (and any asymptotes from Section 4.6).
  2. Find intercepts (set $x=0$ for y-intercept; set $f(x)=0$ for x-intercepts).
  3. Find $f'(x)$, locate critical points, and determine increasing/decreasing intervals.
  4. Classify each critical point using the first or second derivative test.
  5. Find $f''(x)$, determine concavity intervals, and locate inflection points.
  6. Plot the key points and sketch the curve using all the collected information.

4.8 Optimization Problems

Real-world optimization problems ask you to maximize or minimize some quantity subject to a constraint. Standard approach: (1) write a formula for the quantity to be optimized in terms of one variable, using the constraint to eliminate any extra variables; (2) differentiate and find critical points; (3) use the first or second derivative test to confirm a max or min; (4) check the answer makes sense in context (e.g., a length can't be negative).

Worked Example 1 — Finding Critical Points

Q: Find the critical points of $f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5$.

Step 1 — Find f′(x) using the power rule$f'(x)=3x^2-6x-9$.
Step 2 — Set f′(x) equal to zero$3x^2-6x-9=0$.
Step 3 — Factor out the common factor of 3$3(x^2-2x-3)=0$, so $x^2-2x-3=0$.
Step 4 — Factor the quadraticFind two numbers multiplying to $-3$ and adding to $-2$: those are $-3$ and $1$. So $(x-3)(x+1)=0$.
Step 5 — Solve for x$x=3$ or $x=-1$.
Step 6 — State the critical pointsThe critical points are at $x=-1$ and $x=3$ (f'(x) is a polynomial, so it's defined everywhere — no additional undefined-derivative points to check).
Worked Example 2 — Increasing/Decreasing Intervals & First Derivative Test

Q: Using $f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5$ from Example 1, find the intervals of increase/decrease, and classify each critical point.

Step 1 — Recall the critical points and the derivativeFrom Example 1: $f'(x)=3(x-3)(x+1)$, critical points at $x=-1,3$.
Step 2 — Use the critical points to divide the number line into three intervals$(-\infty,-1)$, $(-1,3)$, and $(3,\infty)$.
Step 3 — Test a sample point in $(-\infty,-1)$, say x=-2$f'(-2)=3(-2-3)(-2+1)=3(-5)(-1)=15>0$. So f is increasing on $(-\infty,-1)$.
Step 4 — Test a sample point in $(-1,3)$, say x=0$f'(0)=3(0-3)(0+1)=3(-3)(1)=-9<0$. So f is decreasing on $(-1,3)$.
Step 5 — Test a sample point in $(3,\infty)$, say x=4$f'(4)=3(4-3)(4+1)=3(1)(5)=15>0$. So f is increasing on $(3,\infty)$.
Step 6 — Apply the first derivative test at each critical pointAt $x=-1$: f' changes from + to − (increasing → decreasing) → local MAXIMUM. At $x=3$: f' changes from − to + (decreasing → increasing) → local MINIMUM.
Step 7 — Find the actual y-values of these points$f(-1)=(-1)^3-3(-1)^2-9(-1)+5=-1-3+9+5=10$. $f(3)=27-27-27+5=-22$. So local max at $(-1,10)$, local min at $(3,-22)$.
Worked Example 3 — Concavity and Inflection Points

Q: Find the concavity intervals and inflection point(s) of $f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5$.

Step 1 — Find f″(x) by differentiating f′(x)=3x²-6x-9$f''(x)=6x-6$.

Step 4 — Test a point to the right of x=1, say x=2$f''(2)=6(2)-6=6>0$, so f is concave UP on $(1,\infty)$.
Step 5 — Confirm the sign genuinely changes at x=1Concavity switches from down to up exactly at $x=1$, confirming this is a true inflection point (not just a candidate).
Step 6 — Find the y-coordinate of the inflection point$f(1)=1-3-9+5=-6$. Inflection point: $(1,-6)$.
Worked Example 4 — Second Derivative Test

Q: Use the second derivative test to classify the critical points of $f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5$ (cross-check against Example 2).

Step 1 — Recall f″(x) and the critical points$f''(x)=6x-6$; critical points at $x=-1$ and $x=3$.

Q: Find all vertical and horizontal asymptotes of $f(x)=\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2-4}$.

Step 3 — For the horizontal asymptote, compare the degree of numerator and denominatorBoth numerator ($2x^2$) and denominator ($x^2-4$) have degree 2 — when degrees are equal, the horizontal asymptote is the ratio of the leading coefficients.
Step 4 — Compute the ratio of leading coefficientsLeading coefficient of numerator = 2; leading coefficient of denominator = 1. Ratio = $2/1=2$.
Step 5 — State the final asymptotesVertical asymptotes: $x=2$ and $x=-2$. Horizontal asymptote: $y=2$.

Worked Example 7 — Optimization: Maximum Area

Q: A farmer has 200 m of fencing to enclose a rectangular field against an existing straight wall (so only 3 sides need fencing). Find the dimensions that maximize the enclosed area.

Step 2 — Write the quantity to be maximizedArea $A=xy$.
Step 3 — Use the constraint to eliminate yFrom $2x+y=200$: $y=200-2x$.

Worked Example 8 — Optimization: Minimum Cost (Box)

Q: An open-top box is to be made from a square sheet of cardboard by cutting equal squares of side $x$ from each corner and folding up the sides. If the sheet is 12 cm on each side, find the value of $x$ that maximizes the box's volume.

Step 2 — Write the volume as a function of x$V(x)=x(12-2x)^2$.

We spent four chapters learning to go from a function to its derivative. Now we reverse direction: given a derivative, find the function it came from.

5.1 Antiderivatives

An antiderivative of $f(x)$ is any function $F(x)$ such that $F'(x)=f(x)$. Integration is the process of finding an antiderivative — it's the reverse operation of differentiation, in exactly the way that squaring and square-rooting are reverses of each other.

5.2 Indefinite Integrals

The notation $\int f(x)\,dx$ (the "indefinite integral") represents the entire family of antiderivatives of $f(x)$. The $dx$ signals which variable we're integrating with respect to; the $\int$ symbol is a stretched "S," historically standing for "sum" (a connection made precise in Chapter 7).

5.3 Basic Integration Rules

Basic Rules
$\int x^n\,dx=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C\ (n\ne-1)$power rule for integration — reverse of the differentiation power rule
$\int c\,dx = cx+C$integral of a constant
$\int c\cdot f(x)\,dx = c\int f(x)\,dx$constant multiple rule
$\int[f(x)\pm g(x)]\,dx=\int f(x)\,dx\pm\int g(x)\,dx$sum/difference rule

5.4 The Constant of Integration

Because the derivative of any constant is zero (Chapter 3), $F(x)$ and $F(x)+5$ have exactly the same derivative — so if $F(x)$ is one antiderivative of $f(x)$, so is $F(x)+C$ for literally any constant C. This is why every indefinite integral must end with "+C": without it, you're only reporting ONE member of an infinite family of correct answers, not the complete general answer.

5.5 Checking Answers by Differentiation

Because integration and differentiation are inverse operations, you can always verify an integration result: differentiate your answer, and if you get back the original integrand, your integration was correct. This is the single most reliable way to catch integration mistakes and should become a habit.

Worked Example 1 — Basic Power Rule Integration

Step 3 — Divide by the new exponent$\dfrac{x^5}{5}$.

Worked Example 2 — Polynomial with Multiple Terms

Worked Example 3 — Integrating Negative and Fractional Powers

Worked Example 4 — Expanding Before Integrating

Q: Find $\int(2x-3)(x+4)\,dx$.

Step 2 — Expand $(2x-3)(x+4)$$2x\cdot x+2x\cdot4-3\cdot x-3\cdot4=2x^2+8x-3x-12$.
Step 3 — Combine like terms$2x^2+5x-12$.
Step 4 — Integrate the resulting polynomial term by term$\int(2x^2+5x-12)\,dx=2\cdot\dfrac{x^3}{3}+5\cdot\dfrac{x^2}{2}-12x$.

6.1 Substitution (u-Substitution)

Substitution is the integration counterpart of the chain rule. When the integrand contains a function and (up to a constant) its derivative, replace the inner function with a new variable $u$ to simplify the integral.

Substitution Method
1. Choose $u=g(x)$ (usually the "inside" of a composite function)pick the piece whose derivative also appears
2. Find $du=g'(x)\,dx$differentiate u
3. Rewrite the ENTIRE integral in terms of u onlyno x should remain
4. Integrate with respect to u, then substitute back $u=g(x)$return to the original variable

6.2 Integration by Parts

Integration by parts is the integration counterpart of the product rule, used when the integrand is a product of two different types of functions:

Integration by Parts
$\int u\,dv = uv-\int v\,du$choose u and dv from the integrand
LIATE Guideline Choose u to be whichever type appears earliest in: Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential. Whatever's left becomes dv.

6.3 Partial Fractions

When integrating a rational function whose denominator factors into simpler pieces, partial fraction decomposition splits the single complicated fraction into a sum of simpler fractions, each easy to integrate on its own.

Partial Fractions — Linear Factors
$\dfrac{p(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\dfrac{A}{x-a}+\dfrac{B}{x-b}$solve for A, B by clearing denominators and matching coefficients (or substituting convenient x-values)

6.4 Trigonometric Integrals

Standard Trig Integrals
$\int\sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C$
$\int\cos x\,dx=\sin x+C$
$\int\sec^2x\,dx=\tan x+C$
$\int\sin^2x\,dx=\dfrac x2-\dfrac{\sin2x}4+C$uses the identity $\sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos2x}2$

6.5 Trigonometric Substitution

When an integrand contains $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$, $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$, or $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$, substituting $x$ with an appropriate trig function turns the awkward square root into a clean trig expression via the Pythagorean identity:

Trig Substitution Patterns
$\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$let $x=a\sin\theta$
$\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$let $x=a\tan\theta$
$\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$let $x=a\sec\theta$

6.6 Reduction Formulas

A reduction formula expresses an integral involving $x^n$ (or $\sin^nx$, etc.) in terms of a similar integral with a lower power, letting you step down repeatedly until you reach a base case you already know how to integrate. Example: $\int x^ne^x\,dx=x^ne^x-n\int x^{n-1}e^x\,dx$, derived via repeated integration by parts.

7.1 Riemann Sums

To approximate the area under a curve $y=f(x)$ between $x=a$ and $x=b$, slice the region into $n$ thin vertical rectangles, each of width $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$, with height given by the function's value at some point in each strip. Adding up all the rectangle areas gives a Riemann sum — an approximation that gets more accurate as $n$ increases and the rectangles get thinner.

Riemann Sum
$\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(x_i)\,\Delta x$sum of rectangle areas, $\Delta x=(b-a)/n$
$\int_a^bf(x)\,dx=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(x_i)\Delta x$the definite integral IS the exact limit of this sum

7.2 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This is the single result that ties Chapters 2–6 (differentiation and antiderivatives) directly to area, and it's why integration and differentiation are studied together as "calculus" rather than as two separate subjects.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 2 — the computational version)
$\int_a^bf(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a)$where $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ (i.e. $F'=f$)

This is genuinely remarkable: instead of computing an infinite Riemann sum, you can now find area by simply finding one antiderivative and subtracting its values at the two endpoints.

7.3 Evaluating Definite Integrals

Standard notation: $\Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b$ means "evaluate $F(x)$ at $x=b$, then subtract its value at $x=a$." Practical procedure: find the antiderivative (ignore +C — it cancels out in the subtraction), then evaluate at the upper limit and subtract the value at the lower limit.

7.4 Properties of Definite Integrals

Key Properties
$\int_a^bf(x)\,dx=-\int_b^af(x)\,dx$reversing the limits flips the sign
$\int_a^af(x)\,dx=0$zero-width interval, zero area
$\int_a^bf(x)\,dx=\int_a^cf(x)\,dx+\int_c^bf(x)\,dx$additivity across an intermediate point c
$\int_a^bk\,f(x)\,dx=k\int_a^bf(x)\,dx$constant multiple

7.5 Average Value of a Function

Average Value
$f_{avg}=\dfrac{1}{b-a}\int_a^bf(x)\,dx$the "height" of a rectangle with the same base [a,b] and the same area as the region under f

7.6 Numerical Integration (Trapezoidal Rule)

Some functions have no elementary antiderivative at all (like $e^{-x^2}$), so the Fundamental Theorem can't be applied directly — instead, we approximate. The Trapezoidal Rule improves on plain rectangles by using trapezoids to hug the curve more closely:

Trapezoidal Rule
$\int_a^bf(x)\,dx\approx\dfrac{\Delta x}{2}\Big[f(x_0)+2f(x_1)+\dots+2f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\Big]$$\Delta x=(b-a)/n$; interior points doubled, endpoints single

8.1 Area Under a Curve

For $f(x)\ge0$ on $[a,b]$, the area between the curve and the x-axis is simply $\int_a^bf(x)\,dx$ (this is exactly the geometric meaning behind Chapter 7). If $f(x)$ dips below the x-axis anywhere in the interval, that portion contributes negatively to the integral — so for TRUE area (always positive), you must take $\int_a^b|f(x)|\,dx$, which in practice means splitting the interval at any x-intercepts and taking the absolute value of each negative piece.

8.2 Area Between Two Curves

Area Between Curves
$A=\int_a^b\big[f(x)-g(x)\big]\,dx$where $f(x)\ge g(x)$ on $[a,b]$ — always (top curve) minus (bottom curve)

Finding the limits $a$ and $b$ usually means first solving $f(x)=g(x)$ to locate the curves' intersection points.

8.3–8.5 Volumes of Solids of Revolution

Rotating a 2D region around an axis sweeps out a 3D solid. Three related techniques, chosen based on the shape of the cross-sections perpendicular to the axis:

Disk Method
$V=\pi\int_a^b[f(x)]^2\,dx$region bounded by f(x) and the axis of rotation, no gap — solid disk cross-sections
Washer Method
$V=\pi\int_a^b\Big([f(x)]^2-[g(x)]^2\Big)\,dx$region between two curves rotated — a disk with a hole (washer) cross-section; f = outer radius, g = inner radius
Shell Method
$V=2\pi\int_a^bx\cdot f(x)\,dx$rotating about a vertical axis when slicing vertically is easier than horizontally — cylindrical shell cross-sections
Choosing a Method Disk/washer: slice PERPENDICULAR to the axis of rotation. Shell: slice PARALLEL to the axis of rotation. When solving for the other variable would be messy or impossible, shells usually save you the trouble.

Worked Examples — Step by Step

Worked Example 1 — Area Under a Curve

Q: Find the area under $f(x)=4-x^2$ between $x=-2$ and $x=2$.

Step 1 — Confirm f(x) ≥ 0 on this interval (so a plain integral gives true area)$f(-2)=0$, $f(0)=4$, $f(2)=0$ — the curve stays at or above the x-axis throughout $[-2,2]$, touching zero only at the endpoints.
Step 2 — Set up the definite integral$A=\int_{-2}^2(4-x^2)\,dx$.
Step 3 — Find the antiderivative$4x-\dfrac{x^3}3$.
Step 4 — Evaluate at the upper limit x=2$4(2)-\dfrac{2^3}3=8-\dfrac83=\dfrac{24}3-\dfrac83=\dfrac{16}3$.
Step 5 — Evaluate at the lower limit x=-2$4(-2)-\dfrac{(-2)^3}3=-8-\dfrac{-8}3=-8+\dfrac83=\dfrac{-24}3+\dfrac83=\dfrac{-16}3$.
Step 6 — Subtract (upper) − (lower)$\dfrac{16}3-\left(\dfrac{-16}3\right)=\dfrac{32}3$. So the area is $\dfrac{32}3$ square units.
Worked Example 2 — Area Between Two Curves

Q: Find the area between $f(x)=x+2$ and $g(x)=x^2$.

Step 1 — Find the intersection points by setting f(x)=g(x)$x+2=x^2\Rightarrow x^2-x-2=0$.
Step 2 — Factor and solve$(x-2)(x+1)=0\Rightarrow x=2$ or $x=-1$. These become the limits of integration.
Step 3 — Determine which curve is on top between the intersections (test x=0)$f(0)=0+2=2$; $g(0)=0^2=0$. Since $f(0)>g(0)$, $f(x)=x+2$ is the TOP curve on this interval.
Step 4 — Set up the area integral, top minus bottom$A=\int_{-1}^2\big[(x+2)-x^2\big]\,dx$.
Step 5 — Find the antiderivative$\dfrac{x^2}2+2x-\dfrac{x^3}3$.
Step 6 — Evaluate at the upper limit x=2$\dfrac42+4-\dfrac83=2+4-\dfrac83=6-\dfrac83=\dfrac{18}3-\dfrac83=\dfrac{10}3$.
Step 7 — Evaluate at the lower limit x=-1$\dfrac12-2-\dfrac{-1}3=0.5-2+\dfrac13=-1.5+\dfrac13=-\dfrac32+\dfrac13=-\dfrac96+\dfrac26=-\dfrac76$.
Step 8 — Subtract (upper) − (lower)$\dfrac{10}3-\left(-\dfrac76\right)=\dfrac{20}6+\dfrac76=\dfrac{27}6=\dfrac92$. The area is $\dfrac92$ square units.
Worked Example 3 — Disk Method

Q: Find the volume of the solid formed by rotating $f(x)=\sqrt x$ about the x-axis, from $x=0$ to $x=4$.

Step 1 — Recognize this fits the disk method (rotating a single curve about the axis it touches)The region between $f(x)=\sqrt x$ and the x-axis, rotated about the x-axis, sweeps out solid disks.
Step 2 — Set up the disk method formula$V=\pi\int_0^4[f(x)]^2\,dx=\pi\int_0^4(\sqrt x)^2\,dx$.
Step 3 — Simplify the integrand$(\sqrt x)^2=x$, so $V=\pi\int_0^4x\,dx$.
Step 4 — Find the antiderivative$\dfrac{x^2}2$.
Step 5 — Evaluate between the limits$\pi\Big[\dfrac{x^2}2\Big]_0^4=\pi\left(\dfrac{16}2-0\right)=\pi(8)$.
Step 6 — State the final volume$V=8\pi$ cubic units.
Worked Example 4 — Washer Method

Q: Find the volume when the region between $f(x)=x+2$ (outer) and $g(x)=x^2$ (inner), on the interval found in Example 2 ($x=-1$ to $x=2$), is rotated about the x-axis.

Step 1 — Recognize the washer method applies (region between two curves, rotated)Rotating creates a hole down the middle — a washer, not a solid disk.
Step 2 — Set up the washer formula, outer radius squared minus inner radius squared$V=\pi\int_{-1}^2\Big[(x+2)^2-(x^2)^2\Big]\,dx$.
Step 3 — Expand $(x+2)^2$$(x+2)^2=x^2+4x+4$.
Step 4 — Expand $(x^2)^2$$(x^2)^2=x^4$.
Step 5 — Write the simplified integrand$V=\pi\int_{-1}^2(x^2+4x+4-x^4)\,dx$.
Step 6 — Find the antiderivative$\dfrac{x^3}3+2x^2+4x-\dfrac{x^5}5$.
Step 7 — Evaluate at the upper limit x=2$\dfrac83+2(4)+4(2)-\dfrac{32}5=\dfrac83+8+8-\dfrac{32}5=\dfrac83+16-\dfrac{32}5$. Common denominator 15: $\dfrac{40}{15}+\dfrac{240}{15}-\dfrac{96}{15}=\dfrac{184}{15}$.
Step 8 — Evaluate at the lower limit x=-1 and subtract, then multiply by πAt $x=-1$: $\dfrac{-1}3+2-4-\dfrac{-1}5=-\dfrac13+2-4+\dfrac15$. Common denominator 15: $-\dfrac5{15}+\dfrac{30}{15}-\dfrac{60}{15}+\dfrac3{15}=-\dfrac{32}{15}$. Subtracting: $\dfrac{184}{15}-\left(-\dfrac{32}{15}\right)=\dfrac{216}{15}$. So $V=\pi\cdot\dfrac{216}{15}=\dfrac{216\pi}{15}=\dfrac{72\pi}5$.
Worked Example 5 — Shell Method

Q: Find the volume when the region bounded by $f(x)=x^2$, the x-axis, and $x=2$ is rotated about the y-axis.

Step 1 — Explain why shells are preferred here over disks/washersRotating about the y-axis with a curve given as $y=x^2$ would require solving for x in terms of y ($x=\sqrt y$) to use disks — shells let us integrate directly in x instead, avoiding that extra algebra.
Step 2 — Set up the shell method formula$V=2\pi\int_a^bx\cdot f(x)\,dx$, with $a=0$, $b=2$, $f(x)=x^2$.
Step 3 — Substitute f(x)$V=2\pi\int_0^2x\cdot x^2\,dx=2\pi\int_0^2x^3\,dx$.
Step 4 — Find the antiderivative$\dfrac{x^4}4$.
Step 5 — Evaluate between the limits$2\pi\Big[\dfrac{x^4}4\Big]_0^2=2\pi\left(\dfrac{16}4-0\right)=2\pi(4)$.
Step 6 — State the final volume$V=8\pi$ cubic units.
Worked Example 6 — Work Done by a Variable Force

Q: A spring exerts a restoring force $F(x)=15x$ N when stretched $x$ metres (a form of Hooke's Law). Find the work done stretching it from $x=0$ to $x=0.5$ m.

Step 1 — Set up the work integral$W=\int_a^bF(x)\,dx=\int_0^{0.5}15x\,dx$.
Step 2 — Find the antiderivative$15\cdot\dfrac{x^2}2=\dfrac{15x^2}2$.
Step 3 — Evaluate at the upper limit x=0.5$\dfrac{15(0.5)^2}2=\dfrac{15(0.25)}2=\dfrac{3.75}2=1.875$.
Step 4 — Evaluate at the lower limit x=0$\dfrac{15(0)^2}2=0$.
Step 5 — Subtract and state the answer with units$W=1.875-0=1.875\ \text{J}$ (joules, since force is in newtons and distance in metres).
Worked Example 7 — Center of Mass of a Rod

Q: A thin rod lies along $[0,4]$ with linear density $\rho(x)=3x+1$ (kg/m). Find the total mass and the center of mass.

Step 1 — Set up the total mass integral$M=\int_0^4(3x+1)\,dx$.
Step 2 — Find the antiderivative$\dfrac{3x^2}2+x$.
Step 3 — Evaluate between the limits to find M$\Big[\dfrac{3x^2}2+x\Big]_0^4=\left(\dfrac{3(16)}2+4\right)-0=(24+4)=28$. So $M=28$ kg.
Step 4 — Set up the numerator integral for center of mass$\int_0^4x(3x+1)\,dx=\int_0^4(3x^2+x)\,dx$.
Step 5 — Find this antiderivative$x^3+\dfrac{x^2}2$.
Step 6 — Evaluate between the limits$\Big[x^3+\dfrac{x^2}2\Big]_0^4=\left(64+8\right)-0=72$.
Step 7 — Divide by the total mass M to find the center of mass$\bar x=\dfrac{72}{28}=\dfrac{18}7\approx2.57$ m.
Step 8 — Sanity-check the answerSince the rod gets denser toward $x=4$ (density increases with x), the balance point being past the geometric midpoint (x=2) makes physical sense.
Worked Example 8 — Arc Length

Q: Find the arc length of $f(x)=\dfrac23x^{3/2}$ from $x=0$ to $x=3$.

Step 1 — Find f′(x)$f'(x)=\dfrac23\cdot\dfrac32x^{1/2}=x^{1/2}=\sqrt x$.
Step 2 — Square f′(x)$[f'(x)]^2=x$.
Step 3 — Set up the arc length integral$L=\int_0^3\sqrt{1+x}\,dx$.
Step 4 — Use substitution: let u=1+x$du=dx$; when $x=0,u=1$; when $x=3,u=4$.
Step 5 — Rewrite and integrate in terms of u$\int_1^4\sqrt u\,du=\int_1^4u^{1/2}\,du=\Big[\dfrac23u^{3/2}\Big]_1^4$.
Step 6 — Evaluate at the limits$\dfrac23(4)^{3/2}-\dfrac23(1)^{3/2}=\dfrac23(8)-\dfrac23(1)=\dfrac{16}3-\dfrac23=\dfrac{14}3$.
Step 7 — State the final arc length$L=\dfrac{14}3$ units.
