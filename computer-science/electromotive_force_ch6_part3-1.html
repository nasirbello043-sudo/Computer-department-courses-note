6.20

Electromotive Force (EMF) and Electromagnetic Induction

How a changing magnetic field can create electricity out of nothing but motion.

Watch — Faraday's & Lenz's Law, Induced EMF, Transformers
Electromagnetic Induction Overview
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Electromotive force (EMF) is the energy supplied per unit charge by a source that drives current around a circuit, measured in volts. Electromagnetic induction is the process by which a changing magnetic flux through a circuit generates (induces) an EMF in that circuit.

Explanation. Everything in this chapter so far has treated magnetism and electricity as two separate stories that occasionally interact — a current creates a field (section 6.13), a field pushes a moving charge (section 6.14). Electromagnetic induction reveals that the connection runs even deeper: a magnetic field that is changing can create an electric current all by itself, with no battery required. Push a bar magnet into a coil of wire, and for that brief moment while the magnet is moving, a current flows through the coil — pull the magnet back out, and current flows again, this time in the opposite direction. Hold the magnet perfectly still inside the coil, and nothing happens at all, no matter how strong the magnet is. This tells you something crucial: it isn't the presence of a magnetic field that generates current, it's the rate of change of that field.

The quantity that captures "how much magnetic field is passing through a loop" is called magnetic flux, Φ = BA cosθ, essentially counting how many field lines pass through the loop's area. Electromagnetic induction says that whenever this flux changes — whether because the magnet moves, the coil moves, the coil rotates, or the field strength itself changes — an EMF appears in the coil, ready to drive a current if the circuit is closed. This single idea, first demonstrated by Michael Faraday, underlies every electric generator on the planet: spin a coil inside a magnetic field (or a magnet inside a coil), and the continuously changing flux generates a continuous alternating EMF, which is precisely how power stations turn mechanical motion into the electricity that reaches your home.

Magnetic Flux
Φ = B·A·cosθ
Φ = magnetic flux (Wb, weber)  |  B = magnetic field strength (T)  |  A = area of the loop (m²)  |  θ = angle between B and the normal to the loop
Applications
  • Electric generators in power stations convert mechanical rotation into electrical EMF.
  • Induction cooktops heat pans directly using induced currents.
  • Wireless charging pads induce current in a nearby coil without any physical contact.
  • Metal detectors sense induced currents in buried metal objects.

Worked Examples

Given

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula

cos(0°) = 1
Step 3 — Substitute the values

Step 4 — Multiply

Final AnswerΦ = 0.1 Wb
Ex 2Flux When the Field Is at an Angle
Given

B = 0.8 T

A = 0.05 m²

θ = 60°

Find: magnetic flux Φ

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula

Φ = 0.8 × 0.05 × 0.5
Step 4 — Multiply step by step

Step 2 — Assess whether the flux is changing
Since the magnet is stationary, the flux through the coil is constant.
Final AnswerNo current is induced, no matter how strong the magnet is, because the flux isn't changing.
6.21

Faraday's Law and Lenz's Law

How much EMF is induced, and which way the induced current actually flows.

Watch — Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
Faraday's Law Basic Introduction
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Faraday's Law states that the induced EMF in a coil equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through it, multiplied by the number of turns: EMF = −N(ΔΦ/Δt). Lenz's Law states that the induced current always flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux that created it.

Explanation. Faraday's Law answers "how much" EMF gets induced: the faster the flux changes, and the more turns of wire the coil has, the bigger the induced EMF. Push a magnet into a coil quickly, and you get a bigger momentary EMF than pushing it in slowly, even though the total change in flux is identical either way — because Faraday's Law depends on the rate of change, not just the total change. Wrapping the coil with more turns has a similar multiplying effect, since each turn experiences (and contributes) its own share of induced EMF, and they all add together in series.

The minus sign in Faraday's Law is where Lenz's Law comes in, and it's not just mathematical decoration — it reflects a deep principle of energy conservation. If you push a magnet's north pole into a coil, the induced current doesn't just flow in some arbitrary direction; it flows in exactly the direction needed to create its own magnetic field that opposes the incoming magnet, effectively creating a repelling north pole to resist the magnet's entry. This is why you always feel a slight resistance pushing a magnet into a coil connected to a circuit — you are doing work against that opposition, and that work is exactly what becomes the electrical energy delivered to the circuit. If the induced current instead helped the magnet's motion along, you could get energy from nothing, which would violate the conservation of energy — Lenz's Law is really just energy conservation applied to induction.

Faraday's Law
EMF = −N (ΔΦ / Δt)
EMF = induced electromotive force (V)  |  N = number of turns  |  ΔΦ = change in magnetic flux (Wb)  |  Δt = time interval (s)
The minus sign encodes Lenz's Law — the induced EMF opposes the change that produced it.
Applications
  • Electric generators exploit Faraday's Law by rapidly changing flux through rotating coils.
  • Regenerative braking in electric vehicles uses Lenz's Law to convert motion into stored electrical energy.
  • Induction cooktops and eddy-current brakes rely on Lenz's Law to produce opposing forces or heat.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Induced EMF from a Changing Flux
Given

N = 50 turns

Flux changes from 0.02 Wb to 0.08 Wb in 0.4 s

Find: induced EMF

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the change in flux
ΔΦ = 0.08 − 0.02 = 0.06 Wb
Step 2 — Write Faraday's Law
EMF = −N(ΔΦ/Δt)
Step 3 — Substitute the values (magnitude only)
EMF = 50 × (0.06 / 0.4)
Step 4 — Divide, then multiply
0.06 / 0.4 = 0.15; then 50 × 0.15 = 7.5
EMF = 7.5 V
Final AnswerEMF = 7.5 V (magnitude; direction opposes the increasing flux, by Lenz's Law)
Ex 2Effect of Doubling the Number of Turns
Given

Original EMF = 4 V at N turns; number of turns doubled, flux change and time unchanged

Find: new EMF

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall EMF ∝ N
Since EMF = N(ΔΦ/Δt), doubling N doubles the EMF directly.
Step 2 — Apply the scale factor
EMF_new = 4 V × 2 = 8 V
Final AnswerEMF_new = 8 V
Ex 3Direction of Induced Current (Lenz's Law)
Given

A magnet's south pole is pushed toward a coil, increasing the flux into the coil

Find: the direction of the induced current's own magnetic field

Full Step-by-Step Reasoning
Step 1 — Identify the change
Flux into the coil (from the approaching south pole) is increasing.
Step 2 — Apply Lenz's Law
The induced current must oppose this increase, so it creates its own field pointing out of the coil, toward the approaching magnet.
Step 3 — Identify the effective pole created
A field pointing out toward the approaching south pole means the coil's near face acts as a south pole too, so it repels the approaching magnet.
Final AnswerThe coil's near face becomes an effective south pole, repelling the incoming magnet and opposing its motion.
Ex 4Faster Motion, Bigger EMF
Given

Same flux change of 0.05 Wb occurs in 0.5 s in one trial, and in 0.1 s in a second trial (magnet pushed in faster), N = 100 turns

Find: the EMF in each trial

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Trial 1: apply Faraday's Law
EMF₁ = 100 × (0.05/0.5) = 100 × 0.1 = 10 V
Step 2 — Trial 2: apply Faraday's Law
EMF₂ = 100 × (0.05/0.1) = 100 × 0.5 = 50 V
Final AnswerEMF₁ = 10 V; EMF₂ = 50 V — the same total flux change induces a much larger EMF when it happens faster.
6.22

Self and Mutual Induction

How a coil can induce an EMF in itself — and in a neighbouring coil.

Watch — Self Inductance of Inductors & Coils
Self Inductance
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗

Types of Induction

Self Induction

A coil induces an EMF in itself when its own current changes, opposing that change.

Mutual Induction

A changing current in one coil induces an EMF in a separate, nearby coil.

Definition

Self-inductance (L) is a coil's property of inducing an EMF in itself whenever its own current changes: EMF = −L(ΔI/Δt). Mutual inductance (M) is the property of two coils where a changing current in one induces an EMF in the other: EMF₂ = −M(ΔI₁/Δt).

Explanation. You might expect a coil's own magnetic flux to only affect other objects nearby, but a coil's changing current also creates a changing flux through itself. By Faraday's Law, that self-generated changing flux induces its own EMF back in the very coil producing it — and by Lenz's Law, that self-induced EMF always opposes whatever change in current caused it. Switch on a circuit containing an inductor, and the coil briefly "resists" the current rising to its final value; switch it off, and the coil resists the current dropping to zero, sometimes producing a visible spark as it fights to keep the current flowing. This self-opposition is exactly what "inductance" measures — a property entirely analogous to how mass resists a change in velocity in mechanics.

Mutual induction is the same idea applied between two separate coils placed near each other. Changing the current in coil 1 changes the flux not only through coil 1 itself but also through coil 2, if the two coils are close enough that some of coil 1's field lines pass through coil 2 as well. That changing flux through coil 2 induces its own EMF in coil 2 — without any wire directly connecting the two coils at all. This is the entire operating principle behind transformers, which you'll meet in the next section: energy is transferred from one coil to another purely through a shared, changing magnetic field.

Self and Mutual Inductance
EMF_self = −L (ΔI / Δt)      EMF_mutual = −M (ΔI₁ / Δt)
L = self-inductance (H, henries)  |  M = mutual inductance (H)  |  ΔI/Δt = rate of change of current (A/s)
Applications
  • Self-inductance: chokes and filters in power supplies smooth out current fluctuations.
  • Mutual inductance: transformers, wireless chargers, and RFID readers all transfer energy via mutual induction.
  • Ignition coils use rapid self-inductive collapse to generate a high-voltage spark.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Self-Induced EMF from Changing Current
Given

L = 0.5 H

Current changes from 2 A to 6 A in 0.2 s

Find: self-induced EMF

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the change in current
ΔI = 6 − 2 = 4 A
Step 2 — Write the self-induction formula
EMF = L(ΔI/Δt)
Step 3 — Substitute the values (magnitude)
EMF = 0.5 × (4/0.2)
Step 4 — Divide, then multiply
4 / 0.2 = 20; then 0.5 × 20 = 10
EMF = 10 V
Final AnswerEMF = 10 V, opposing the rising current
Ex 2Finding Self-Inductance
Given

Induced EMF = 6 V

Current changes at a rate of 3 A/s

Find: self-inductance L

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Rearrange EMF = L(ΔI/Δt) for L
L = EMF / (ΔI/Δt)
Step 2 — Substitute and divide
L = 6 / 3 = 2 H
Final AnswerL = 2 H
Ex 3Mutual Induction Between Two Coils
Given

Mutual inductance, M = 0.3 H

Current in coil 1 changes from 1 A to 4 A in 0.5 s

Find: EMF induced in coil 2

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the change in current in coil 1
ΔI₁ = 4 − 1 = 3 A
Step 2 — Write the mutual induction formula
EMF₂ = M(ΔI₁/Δt)
Step 3 — Substitute the values (magnitude)
EMF₂ = 0.3 × (3/0.5)
Step 4 — Divide, then multiply
3 / 0.5 = 6; then 0.3 × 6 = 1.8
EMF₂ = 1.8 V
Final AnswerEMF₂ = 1.8 V induced in coil 2
6.23

Transformers: Step-Up and Step-Down

Using mutual induction to change AC voltage up or down.

Definition

A transformer is a device that uses mutual induction between two coils (primary and secondary), wound around a shared iron core, to change an alternating voltage from one level to another.

Explanation. A transformer has two separate coils of wire wrapped around the same iron core, with no direct electrical connection between them at all — the two circuits are entirely separate. An alternating current in the primary coil creates a constantly changing magnetic flux in the iron core (iron is used because it channels magnetic field lines extremely efficiently). That same changing flux threads through the secondary coil too, inducing its own alternating EMF there by mutual induction, exactly as described in the previous section. Because the core carries essentially all of the flux from the primary to the secondary, almost none of the magnetic field escapes and gets "wasted," making transformers remarkably efficient.

What determines whether the secondary voltage is higher or lower than the primary is simply the ratio of turns between the two coils. Each turn of the secondary coil experiences the same changing flux as each turn of the primary, so it induces the same EMF per turn — meaning the total secondary voltage scales directly with how many turns the secondary coil has, relative to the primary. A step-up transformer has more turns on the secondary than the primary, boosting the voltage (used to send electricity long distances at high voltage, minimising power loss in the cables). A step-down transformer has fewer turns on the secondary, reducing the voltage back down to safer levels for homes and appliances.

Types of Transformers

Step-Up Transformer

More secondary turns than primary; increases voltage, decreases current.

Step-Down Transformer

Fewer secondary turns than primary; decreases voltage, increases current.

Transformer Equation
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np      (assuming 100% efficiency: Vp·Ip = Vs·Is)
Vp, Vs = primary, secondary voltage (V)  |  Np, Ns = primary, secondary turns  |  Ip, Is = primary, secondary current (A)
Advantages
  • Very high efficiency (often over 95%).
  • Allow safe, low-loss long-distance power transmission at high voltage.
  • No moving parts, so very reliable and low maintenance.
Disadvantages
  • Only work with AC, not DC (since DC produces no changing flux).
  • Some energy is still lost as heat in the core and windings.
  • Large power transformers are heavy and require careful cooling.
Applications
  • Power grid substations step voltage up for transmission, then down for household use.
  • Phone and laptop chargers use small step-down transformers (or their electronic equivalents).
  • Doorbell circuits and some toys use step-down transformers to produce safe low voltages.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Step-Down Transformer Voltage
Given

Vp = 240 V

Np = 1000 turns

Ns = 50 turns

Find: secondary voltage Vs

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the transformer equation
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np
Step 2 — Rearrange to make Vs the subject
Vs = Vp × (Ns / Np)
Step 3 — Substitute the values
Vs = 240 × (50 / 1000)
Step 4 — Simplify the fraction, then multiply
50/1000 = 0.05; then 240 × 0.05 = 12
Vs = 12 V
Final AnswerVs = 12 V — this is a step-down transformer
Ex 2Step-Up Transformer — Finding Turns
Given

Vp = 200 V, Vs = 10,000 V

Np = 100 turns

Find: number of secondary turns, Ns

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the transformer equation and rearrange for Ns
Ns = Np × (Vs / Vp)
Step 2 — Substitute the values
Ns = 100 × (10,000 / 200)
Step 3 — Simplify the fraction
10,000 / 200 = 50
Step 4 — Multiply
Ns = 100 × 50 = 5000 turns
Final AnswerNs = 5000 turns
Ex 3Current in an Ideal Transformer
Given

Vp = 120 V, Ip = 5 A

Vs = 12 V

Find: secondary current Is (assume 100% efficiency)

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall that power in equals power out for an ideal transformer
Vp·Ip = Vs·Is
Step 2 — Rearrange to make Is the subject
Is = (Vp·Ip) / Vs
Step 3 — Find the primary power first
Vp·Ip = 120 × 5 = 600 W
Step 4 — Divide by the secondary voltage
Is = 600 / 12 = 50 A
Final AnswerIs = 50 A
Common mistake

Forgetting that current changes in the opposite ratio to voltage — a step-down transformer (lower voltage) always has a higher secondary current.

Maxwell's Equations

The four equations that unify everything in this chapter into a single, complete theory.

Watch — Maxwell's Equations: The Ultimate Beginner's Guide
Maxwell's Equations Explained
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Maxwell's Equations are four equations that together completely describe classical electricity and magnetism, showing that electric and magnetic fields are two aspects of a single unified electromagnetic field.

Explanation. Every law you've studied in this chapter — Coulomb's Law, the absence of magnetic monopoles (every magnet has both a north and south pole, never just one), Faraday's Law, and Ampère's Law — turns out to be one piece of a single, more complete four-part theory, assembled by James Clerk Maxwell in the 1860s. Individually, these four laws had already been discovered by Coulomb, Gauss, Faraday, and Ampère; Maxwell's own contribution was noticing a missing piece and adding it, which revealed something nobody had expected: light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

The Four Maxwell Equations

1. Gauss's Law (Electricity)

Electric field lines radiate from electric charges — this is the field version of Coulomb's Law you met in section 6.4.

2. Gauss's Law (Magnetism)

Magnetic field lines never radiate from a single point — there are no magnetic monopoles, only north-south pairs.

3. Faraday's Law

A changing magnetic field creates an electric field — the same principle from section 6.21, generalised beyond just circuits.

4. Ampère-Maxwell Law

Electric currents create magnetic fields (Ampère's Law, section 6.16) — and Maxwell added that a changing electric field does too.

That fourth equation is Maxwell's own addition, and it's the key to the whole story. Faraday had shown that a changing magnetic field creates an electric field; Maxwell reasoned that, for the theory to be mathematically consistent, a changing electric field must symmetrically create a magnetic field too. Once both directions of this relationship were in place, Maxwell realised something remarkable: a changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field, which creates a changing electric field, which creates a changing magnetic field — an endless, self-sustaining chain reaction that could propagate through empty space without needing any wire or medium at all. Calculating the speed this disturbance would travel at, using nothing but the constants ε₀ and μ₀ you've already met in this chapter, gave exactly the known speed of light — proving that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

Applications
  • All of radio, television, mobile phone, and Wi-Fi communication relies on generating and detecting electromagnetic waves.
  • Optics and photonics are, at their foundation, applications of Maxwell's equations to light.
  • Radar and remote sensing depend on predicting exactly how electromagnetic waves propagate and reflect.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Identifying Which Maxwell Equation Applies
Given

A bar magnet is cut in half

Find: which Maxwell equation explains what happens to its poles

Full Step-by-Step Reasoning
Step 1 — Recall Gauss's Law for magnetism
This law states there are no magnetic monopoles — magnetic field lines always form closed loops with both a north and south end.
Step 2 — Apply it to the cut magnet
Cutting a bar magnet in half doesn't isolate a single pole — each half simply becomes its own smaller magnet, with its own north and south pole.
Final AnswerBoth halves become complete magnets with their own N and S poles, as required by Gauss's Law for magnetism.
Ex 2Finding the Speed of an Electromagnetic Wave
Given

ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m

μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A

Find: speed of an electromagnetic wave, c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀)

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Multiply ε₀ and μ₀
ε₀μ₀ = (8.85×10⁻¹²)(4π×10⁻⁷)
Step 2 — Multiply the coefficients, then combine powers of ten
8.85 × 4π ≈ 8.85 × 12.566 ≈ 111.2
ε₀μ₀ ≈ 111.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 1.112×10⁻¹⁷
Step 3 — Take the square root
√(1.112×10⁻¹⁷) ≈ 3.33×10⁻⁹
Step 4 — Take the reciprocal to find c
c = 1 / (3.33×10⁻⁹) ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s
Final Answerc ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s — exactly the known speed of light
6.25

Electromagnetic Oscillations

How energy sloshes back and forth between a capacitor and an inductor.

Watch — AC Circuits Basics, Impedance, Resonant Frequency
AC Circuits, Impedance & Resonance
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

An LC circuit (inductor-capacitor circuit) oscillates energy back and forth between the capacitor's electric field and the inductor's magnetic field, at a natural frequency f = 1/(2π√(LC)).

Explanation. Charge a capacitor, then connect it directly to an inductor with no battery involved, and something remarkable happens: the circuit doesn't just settle down, it oscillates on its own. The charged capacitor starts discharging through the inductor, and as current builds up in the inductor, its magnetic field grows, storing energy there instead. Once the capacitor is fully discharged, the inductor's collapsing magnetic field — which, remember, opposes any change in current — keeps pushing current in the same direction, charging the capacitor back up, but with reversed polarity. The whole process then repeats in reverse, and the cycle continues, energy sloshing endlessly between the capacitor's electric field and the inductor's magnetic field.

This behaviour is mathematically identical to a mass oscillating on a spring: the capacitor plays the role of the spring (storing potential energy), the inductor plays the role of the mass (storing kinetic-like energy through its opposition to changing current), and the whole system oscillates at a natural frequency determined entirely by L and C. A larger inductance, like a heavier mass, slows the oscillation down; a larger capacitance, like a softer spring, also slows it down. In any real circuit, some resistance is always present, gradually draining energy away as heat, so real LC oscillations slowly die out rather than continuing forever — but the ideal case captures the essential physics behind every radio tuner and every wireless signal.

Natural Frequency of an LC Circuit
f = 1 / (2π√(LC))
f = natural (resonant) frequency (Hz)  |  L = inductance (H)  |  C = capacitance (F)
Applications
  • Radio tuners use an LC circuit's resonant frequency to select one station out of many.
  • Wireless communication relies on LC oscillators to generate carrier frequencies.
  • MRI machines and metal detectors both exploit resonant LC behaviour.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Finding the Natural Frequency
Given

L = 0.02 H

C = 5 × 10⁻⁶ F

Find: natural frequency f

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
f = 1 / (2π√(LC))
Step 2 — Multiply L and C
LC = 0.02 × 5×10⁻⁶ = 1×10⁻⁷
Step 3 — Take the square root
√(1×10⁻⁷) ≈ 3.162×10⁻⁴
Step 4 — Multiply by 2π
2π × 3.162×10⁻⁴ ≈ 1.987×10⁻³
Step 5 — Take the reciprocal
f = 1 / (1.987×10⁻³) ≈ 503 Hz
Final Answerf ≈ 503 Hz
Ex 2Effect of Doubling the Capacitance
Given

Original frequency, f = 1000 Hz; capacitance is doubled, inductance unchanged

Find: new frequency

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall f ∝ 1/√C
Since f = 1/(2π√(LC)), doubling C scales f by 1/√2.
Step 2 — Apply the scale factor
f_new = 1000 × (1/√2) ≈ 1000 × 0.707 ≈ 707 Hz
Final Answerf_new ≈ 707 Hz
6.26

AC Voltage and Current: Inductors, Capacitors and Resistors

Why AC circuits need a whole new idea beyond plain resistance.

Definition

In an AC circuit, inductors and capacitors oppose current in a way that depends on frequency, called reactance. Combined with ordinary resistance, the total opposition to AC current is called impedance (Z).

Explanation. Ohm's Law (V = IR) works perfectly for resistors in any circuit, AC or DC, because a resistor's opposition to current doesn't care how fast the current is changing. Inductors and capacitors are different — how much they oppose an alternating current depends heavily on how quickly that current is alternating. An inductor, remember, opposes any change in current (section 6.22); the faster the AC current changes direction, the harder the inductor fights it, so inductive reactance increases with frequency. A capacitor works the opposite way: at low frequencies, it has time to fully charge and block current, but at high frequencies, it barely charges before the current reverses again, letting current pass through almost freely — so capacitive reactance decreases with frequency.

In a circuit containing a resistor, inductor, and capacitor together (an RLC circuit), the total opposition to current — impedance — combines all three effects. Because inductive and capacitive reactance push against each other in opposite ways as frequency changes, there's always a special frequency where they exactly cancel out, leaving only the resistance to oppose the current. This is called resonance, and at that frequency, the circuit allows the maximum possible current to flow — precisely the effect a radio tuner exploits to isolate a single frequency from all the others in the air.

Reactance and Impedance
X_L = 2πfL      X_C = 1 / (2πfC)      Z = √(R² + (X_L − X_C)²)
X_L = inductive reactance (Ω)  |  X_C = capacitive reactance (Ω)  |  Z = total impedance (Ω)  |  f = frequency (Hz)  |  R = resistance (Ω)
Applications
  • Audio crossovers in speakers use reactance to send high frequencies to tweeters and low frequencies to woofers.
  • Radio and Wi-Fi tuning circuits use resonance to select one frequency out of many.
  • Power factor correction in industrial electrical systems manages reactance to improve efficiency.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Finding Inductive Reactance
Given

L = 0.1 H

f = 60 Hz

Find: inductive reactance X_L

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
X_L = 2πfL
Step 2 — Substitute the values
X_L = 2π × 60 × 0.1
Step 3 — Multiply the coefficients
2 × 60 × 0.1 = 12
X_L = 12π
Step 4 — Multiply by π ≈ 3.1416
X_L ≈ 37.7 Ω
Final AnswerX_L ≈ 37.7 Ω
Ex 2Finding Capacitive Reactance
Given

C = 20 × 10⁻⁶ F

f = 50 Hz

Find: capacitive reactance X_C

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
X_C = 1 / (2πfC)
Step 2 — Work out the denominator
2π × 50 × 20×10⁻⁶ = 2π × 0.001 ≈ 0.00628
Step 3 — Take the reciprocal
X_C = 1 / 0.00628 ≈ 159.2 Ω
Final AnswerX_C ≈ 159.2 Ω
Ex 3Total Impedance of an RLC Circuit
Given

R = 30 Ω

X_L = 40 Ω

X_C = 10 Ω

Find: total impedance Z

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the net reactance
X_L − X_C = 40 − 10 = 30 Ω
Step 2 — Write the impedance formula
Z = √(R² + (X_L−X_C)²)
Step 3 — Square both terms
R² = 30² = 900; (X_L−X_C)² = 30² = 900
Step 4 — Add and take the square root
Z = √(900+900) = √1800 ≈ 42.4 Ω
Final AnswerZ ≈ 42.4 Ω
Ex 4Current at a Given Impedance
Given

Supply voltage, V = 120 V

Impedance, Z = 42.4 Ω

Find: current I

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Apply Ohm's Law using impedance in place of resistance
I = V / Z
Step 2 — Substitute and divide
I = 120 / 42.4 ≈ 2.83 A
Final AnswerI ≈ 2.83 A

  • Faraday's Law: EMF = −N(ΔΦ/Δt). Lenz's Law: induced current opposes the change that caused it.
  • Self induction: a coil opposes changes in its own current. Mutual induction: a changing current in one coil induces EMF in another.
  • Transformers: Vs/Vp = Ns/Np; step-up increases voltage, step-down decreases it.
  • Maxwell's Equations: four laws unifying electricity and magnetism, revealing light as an electromagnetic wave.
  • LC oscillation: f = 1/(2π√(LC)) — energy sloshes between capacitor and inductor.
  • AC circuits: reactance (X_L = 2πfL, X_C = 1/2πfC) and impedance (Z = √(R²+(X_L−X_C)²)) replace simple resistance.
