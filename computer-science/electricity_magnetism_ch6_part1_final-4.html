6.1

Force in Nature

Where does "electric force" fit among all the forces in the universe?

Physicists have found that every push and pull ever observed can be traced back to just four fundamental forces: gravity, the electromagnetic force, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force. Two of these act only inside the atomic nucleus; two act over long distances and shape everyday life.

The Four Fundamental Forces of Nature

The Four Fundamental Forces

Gravitational Force

Acts between any two masses. Always attractive, extremely weak, infinite range.

Electromagnetic Force

Acts between electric charges. Attractive or repulsive, about 10³⁶ times stronger than gravity. The subject of this chapter.

Strong Nuclear Force

Binds protons and neutrons in the nucleus. Very strong, very short range.

Weak Nuclear Force

Responsible for radioactive decay. Weaker than the strong force, very short range.

Definition

The electromagnetic force is the force between electrically charged particles. This chapter unpacks it in detail — first as electrostatics (charges at rest), then as current electricity, and in later parts as magnetism.

Explanation. To see why physicists bother separating these four forces at all, picture yourself holding a small magnet near a paperclip lying on a table. The magnet lifts the paperclip clean off the surface — beating Earth's entire gravitational pull with a piece of metal you can hold in one hand. That single demonstration tells you something important: the electromagnetic force, weak as a fridge magnet feels, is astronomically stronger than gravity at the scale of everyday objects. The only reason gravity feels dominant in daily life is that Earth's mass is enormous, while most objects around you are electrically neutral overall (their positive and negative charges cancel out), so the electromagnetic force mostly disappears from view — except when it doesn't, as with your fridge magnet.

Now picture zooming in past atoms, into the nucleus itself. Protons, all positively charged, are crammed together there — and by Coulomb's Law (which you'll meet properly in section 6.3), they should be violently repelling each other. They don't fly apart because an even stronger force, the strong nuclear force, is gripping them together at that tiny range. Zoom out just slightly further — beyond the nucleus — and the strong force vanishes almost to nothing, leaving the electromagnetic force to govern everything from chemical bonds to the electricity in your walls. This is the pattern worth remembering: each of the four forces "wins" in its own regime of distance and scale, and understanding electricity means understanding exactly where the electromagnetic force's regime begins and ends.

Why it matters

Gravity shapes planets and galaxies; the electromagnetic force shapes chemistry, biology, and every circuit ever built. Almost every piece of technology you use runs on it.

6.2

Electrostatics: Electric Charge and Its Properties

Meet the actor at the centre of this chapter: electric charge.

Definition

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electromagnetic field. Charge comes in two types — positive and negative — carried respectively (in ordinary matter) by protons and electrons.

Explanation. Every atom is built from a nucleus of protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons. Protons carry positive charge, electrons carry negative charge, and neutrons carry none at all. In a normal, neutral atom, the number of protons exactly equals the number of electrons, so their charges cancel out and the atom shows no charge to the outside world. "Charging" an object is really just disturbing this balance — either stripping electrons away (leaving a surplus of protons, so the object reads positive) or piling extra electrons on (leaving a surplus of electrons, so the object reads negative). Nothing is created or destroyed in this process; charge only ever moves from one place to another, which is exactly what "charge is conserved" means in practice.

The "like repels, unlike attracts" rule becomes intuitive once you picture it this way: bring two negatively-charged objects together, and you're really bringing two clouds of surplus electrons close to each other. Electrons already repel each other (they're both negative), so the two objects push apart. Bring a positive and a negative object together instead, and the negative object's surplus electrons are drawn toward the positive object's deficit — an attraction. This single mental picture, of counting surplus and deficit electrons, explains everything else you'll read about charge in this section.

Characteristics (Properties) of Charge

  • Like charges repel, unlike charges attract.
  • Charge is quantised — it always comes in whole-number multiples of e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
  • Charge is conserved — the total charge in a closed system never changes.
  • Charge is a scalar — magnitude and sign only, no direction.
  • Charge is measured in coulombs (C); everyday charges are usually µC or nC.

Types of Charging

An object becomes "charged" when it gains an excess or deficit of electrons. There are three everyday methods:

1. Friction

Rubbing two different materials transfers electrons from one surface to the other.

2. Conduction (Contact)

A charged object touches a neutral conductor; charge is shared, giving the same sign result.

3. Induction

A charged object is brought near (not touching) a neutral conductor, then it is earthed, giving the opposite sign result.

Advantages
  • Friction is simple and needs no equipment.
  • Conduction is fast and predictable.
  • Induction doesn't deplete the original charged object.
Disadvantages
  • Friction gives small, hard-to-control charge.
  • Conduction reduces the charge on the original object (shared, not copied).
  • Induction needs more steps (earthing) and only works on conductors.
Applications
  • Photocopiers and laser printers charge a drum to attract toner.
  • Electrostatic precipitators charge smoke particles to remove them from factory exhaust.
  • Spray painting charges paint droplets for even, low-waste coverage.
Common mistake

Mixing up conduction and induction. Remember: conduction = touching (same sign result); induction = no touching (opposite sign result).

Charge on rod before contact, Q = +8 µC

Charge on identical neutral sphere = 0 µC

Find: final charge on each sphere after touching

The objects touch directly, so this is charging by conduction. Total charge is conserved and shared equally between two identical conductors.

Ex 3Charging by Induction
Given

A negatively charged rod is brought near (not touching) a neutral metal sphere. The sphere is earthed while the rod stays nearby, then the earth wire is removed, then the rod is taken away.

Find: the final sign of charge on the sphere

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Consider the effect of bringing the rod near
The rod's negative charge repels the sphere's free electrons to the far side, leaving the near side positive and the far side negative (charge separation, no net charge yet).
Step 2 — Consider what happens while earthed
The repelled electrons on the far side can now escape down the earth wire to the ground.
Step 3 — Remove the earth wire
This traps the sphere with a permanent deficit of electrons, while the rod is still nearby holding the remaining charge in place.
Step 4 — Remove the rod
With the rod gone, the remaining positive charge spreads evenly over the sphere.
Final AnswerThe sphere is left positively charged.

Given

Sphere A charge = +6 µC

Sphere B charge = −2 µC

Spheres are identical in size; they touch, then separate

Find: final charge on each sphere

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Apply conservation of charge
Total charge before contact equals total charge after contact:
Q_total = (+6 µC) + (−2 µC) = +4 µC
Step 2 — Share equally (identical conductors)
Q_each = 4 µC ÷ 2 = 2 µC
Final AnswerEach sphere ends with +2 µC
Ex 5Why Friction Between Identical Materials Fails
Given

Two pieces of the same plastic are rubbed together.

Find: why little or no charge builds up

Full Step-by-Step Reasoning
Step 1 — Recall what friction charging needs
Charging by friction relies on one material holding electrons more strongly than the other (a difference in electron affinity).
Step 2 — Compare the two materials
Since both pieces are identical, they have equal electron affinity — neither "wins" the tug-of-war for electrons.
Step 3 — Conclude
Electrons that transfer one way are just as likely to transfer back, so no significant net charge builds up on either surface.
Final AnswerNo noticeable charge, because both materials have equal electron affinity.

Given

Charge gained by a balloon, Q = −4 × 10⁻⁸ C

Elementary charge, e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

Find: number of electrons transferred onto the balloon

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Start from Q = n × e and rearrange for n
n = Q / e
Step 2 — Substitute the magnitudes (ignore sign for counting electrons)
n = (4 × 10⁻⁸) / (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)
Step 3 — Divide the coefficients, then the powers of ten
4 ÷ 1.6 = 2.5; powers: 10⁻⁸ ÷ 10⁻¹⁹ = 10¹¹
n = 2.5 × 10¹¹ electrons

Ex 7Induction on Two Neighbouring Spheres
Given

Two identical neutral metal spheres, X and Y, touch each other. A negatively charged rod is brought near X (not touching). While the rod is still there, the spheres are separated, then the rod is removed.

Find: the final charge type on each sphere

Full Step-by-Step Reasoning
Step 1 — Consider the effect of the rod on the touching pair
The rod repels free electrons in the combined X–Y conductor away from X, toward Y (electrons move away from the negative rod).
Step 2 — Separate the spheres while the rod is still present
Separating now "locks in" the charge distribution: X is left with a deficit of electrons, Y with a surplus.
Step 3 — Remove the rod
With no rod nearby, each sphere's trapped imbalance remains as its own net charge.
Final AnswerSphere X (nearer the rod) ends up positively charged; sphere Y ends up negatively charged.
6.3

Coulomb's Law and the Principle of Superposition

The one formula that tells you exactly how hard two charges push or pull on each other.

Watch — Coulomb's Law: Net Electric Force and Point Charges
Coulomb's Law
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Coulomb's Law states that the force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Explanation. Imagine two small charged balls hanging from threads, both carrying a positive charge. As you bring them closer together, you'd feel a growing push apart. Move them twice as close, and that push doesn't just double — it quadruples, because the force depends on the square of the distance, not the distance itself. This is what makes Coulomb's Law an "inverse-square law," the same mathematical shape gravity follows between planets.

Now suppose one ball carries three times as much charge as the other originally did. The push between them also grows — but only in direct proportion. Triple one charge, and the force triples; triple both, and the force grows ninefold. This is the "directly proportional to the product of the charges" part of the law: each charge contributes its own multiplying effect to the total force.

What decides whether the two balls push apart or pull together is simply whether their charges match. Two positives, or two negatives, always push apart. A positive and a negative always pull together. Coulomb's Law captures all three of these ideas — how charge size, distance, and charge sign combine — in a single formula.

Characteristics of Coulomb's Law

  • Inverse-square relationship: doubling the distance cuts the force to a quarter.
  • Directly proportional to each charge: doubling either charge doubles the force.
  • Acts along the line joining the charges.
  • Obeys Newton's third law: equal and opposite forces on each charge.
  • Depends on the medium between the charges (via the constant k).
Coulomb's Law
F = k·q₁·q₂ / r²
F = force (N)  |  q₁, q₂ = charges (C)  |  r = separation (m)  |  k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
Like signs → repulsion. Unlike signs → attraction.
Applications
  • Explains atomic structure — why electrons are bound to the nucleus.
  • Used in ion traps and mass spectrometers to control charged particles.
  • Underlies electrostatic precipitators and photocopiers.

The Principle of Superposition

Definition

When a charge feels forces from more than one other charge, the net force is the vector sum of each individual Coulomb force, calculated separately.

Explanation. Suppose a charge sits between two other charges, one pulling it left and one pushing it further left too. Superposition simply says: work out each force exactly as if the other charge weren't there, then add the results together like ordinary vectors — same direction adds, opposite direction subtracts, and forces at an angle combine using Pythagoras or trigonometry. Nothing about Coulomb's Law changes when more charges are present; you just repeat the same calculation once per pair of charges, then combine.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Force Between Two Point Charges
Given

q1 = +3 × 10⁻⁶ C

q2 = +5 × 10⁻⁶ C

r = 0.2 m

k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²

Find: force F between them

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write down the formula
F = k·q1·q2 / r²
Step 2 — Substitute the known values
F = (9×10⁹)(3×10⁻⁶)(5×10⁻⁶) / (0.2)²
Step 3 — Multiply the numerator: coefficients first, then powers of ten
9 × 3 × 5 = 135; powers: 10⁹ × 10⁻⁶ × 10⁻⁶ = 10⁻³
Numerator = 135 × 10⁻³ = 0.135
Step 4 — Work out the denominator
(0.2)² = 0.04
Step 5 — Divide numerator by denominator
F = 0.135 / 0.04 = 3.375 N
Step 6 — Determine the nature of the force
Both charges are positive (like charges), so the force is repulsive.
Final AnswerF = 3.375 N, repulsive
Common mistake

Forgetting to convert µC to C, or squaring the whole denominator expression instead of just r.

Ex 2Force Between Unlike Charges
Given

q1 = +2 × 10⁻⁶ C

q2 = −4 × 10⁻⁶ C

r = 0.1 m

Find: magnitude and nature of the force

Full Step-by-Step Solution

Step 2 — Substitute the magnitudes
F = (9×10⁹)(2×10⁻⁶)(4×10⁻⁶) / (0.1)²

9 × 2 × 4 = 72; powers: 10⁹ × 10⁻⁶ × 10⁻⁶ = 10⁻³
Numerator = 72 × 10⁻³ = 0.072
Step 4 — Work out the denominator
(0.1)² = 0.01
Step 5 — Divide

Opposite signs, so the force is attractive.

Ex 3Finding an Unknown Charge
Given

Two identical charges q, each

r = 0.3 m

F = 0.9 N (repulsive)

Find: q

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula for identical charges
F = k·q² / r²
Step 2 — Rearrange to make q² the subject
q² = F·r² / k
Step 3 — Substitute the values
q² = (0.9 × 0.3²) / (9×10⁹)
Step 4 — Work out 0.3² and multiply by 0.9
0.3² = 0.09;   0.9 × 0.09 = 0.081
Step 5 — Divide by k
q² = 0.081 / (9×10⁹) = 9×10⁻¹²
Step 6 — Take the square root of both sides
q = √(9×10⁻¹²) = 3×10⁻⁶ C

Ex 4Effect of Doubling the Distance
Given

Original force, F = 2 N at r = 0.4 m

New distance = 0.8 m (doubled)

Find: new force, without recalculating from scratch

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall the proportionality relationship
Coulomb's Law gives F ∝ 1/r² — force is inversely proportional to the square of distance.
Step 2 — Work out the scale factor for doubling r
New factor = 1/(2)² = 1/4
Step 3 — Apply the scale factor to the original force
F_new = 2 N × 1/4 = 0.5 N
Final AnswerF_new = 0.5 N
Ex 5Force in a Medium Other Than Air
Given

q1 = q2 = +6 × 10⁻⁶ C

r = 0.5 m

Relative permittivity, εᵣ = 4

Find: force F in this medium

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the medium's constant
A medium reduces Coulomb's constant by a factor of εᵣ:
k_medium = k_air / εᵣ = (9×10⁹) / 4 = 2.25×10⁹
Step 2 — Write Coulomb's Law with k_medium
F = k_medium · q1 · q2 / r²
Step 3 — Substitute the values
F = (2.25×10⁹)(6×10⁻⁶)(6×10⁻⁶) / (0.5)²
Step 4 — Multiply the numerator
2.25 × 6 × 6 = 81; powers: 10⁹ × 10⁻⁶ × 10⁻⁶ = 10⁻³
Numerator = 81 × 10⁻³ = 0.081
Step 5 — Work out the denominator and divide
(0.5)² = 0.25;   F = 0.081 / 0.25 = 0.324 N
Final AnswerF = 0.324 N, repulsive
Ex 6Superposition — Three Charges on a Line
Given

q1 = +2 µC at x = 0

q2 = +3 µC at x = 0.2 m

q3 = −4 µC at x = 0.5 m

Find: net force on q2

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the force on q2 from q1
Distance q1 to q2 = 0.2 m. Both positive → repels q2 in the +x direction.
F₁₂ = (9×10⁹)(2×10⁻⁶)(3×10⁻⁶)/(0.2)² = 0.054/0.04 = 1.35 N (+x)
Step 2 — Find the force on q2 from q3
Distance q2 to q3 = 0.3 m. Opposite signs → attracts q2 toward q3, also +x.
F₃₂ = (9×10⁹)(3×10⁻⁶)(4×10⁻⁶)/(0.3)² = 0.108/0.09 = 1.2 N (+x)
Step 3 — Add the two forces (same direction)
F_net = 1.35 + 1.2 = 2.55 N
Final AnswerF_net = 2.55 N in the +x direction
Ex 7Superposition at Right Angles
Given

Two charges of +5 µC each at opposite corners of a square of side 0.3 m

A +2 µC charge sits at a third corner (adjacent to both)

Find: net force on the +2 µC charge

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Identify the distances
The +2 µC charge is 0.3 m from each +5 µC charge along the two adjacent sides of the square.
Step 2 — Find the magnitude of each individual force
F = (9×10⁹)(5×10⁻⁶)(2×10⁻⁶)/(0.3)² = 0.09/0.09 = 1 N
Step 3 — Recognise the two forces act at right angles
Both are repulsive and act along the two perpendicular sides of the square.
Step 4 — Combine perpendicular forces using Pythagoras
F_net = √(1² + 1²) = √2 ≈ 1.41 N
Final AnswerF_net ≈ 1.41 N, directed along the square's diagonal, away from both charges

Electric Field and Electric Potential

How a charge "reaches out" and affects the space around it.

Electric Field and Potential
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗

Electric Field

Definition

An electric field is the region of space around a charge in which another charge would experience a force. It is a vector, pointing away from positive charges and toward negative charges.

Explanation. A field is a way of describing "what's here, even if nothing is here to feel it yet." A positive charge sitting alone in a room doesn't need anything else nearby to have an effect — it quietly sets up a field filling the space around it, the way a heater changes the temperature of a room whether or not anyone walks in to feel it. If you then bring a second, small "test" charge into that space, the field tells you exactly which way it will be pushed or pulled, and how hard, at every point. This is why field strength is defined as E = F/q — it's the force per unit of test charge, stripping away the size of the test charge so that what's left describes only the space itself.

Types of Electric Fields

Uniform Field

Same magnitude and direction everywhere — e.g. between parallel charged plates.

Radial Field

Produced by a single point charge; spreads out and weakens with distance.

Electric Field Formulas
E = F / q      E = k·Q / r²
E = field strength (N/C)  |  F = force on test charge (N)  |  q = test charge (C)  |  Q = source charge (C)  |  r = distance (m)

Electric Potential

Definition

Electric potential (V) at a point is the work done per unit charge bringing a small positive test charge from infinity to that point. It is a scalar, measured in volts.

Explanation. Potential is best understood by comparison to height in gravity. Lifting a ball higher off the ground stores more gravitational potential energy in it, ready to be released as it falls. In exactly the same way, pushing a positive charge closer to another positive charge (against its repulsion) stores more electric potential energy in it, ready to be released the moment you let go and it shoots away. Electric potential, V, is simply that stored energy measured "per unit charge," so it describes the point in space itself rather than any particular charge sitting there — much like how a hill has a height whether or not a ball happens to be resting on it. This is also why potential is a scalar (just a number, no direction), whereas the electric field is a vector.

Electric Potential Formulas
V = k·Q / r      V = W / q
V = potential (V)  |  W = work done (J)  |  q = charge moved (C)
Applications
  • Field: Faraday cages, lightning rods, shielding electronics.
  • Potential: batteries, power supplies, voltage ratings.
  • Both: particle accelerators steer and speed up charged particles.

Capacitance

How well can a device "store" separated charge?

Watch — Capacitors: Basic Introduction
Capacitors Basic Introduction
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Capacitance (C) is the ability of a capacitor to store electric charge per unit of potential difference across it. Measured in farads (F); in practice, µF, nF, or pF.

Explanation. Picture two metal plates facing each other with a small gap between them, connected to a battery. The battery pushes electrons onto one plate (making it negative) and pulls electrons off the other (making it positive). As charge piles up, the plates start repelling any more charge from arriving — it gets harder and harder to add more, the same way a bucket gets harder to fill as the water level rises. Capacitance is a measure of exactly how much charge a particular capacitor can hold before that "pushback" reaches a given voltage: a wide, closely-spaced pair of plates can store far more charge at the same voltage than a narrow, widely-spaced pair, which is exactly why capacitance depends on plate area (A) and plate separation (d) in the formula.

The dielectric material between the plates matters too. Certain materials, when placed in an electric field, become mildly "polarised" themselves, partially cancelling the field between the plates. This lets the battery push even more charge onto the plates before reaching the same voltage — which is why inserting a dielectric with a higher relative permittivity, εᵣ, always increases capacitance.

Types of Capacitors

Parallel-Plate

Two flat plates separated by a dielectric — the simplest form.

Electrolytic

Thin oxide dielectric, high capacitance in small size, polarised.

Ceramic

Small, cheap, non-polarised, very common.

Variable

Capacitance adjustable by changing overlap area — used in tuning circuits.

Capacitance Formulas

C = capacitance (F)  |  Q = charge (C)  |  V = voltage (V)  |  ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² F/m  |  εᵣ = relative permittivity  |  A = plate area (m²)  |  d = plate separation (m)

  • Charges and discharges very quickly.
  • Very long lifespan.
  • No chemical reactions involved.
Disadvantages
  • Stores far less total energy than a battery.
  • Voltage drops as it discharges.
  • Electrolytic types can fail if reversed.

Electric Dipoles

What happens when a positive and a negative charge sit close together?

Watch — Torque on a Dipole in a Uniform Electric Field
Electric Dipole
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

An electric dipole is a pair of equal and opposite point charges, +q and −q, separated by a small distance d. It is characterised by its dipole moment, a vector pointing from the negative to the positive charge.

Explanation. Many real molecules aren't charged overall, but their positive and negative charge isn't distributed evenly either. A water molecule, for example, has its oxygen atom pulling electrons slightly more strongly than its two hydrogen atoms, leaving the oxygen end slightly negative and the hydrogen end slightly positive — even though the molecule as a whole is neutral. This "lopsided" charge distribution is exactly what a dipole models: not a single big charge, but a small separation between an equal amount of positive and negative charge. The dipole moment, p = qd, captures both how much charge is separated and how far apart it sits, combined into one vector that points from the negative end toward the positive end — which is also the direction the molecule tends to line up when an external electric field is applied.

Dipole Moment
p = q × d
p = dipole moment (C·m)  |  q = magnitude of each charge (C)  |  d = separation (m)
Applications
  • Water molecules are natural dipoles — this is why water dissolves salts and why water molecules stick together.
  • Microwave ovens heat food by making dipolar water molecules rotate rapidly in an oscillating field.

Energy Stored in an Electric Field

Separating charge takes work — and that work is stored, not lost.

Watch — Energy Stored in a Capacitor (Derivation)
Energy Stored in a Capacitor
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Charging a capacitor means doing work against the growing repulsion of charge already on its plates. This work is stored as electrical potential energy in the field between the plates.

Explanation. Think about pushing the first bit of charge onto an empty capacitor plate — it's easy, since the plate starts out neutral and offers no resistance. But each additional bit of charge you push on has to fight the repulsion of all the charge that arrived before it, so the "cost" of adding charge keeps rising as the plate fills up. This is exactly like blowing up a balloon: the first breath is easy, but each further breath takes more effort as the balloon stretches tighter. All that effort doesn't vanish — it's stored as potential energy in the compressed electric field between the plates, ready to be released the moment the capacitor is allowed to discharge (which is exactly how a camera flash releases its stored energy in a fraction of a second).

Energy Stored in a Capacitor
U = ½QV  =  ½CV²  =  Q²/2C
U = energy (J)  |  Q = charge (C)  |  V = voltage (V)  |  C = capacitance (F)

Conductors and Insulators

Why charge flows freely through some materials and barely moves through others.

Watch — Conductors and Insulators (Khan Academy)
Conductors and Insulators
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

A conductor has many free electrons that move easily through it, allowing charge to flow — metals like copper are typical examples. An insulator has tightly bound electrons, so charge cannot flow through it easily — rubber, glass, and plastic are typical examples.

Explanation. Inside a metal, the outermost electrons of each atom aren't strongly tied to any single nucleus — they're shared across the whole structure, drifting almost like a fluid that fills the spaces between fixed metal ions. This "sea of electrons" is why metals conduct so well: apply even a small voltage, and the whole sea shifts slightly, creating a current, since the electrons were never firmly anchored in the first place. In an insulator, by contrast, every electron is tightly bound to its own atom (or shared only within a single molecule), with no spare electrons free to drift. Apply the same voltage, and almost nothing happens — the electrons simply aren't free to move far enough to constitute a meaningful current. Semiconductors sit in the middle: their electrons are normally bound like an insulator's, but a small amount of energy (heat, light, or an applied voltage) can free just enough of them to let a controllable trickle of current flow, which is exactly the property that makes transistors possible.

Types of Electrical Materials

Conductors

Free electrons move easily. Examples: copper, aluminium, silver.

Insulators

Electrons tightly bound. Examples: rubber, glass, plastic.

Semiconductors

Conduct a little; conductivity finely controllable. Examples: silicon, germanium.

Advantages
  • Conductors: efficient current flow, low energy loss.
  • Insulators: safety — prevent shocks and short circuits.
Disadvantages
  • Conductors: can cause shocks or short circuits if exposed.
  • Insulators: cannot carry current when it's actually needed.
Applications
  • House wiring uses a copper conductor wrapped in a plastic insulator.
  • Semiconductors are the backbone of transistors and computer chips.

Ohm's Law

The single most-used equation in circuit analysis.

Watch — Ohm's Law Explained
Ohm's Law
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Ohm's Law states that, at constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it.

Explanation. Go back to the water-pipe picture: if you increase the pressure (voltage) pushing water through a pipe of fixed width, more water flows through per second (current) — and the increase is directly proportional, so doubling the pressure doubles the flow, provided the pipe itself (resistance) doesn't change. Resistance is the constant that links the two: a wide, unobstructed pipe (low resistance) lets a lot of current flow for a given voltage, while a narrow, obstructed pipe (high resistance) only allows a little current for that same voltage. This is exactly what V = IR captures — voltage is what you supply, resistance is a fixed property of the component, and current is whatever results from dividing one by the other.

Ohm's Law
V = I × R
V = voltage (V)  |  I = current (A)  |  R = resistance (Ω)  |  Rearranged: I = V/R,  R = V/I

Resistor Combinations

Two ways to wire resistors together — with very different results.

Watch — Series and Parallel Circuits Explained
Series and Parallel Circuits
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗

Types of Resistor Combinations

Series

End-to-end connection; same current through each resistor; resistances add.

Parallel

Same two nodes; same voltage across each; current splits; combined resistance drops below the smallest resistor.

Explanation. In a series connection, resistors are chained one after another, so every electron travelling around the circuit has to pass through all of them in turn — there's only one possible path. Each resistor adds its own obstruction to that single path, so the total resistance is simply the sum of them all, exactly like adding more speed bumps one after another along the same road. In a parallel connection, by contrast, the resistors sit side by side, each offering its own separate path between the same two points. Adding another resistor in parallel is like opening another lane on the road: it gives current an additional route to take, so the overall resistance to traffic actually goes down, not up — which is why the parallel formula combines resistances as reciprocals rather than adding them directly, and why the combined result is always smaller than the smallest individual resistor.

Series Resistance
R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Parallel Resistance
1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

Analysing DC Circuits

Putting current, voltage, resistance, and combinations together.

Watch — Series and Parallel Circuit Analysis
Circuit Analysis
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗

A reliable method for any circuit:

  • Step 1 — Simplify: combine series/parallel resistors into a single total.
  • Step 2 — Apply Ohm's law to the simplified circuit to find total current.
  • Step 3 — Work back branch by branch, using shared current (series) or shared voltage (parallel).

Explanation. A circuit with a mix of series and parallel resistors can look intimidating at first, but the trick is to work from the outside in — the same way you'd solve a complicated bracketed expression in maths by dealing with the innermost brackets first. Any parallel block can be replaced, temporarily, by a single equivalent resistor using the parallel formula; once that's done, the circuit looks purely series, and you simply add resistances as normal. This lets you find one single total resistance for the entire circuit, from which the total current follows immediately from Ohm's law. The final step reverses the simplification: since you know the total current and where it splits, you can work back into the original parallel block and find exactly how much current (or voltage) each individual branch actually carries.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Full Circuit Analysis
Given

V = 12 V; R1 = 4 Ω in series with (R2 = 6 Ω parallel R3 = 3 Ω)

Find: (a) total resistance, (b) total current, (c) current through R2

Part (a) — Total Resistance
Step 1 — Combine the parallel pair
1/R_p = 1/6 + 1/3 = 3/6 = 1/2 → R_p = 2 Ω
Step 2 — Add the series resistor
R_total = R1 + R_p = 4 + 2 = 6 Ω
Part (b) — Total Current
Step 3 — Apply Ohm's law to the whole circuit
I_total = V / R_total = 12 / 6 = 2 A
Part (c) — Current Through R2
Step 4 — Find the voltage across the parallel block
All 2 A passes through R1 first, so:
V_p = I_total × R_p = 2 × 2 = 4 V
Step 5 — Apply Ohm's law to the R2 branch alone
Both branches in a parallel block share the same voltage (4 V):
I₂ = V_p / R2 = 4 / 6 = 0.667 A
Final AnswerR_total = 6 Ω  |  I_total = 2 A  |  I (through R2) ≈ 0.667 A
Common mistake

Applying the total current directly to R2. Inside a parallel block current splits — always find the block's voltage first.

Ex 2Series-Only Circuit
Given

V = 6 V; two 4 Ω resistors in series

Find: current and voltage across each resistor

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find total resistance
R_total = 4 + 4 = 8 Ω
Step 2 — Find total current
I = V / R_total = 6 / 8 = 0.75 A
Step 3 — Find voltage across each resistor
Same current flows through both in series:
V_each = I × R = 0.75 × 4 = 3 V
Step 4 — Check the voltages add up to the source
3 V + 3 V = 6 V ✓
Final AnswerI = 0.75 A; 3 V across each resistor
Ex 3Series-Parallel with Equal Parallel Resistors
Given

V = 10 V; R1 = 2 Ω in series with two 8 Ω resistors in parallel

Find: total resistance, total current, current through each 8 Ω resistor

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Combine the parallel pair
1/R_p = 1/8 + 1/8 = 2/8 = 1/4 → R_p = 4 Ω
Step 2 — Add the series resistor
R_total = 2 + 4 = 6 Ω
Step 3 — Find total current
I_total = 10 / 6 = 1.667 A
Step 4 — Find voltage across the parallel block
V_p = I_total × R_p = 1.667 × 4 = 6.667 V
Step 5 — Find current through each 8 Ω branch
I_each = V_p / 8 = 6.667 / 8 = 0.833 A
Step 6 — Check the branch currents add up
0.833 + 0.833 = 1.667 A ✓
Final AnswerR_total = 6 Ω; I_total = 1.667 A; 0.833 A through each 8 Ω resistor

Chapter 6, Part 1 Summary — Electrostatics & DC Circuits

  • Charge: + and − types, quantised (e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C), always conserved.
  • Charging methods: friction, conduction (same sign), induction (opposite sign).
  • Coulomb's Law: F = kq₁q₂/r²; superposition adds forces as vectors.
  • Field: E = F/q = kQ/r² (vector). Potential: V = kQ/r = W/q (scalar).
  • Capacitance: C = Q/V; energy U = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/2C.

  • Conductors, insulators, semiconductors differ in how freely electrons move.
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR. Series: R_total = ΣR. Parallel: 1/R_total = Σ(1/R).
