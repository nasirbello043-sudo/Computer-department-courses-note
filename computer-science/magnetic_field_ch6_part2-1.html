6.13

Magnetic Field

What a magnet actually does to the space around it — and how currents make magnets too.

Watch — Magnetism, Magnetic Field Force, Right Hand Rule
Magnetism & Magnetic Field Basics
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

A magnetic field is the region of space around a magnet or a moving charge (such as a current-carrying wire) in which a magnetic force can be detected. It is a vector, measured in tesla (T).

Explanation. You've probably felt a magnetic field without ever seeing it — holding two bar magnets and feeling them push apart or snap together before they even touch. That invisible "reach" is the magnetic field: the magnet doesn't need to touch anything to have an effect, it simply changes the space around it, and any other magnetic material (or moving charge) entering that space feels a force. Every magnet has two ends, called poles, conventionally labelled north (N) and south (S). Bring two north poles together, or two south poles, and they push apart; bring a north and a south together, and they snap together — the same "like repels, unlike attracts" pattern you saw with electric charge, just for magnetic poles instead.

What makes magnetism especially interesting is that magnetic fields aren't only produced by permanent magnets like the ones on your fridge — they're also produced by moving electric charge. Any wire carrying a current generates its own magnetic field circling around it, which is precisely the connection between electricity and magnetism this whole part of the chapter explores. Coil that same current-carrying wire into a loop, or many loops (a solenoid), and the individual field contributions from each loop add together, producing a field that looks remarkably like that of a bar magnet — strong and uniform inside the coil, weaker and spread out outside it.

Types of Magnetic Fields

Field of a Permanent Magnet

Fixed field produced by aligned magnetic domains inside certain materials (like iron).

Field of a Straight Current-Carrying Wire

Circles around the wire; direction given by the right-hand rule.

Field of a Solenoid

Many current loops stacked together; strong, uniform field inside, similar to a bar magnet.

Characteristics of Magnetic Field Lines

  • Field lines emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole (outside the magnet).
  • Field lines never cross.
  • Closer lines mean a stronger field.
  • Around a current-carrying wire, field lines form closed circles with no start or end (unlike electric field lines).
Applications
  • Compasses use Earth's magnetic field to indicate direction.
  • MRI scanners use extremely strong magnetic fields to image the body.
  • Electric motors and generators rely on magnetic fields interacting with currents.
  • Magnetic data storage (hard drives) encodes information using tiny magnetised regions.

6.14

Lorentz Force

The force a magnetic field exerts on a moving charge.

Watch — Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge in a Magnetic Field
Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

The Lorentz force is the force experienced by a charged particle moving through a magnetic field. Its magnitude is F = qvB sinθ, and its direction is given by the right-hand rule.

Explanation. A stationary charge sitting in a magnetic field feels absolutely nothing from that field — magnetism only "notices" charge that is moving. The moment that charge starts moving, though, it experiences a push, and that push has a very particular character: it always acts perpendicular to both the charge's velocity and the magnetic field direction. This is very different from Coulomb's Law, where the force acts straight along the line between two charges. Because the Lorentz force is always perpendicular to velocity, it never speeds up or slows down a moving charge — it only ever changes its direction, which is why charged particles in a magnetic field tend to move in circles or spirals rather than speeding away in a straight line.

The size of this force also depends on the angle between the charge's velocity and the field. If the charge moves exactly along the field lines (parallel to the field), it feels no magnetic force at all — sinθ = 0. If it moves exactly perpendicular to the field, it feels the maximum possible force — sinθ = 1. Everything in between is scaled by sinθ, which is exactly why the formula includes that term.

Lorentz Force (Magnetic Component)
F = q·v·B·sinθ
F = magnetic force (N)  |  q = charge (C)  |  v = speed of the charge (m/s)  |  B = magnetic field strength (T)  |  θ = angle between velocity and field
Applications
  • Particle accelerators use the Lorentz force to steer charged particles around circular paths.
  • Mass spectrometers separate ions by mass using their curved paths in a magnetic field.
  • Cathode-ray tubes (old TVs) steered electron beams using magnetic deflection.
  • The Van Allen radiation belts trap charged particles using Earth's magnetic field.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Force on a Charge Moving Perpendicular to the Field
Given

q = +3 × 10⁻⁶ C

v = 4 × 10⁴ m/s

B = 0.5 T

θ = 90° (perpendicular)

Find: force F

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
F = q·v·B·sinθ
Step 2 — Evaluate sinθ
sin(90°) = 1
Step 3 — Substitute all values
F = (3×10⁻⁶)(4×10⁴)(0.5)(1)
Step 4 — Multiply step by step
3×10⁻⁶ × 4×10⁴ = 12×10⁻² = 0.12; then 0.12 × 0.5 = 0.06
F = 0.06 N
Final AnswerF = 0.06 N = 6×10⁻² N
Ex 2Force When Moving Parallel to the Field
Given

A charged particle moves exactly parallel to a magnetic field line

Find: the magnetic force on it

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Identify the angle between v and B
Moving parallel to the field means θ = 0°.
Step 2 — Evaluate sinθ
sin(0°) = 0
Step 3 — Substitute into F = qvB sinθ
F = q·v·B·0 = 0
Final AnswerF = 0 N — no magnetic force acts on a charge moving parallel to the field.
Ex 3Finding the Speed from a Known Force
Given

F = 4 × 10⁻¹⁵ N

q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (an electron)

B = 0.25 T

θ = 90°

Find: speed v

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Start from F = qvB sinθ and rearrange for v
v = F / (q·B·sinθ)
Step 2 — Evaluate sinθ
sin(90°) = 1
Step 3 — Substitute the values
v = (4×10⁻¹⁵) / [(1.6×10⁻¹⁹)(0.25)(1)]
Step 4 — Work out the denominator
(1.6×10⁻¹⁹)(0.25) = 4×10⁻²⁰
Step 5 — Divide
v = (4×10⁻¹⁵) / (4×10⁻²⁰) = 1×10⁵ m/s
Final Answerv = 1×10⁵ m/s
Ex 4Force at an Angle Other Than 90°
Given

q = 2 × 10⁻⁶ C

v = 3 × 10⁵ m/s

B = 0.4 T

θ = 30°

Find: force F

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
F = q·v·B·sinθ
Step 2 — Evaluate sin(30°)
sin(30°) = 0.5
Step 3 — Substitute all values
F = (2×10⁻⁶)(3×10⁵)(0.4)(0.5)
Step 4 — Multiply step by step
2×10⁻⁶ × 3×10⁵ = 6×10⁻¹ = 0.6; then 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24; then 0.24 × 0.5 = 0.12

6.15

Biot-Savart Law

How to calculate the magnetic field produced by a current itself.

Watch — Biot-Savart Law Introduction
Biot-Savart Law Introduction
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

The Biot-Savart Law describes the magnetic field produced at a point in space by a small segment of current-carrying wire, and (for a long straight wire) simplifies to B = μ₀I / 2πr.

Explanation. Section 6.13 told you that a current-carrying wire produces a magnetic field circling around it, but it didn't tell you exactly how strong that field is at a given distance. The Biot-Savart Law fills that gap. For the simplest and most common case — a long, straight wire — it reduces to a clean result: the field strength is directly proportional to the current flowing (more current, stronger field) and inversely proportional to the distance from the wire (further away, weaker field, though this time as a simple 1/r relationship, not an inverse-square like Coulomb's Law). The constant μ₀, the permeability of free space, plays a role here similar to the role k plays in Coulomb's Law — it's simply a fixed number that makes the units and magnitudes work out correctly for our universe.

The direction of this field is found using the right-hand rule: point your right thumb along the direction of conventional current flow, and your curled fingers show the direction the magnetic field circles around the wire, forming closed loops with no beginning or end — unlike electric field lines, which always start and stop on charges.

Magnetic Field of a Long Straight Wire
B = μ₀·I / (2πr)
B = magnetic field strength (T)  |  μ₀ = permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A  |  I = current (A)  |  r = distance from the wire (m)
Applications
  • Designing electromagnets and inductors, where predicting field strength precisely matters.
  • Calculating the fields inside solenoids and coils used in speakers, motors, and transformers.
  • Understanding electromagnetic interference between current-carrying cables.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Field at a Given Distance from a Wire
Given

I = 5 A

r = 0.1 m

μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A

Find: magnetic field B

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
B = μ₀I / (2πr)
Step 2 — Substitute the values
B = (4π×10⁻⁷)(5) / (2π × 0.1)
Step 3 — Cancel π from top and bottom
B = (4×10⁻⁷ × 5) / (2 × 0.1)
Step 4 — Simplify the numerator and denominator
Numerator = 20×10⁻⁷;   Denominator = 0.2
Step 5 — Divide
B = 20×10⁻⁷ / 0.2 = 1×10⁻⁵ T
Final AnswerB = 1×10⁻⁵ T (10 µT)
Ex 2Finding the Current from the Field
Given

B = 4 × 10⁻⁵ T

r = 0.05 m

Find: current I

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Rearrange B = μ₀I/(2πr) for I
I = B·2πr / μ₀
Step 2 — Substitute the values
I = (4×10⁻⁵)(2π × 0.05) / (4π×10⁻⁷)
Step 3 — Cancel π and simplify
I = (4×10⁻⁵ × 2 × 0.05) / (4×10⁻⁷)
Step 4 — Work out the numerator
4×10⁻⁵ × 0.1 = 4×10⁻⁶
Step 5 — Divide by the denominator
I = 4×10⁻⁶ / 4×10⁻⁷ = 10 A
Final AnswerI = 10 A
Ex 3Effect of Doubling the Distance
Given

Original field B = 6×10⁻⁶ T at distance r; distance is doubled

Find: new field strength

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall the proportionality
B ∝ 1/r, so doubling r halves B (not a square relationship here, unlike Coulomb's Law).
Step 2 — Apply the scale factor
B_new = 6×10⁻⁶ × ½ = 3×10⁻⁶ T
Final AnswerB_new = 3×10⁻⁶ T
Common mistake

Treating this like Coulomb's Law and quartering the field. The Biot-Savart field from a straight wire follows 1/r, not 1/r² — only halve it.

6.16

Ampère's Law

A shortcut for finding magnetic fields in symmetric situations, without needing calculus.

Watch — Ampere's Law & Magnetic Field of a Solenoid
Ampere's Law & Solenoid
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Ampère's Law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the total current passing through that loop. For a long solenoid, it gives B = μ₀nI.

Explanation. The Biot-Savart Law is powerful but can be mathematically heavy for complicated shapes of wire. Ampère's Law offers a shortcut for situations with enough symmetry — like a long straight wire, or a long solenoid — letting you jump straight to the answer without working through the full calculus. The idea is this: imagine drawing an imaginary closed loop (called an "Amperian loop") through the region where you want to know the field. Ampère's Law says that if you add up the magnetic field along that entire loop, the total is directly proportional to however much current passes through the loop. Choose a loop that lines up neatly with the symmetry of the situation — a circle around a straight wire, or a rectangle threading through a solenoid — and the maths collapses down to a simple formula.

For a solenoid specifically, this gives the pleasingly simple result B = μ₀nI, where n is the number of turns of wire per unit length. Notice this doesn't depend on the solenoid's radius or position inside the coil at all — provided you're deep inside a long solenoid, the field is remarkably uniform, which is exactly why solenoids are used whenever a steady, predictable magnetic field is needed.

Magnetic Field Inside a Long Solenoid
B = μ₀·n·I
B = magnetic field strength (T)  |  μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A  |  n = number of turns per unit length (turns/m)  |  I = current (A)
Applications
  • Electromagnets used in cranes, MRI machines, and particle accelerators are all solenoids.
  • Relays and solenoid valves use this principle to convert electrical signals into mechanical motion.
  • Speaker coils and electric motor windings depend on predictable solenoid fields.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Field Inside a Solenoid
Given

Turns per unit length, n = 2000 turns/m

Current, I = 3 A

μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A

Find: field strength B inside the solenoid

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
B = μ₀·n·I
Step 2 — Substitute the values
B = (4π×10⁻⁷)(2000)(3)
Step 3 — Multiply the coefficients first
4 × 2000 × 3 = 24,000
B = 24,000π × 10⁻⁷
Step 4 — Multiply by π ≈ 3.1416
24,000 × 3.1416 ≈ 75,400
Step 5 — Apply the power of ten
B ≈ 75,400 × 10⁻⁷ = 7.54×10⁻³ T
Final AnswerB ≈ 7.54×10⁻³ T (7.54 mT)
Ex 2Finding the Required Current
Given

Desired field, B = 0.02 T

n = 1500 turns/m

Find: current I needed

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Rearrange B = μ₀nI for I
I = B / (μ₀n)
Step 2 — Work out the denominator
μ₀n = (4π×10⁻⁷)(1500) = 6000π×10⁻⁷ ≈ 1.885×10⁻³
Step 3 — Divide B by this value
I = 0.02 / (1.885×10⁻³) ≈ 10.6 A
Final AnswerI ≈ 10.6 A
Ex 3Effect of Doubling the Number of Turns
Given

Original field B = 5 mT at a given n and I; number of turns per unit length doubled, current unchanged

Find: new field strength

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall B ∝ n at fixed current
Since B = μ₀nI and I is unchanged, doubling n doubles B directly.
Step 2 — Apply the scale factor
B_new = 5 mT × 2 = 10 mT
Final AnswerB_new = 10 mT
6.17

Magnetic Dipoles

Why a current loop behaves just like a tiny bar magnet.

Watch — Torque on a Current Loop & Magnetic Dipole Moment
Magnetic Dipole Moment
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

A magnetic dipole is a current loop (or bar magnet) that produces a magnetic field pattern with a north-like and south-like end. It is characterised by its magnetic dipole moment, m = nIA, a vector perpendicular to the loop.

Explanation. Take a single loop of wire carrying a current, and it turns out to behave, from a distance, exactly like a tiny bar magnet — one face of the loop acts like a north pole, the opposite face like a south pole, with a magnetic field pattern emerging from one side and curving around into the other, just like the field around a permanent magnet. This is not a coincidence: bar magnets are themselves made of enormous numbers of aligned atomic-scale current loops (from orbiting and spinning electrons), so the current loop is really the fundamental building block of all magnetism.

The magnetic dipole moment, m = nIA, packages three things into a single vector: how many loops there are (n), how much current flows through them (I), and how large each loop is (A). Placed in an external magnetic field, this dipole moment interacts with the field to produce a torque, τ = mB sinθ, which twists the loop until it lines up with the field — precisely the mechanism that spins the rotor in every electric motor.

Magnetic Dipole Moment and Torque
m = n·I·A      τ = m·B·sinθ
m = dipole moment (A·m²)  |  n = number of loops  |  I = current (A)  |  A = loop area (m²)  |  τ = torque (N·m)  |  B = field strength (T)  |  θ = angle between m and B
Applications
  • Electric motors use the torque on a current loop to produce rotation.
  • Galvanometers measure current using the torque on a small coil.
  • MRI machines exploit the magnetic dipole moment of atomic nuclei.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Calculating a Dipole Moment
Given

n = 20 loops

I = 3 A

A = 0.1 m × 0.1 m = 0.01 m²

Find: dipole moment m

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
m = n·I·A
Step 2 — Substitute the values
m = 20 × 3 × 0.01
Step 3 — Multiply step by step
20 × 3 = 60; then 60 × 0.01 = 0.6
m = 0.6 A·m²
Final Answerm = 0.6 A·m²
Ex 2Maximum Torque on a Coil
Given

Dipole moment, m = 0.6 A·m²

B = 0.8 T

Find: maximum torque

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
τ = m·B·sinθ
Step 2 — Identify the angle for maximum torque
Torque is maximum when θ = 90° (coil face parallel to the field), so sinθ = 1.
Step 3 — Substitute the values
τ = 0.6 × 0.8 × 1
Step 4 — Multiply
τ = 0.48 N·m
Final Answerτ_max = 0.48 N·m
Ex 3Torque at an Angle
Given

m = 0.4 A·m²

B = 0.5 T

θ = 30°

Find: torque τ

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
τ = m·B·sinθ
Step 2 — Evaluate sin(30°)
sin(30°) = 0.5
Step 3 — Substitute all values
τ = 0.4 × 0.5 × 0.5
Step 4 — Multiply step by step
0.4 × 0.5 = 0.2; then 0.2 × 0.5 = 0.1
τ = 0.1 N·m
Final Answerτ = 0.1 N·m
6.18

Dielectrics

How insulating materials respond when placed inside an electric or magnetic field.

Watch — Dielectrics & Capacitors
Dielectrics & Capacitors
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

A dielectric is an insulating material that becomes polarised when placed in an electric field, and in doing so, weakens the net field inside it and increases the capacitance of any capacitor it fills.

Explanation. A dielectric doesn't conduct current the way a metal does — its electrons stay bound to their own atoms. But those atoms can still respond to an outside electric field, even without any free electrons moving through the material. Each individual atom or molecule stretches slightly, with its positive charge nudged one way and its negative charge nudged the other, creating countless tiny dipoles all lined up with the external field. This effect is called polarisation. Some materials, like water, already have a permanent lopsided charge distribution even without an outside field (as you saw in section 6.6); these "polar" dielectrics polarise especially easily, since their molecules just need to rotate into alignment rather than stretch from scratch.

The practical consequence is that inserting a dielectric between a capacitor's plates always increases its capacitance, because the induced polarisation in the dielectric creates its own small field pointing opposite to the original one, partially cancelling it and letting the plates hold more charge for the same applied voltage. The relative permittivity, εᵣ, is simply a number describing how strongly a given material polarises — vacuum has εᵣ = 1 by definition, and every real dielectric has εᵣ greater than 1.

Types of Dielectrics

Polar Dielectrics

Molecules have a permanent charge separation (e.g. water) — align easily with an external field.

Non-Polar Dielectrics

Molecules have no permanent charge separation — the field must induce one from scratch (e.g. most plastics).

Advantages
  • Increase capacitance without needing larger plates.
  • Allow higher voltages before electrical breakdown.
  • Provide mechanical support and spacing between plates.
Disadvantages
  • Can break down (become conductive) if the field is too strong.
  • Add weight and cost to a capacitor.
  • Some dielectrics degrade with heat or age.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Capacitance Change After Inserting a Dielectric
Given

Air-filled capacitance, C₀ = 50 µF

Dielectric constant, εᵣ = 4.0

Find: new capacitance

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall how capacitance scales with the dielectric constant
C_new = εᵣ × C₀
Step 2 — Substitute the values
C_new = 4.0 × 50 µF
Step 3 — Multiply
C_new = 200 µF
Final AnswerC_new = 200 µF
Ex 2Voltage Change at Constant Charge
Given

An air-filled 50 µF capacitor is charged to 10 V, then disconnected from the battery

A dielectric with εᵣ = 4.0 is inserted (charge stays constant since it's disconnected)

Find: new voltage

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Find the original charge
Q = C₀V₀ = (50×10⁻⁶)(10) = 5×10⁻⁴ C
Step 2 — Find the new capacitance
C_new = εᵣC₀ = 4.0 × 50 µF = 200 µF
Step 3 — Use the same (unchanged) charge with the new capacitance
V_new = Q / C_new = (5×10⁻⁴) / (200×10⁻⁶)
Step 4 — Divide
V_new = 2.5 V
Final AnswerV_new = 2.5 V — the voltage drops because the same charge is now spread across a larger capacitance
6.19

Energy Stored in the Magnetic Field

Building up a current in a coil takes work — and that work is stored in the field.

Watch — Energy Stored in an Inductor
Energy Stored in an Inductor
Tap to watch on YouTube ↗
Definition

Establishing a current in an inductor (a coil) means doing work against the induced voltage that opposes any change in current. This work is stored as magnetic potential energy in the coil's field.

Explanation. When you first switch on a coil of wire, the current doesn't jump instantly to its final value — as you'll see in Part 3, any change in current induces a voltage that opposes that very change. Pushing the current up from zero to its full value therefore takes real work, fighting against this opposition the whole way. Just as with a charged capacitor, that work doesn't disappear; it is stored as potential energy, this time in the magnetic field filling the space in and around the coil, ready to be released if the current is interrupted (which is exactly why switching off a large inductor abruptly can produce a spark, as the stored energy forces the current to keep flowing somewhere).

Energy Stored in an Inductor
U = ½·L·I²
U = energy stored (J)  |  L = inductance (H)  |  I = current (A)
Applications
  • Inductors in power supplies smooth out current fluctuations, storing and releasing energy each cycle.
  • Ignition coils in petrol engines release stored magnetic energy as a high-voltage spark.
  • Wireless charging pads transfer energy via magnetic fields between coils.

Worked Examples

Ex 1Energy Stored in an Inductor
Given

L = 0.2 H

I = 3 A

Find: energy stored U

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Write the formula
U = ½LI²
Step 2 — Square the current first
I² = 3² = 9
Step 3 — Multiply by L
0.2 × 9 = 1.8
Step 4 — Multiply by ½
U = 0.5 × 1.8 = 0.9 J
Final AnswerU = 0.9 J
Ex 2Finding the Inductance from Stored Energy
Given

U = 4 J

I = 5 A

Find: inductance L

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Start from U = ½LI² and rearrange
L = 2U / I²
Step 2 — Square the current
I² = 5² = 25
Step 3 — Substitute and multiply the numerator
L = (2 × 4) / 25 = 8 / 25
Step 4 — Divide
L = 0.32 H
Final AnswerL = 0.32 H
Ex 3Effect of Doubling the Current
Given

Original energy stored, U = 2 J at current I; current is doubled, L unchanged

Find: new energy stored

Full Step-by-Step Solution
Step 1 — Recall U ∝ I²
Since U = ½LI² and L is fixed, energy scales with the square of the current.
Step 2 — Apply the scale factor for doubling I
Scale factor = 2² = 4
Step 3 — Multiply the original energy
U_new = 2 J × 4 = 8 J
Final AnswerU_new = 8 J

Chapter 6, Part 2 Summary — The Magnetic Field

  • Magnetic field: region around a magnet or moving charge where a magnetic force can be felt; produced by permanent magnets and by currents.
  • Lorentz force: F = qvB sinθ, always perpendicular to velocity and field.
  • Biot-Savart Law: B = μ₀I/2πr for a long straight wire; field follows 1/r, not 1/r².
  • Ampère's Law: B = μ₀nI inside a long solenoid.
  • Magnetic dipoles: m = nIA; torque τ = mB sinθ, the basis of electric motors.
  • Dielectrics: insulators that polarise in a field, increasing capacitance; polar vs non-polar types.
  • Energy in the magnetic field: U = ½LI², stored in an inductor's field.
