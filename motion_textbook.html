A complete study of motion — from fundamental definitions through Newton's Laws, equations of motion with full worked calculations, and all textbook content with diagrams and exercises.

A

What is Motion?

Before we can study the equations and laws that govern motion, we must clearly understand what motion is and the language we use to describe it. Every calculation in physics is built on these definitions.

Definition

Motion is the change in position of a body with respect to time, measured from a fixed reference point. An object is said to be in motion if its position changes over time relative to a stationary observer or reference point.

Motion exists in all states of matter — solids, liquids, and gases. The molecules in a gas are constantly moving in random directions. The planets orbit the Sun. Blood flows through your veins. All of this is motion.

📌

Reference Point: Motion is always described relative to something. A person sitting in a moving bus is at rest relative to the bus, but is in motion relative to the road and buildings outside. This is why we always need a reference point.

Many scientists have studied motion. The Italian scientist Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) was the first to carry out systematic experiments on motion. Sir Isaac Newton later did more detailed work and published his famous three laws of motion in 1687.

B

Key Quantities in Motion

To describe motion mathematically, we use five main quantities. Understanding each one clearly — and the difference between similar ones — is essential.

Distance and Displacement

Distance

Distance is the total length of the path covered by a moving body. It does not consider direction — only the total ground covered. It is a scalar quantity (has magnitude only).

Displacement

Displacement is the straight-line distance from the starting point to the final position, measured in a specific direction. It is a vector quantity (has both magnitude and direction).

Example: A student walks 6 m North, then 8 m East.

  • Distance = 6 + 8 = 14 m (total path length — just add the numbers)
  • Displacement = √(6² + 8²) = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10 m in the North-East direction
Fig. A.1: Distance (red + blue path = 14 m) vs. Displacement (green diagonal = 10 m)

Speed and Velocity

Speed

Speed is the distance covered per unit time. It is a scalar quantity — it only tells you how fast, not in what direction.

Velocity

Velocity is the displacement per unit time. It is a vector quantity — it tells you how fast AND in which direction.

Speed & Velocity Formulas
Speed = Distance ÷ Time      v = d / t
Velocity = Displacement ÷ Time      v = s / t
Unit: metres per second (m/s) or kilometres per hour (km/h)

A car travelling in a circle at constant speed of 60 km/h has a changing velocity — because its direction keeps changing, even though its speed is constant. This is an important distinction.

Acceleration

Definition

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. Whenever an object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction — it is accelerating.

Acceleration Formula
a = (v − u) / t
a = acceleration (m/s²)  |  v = final velocity (m/s)  |  u = initial velocity (m/s)  |  t = time (s)
  • If a is positive: the object is speeding up (accelerating)
  • If a is negative: the object is slowing down (decelerating or retardation)
  • If a = 0: the object moves at constant velocity

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

Sir Isaac Newton published his three laws of motion in his work Principia Mathematica in 1687. These laws are the foundation of classical mechanics and explain why and how objects move. Everything in this chapter connects back to these laws.

Newton's First Law

The Law of Inertia

An object will remain at rest or continue to move in a straight line at constant velocity unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force.

What this means: Objects do not change their state of motion on their own. A ball sitting still stays still. A ball rolling on a frictionless surface keeps rolling forever. Only a force can change this.

Inertia is the property of a body that resists changes to its state of motion. The greater the mass, the greater the inertia. A fully loaded truck has much more inertia than a bicycle — it is much harder to start moving and much harder to stop.

  • When a bus suddenly brakes, passengers lurch forward — their bodies want to keep moving (inertia).
  • A book on a table stays at rest unless you push it.
  • A ball thrown in space (no gravity, no air) would travel in a straight line forever.
Newton's Second Law

The Law of Force and Acceleration

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force applied to it and inversely proportional to its mass. The direction of acceleration is the same as the direction of the net force.
The Second Law Formula
F = m × a
F = net force (Newtons, N)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  a = acceleration (m/s²)
Rearranged:   a = F / m    or    m = F / a

One Newton (1 N) is the force required to give a mass of 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m/s². So 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

  • Kicking a football harder (more force) gives it more acceleration.
  • A heavier object (more mass) accelerates less for the same kick.
  • A rocket engine provides force; the rocket (mass) accelerates accordingly.
Newton's Third Law

The Law of Action and Reaction

For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. These forces act on different objects and never cancel each other out.

Critical point: The action and reaction forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, but they act on different bodies. This is why they do not cancel each other.

  • A rocket pushes exhaust gas downward (action); the gas pushes the rocket upward (reaction).
  • When you walk, your foot pushes backward on the ground (action); the ground pushes your foot forward (reaction).
  • A gun fires a bullet forward (action); the gun recoils backward (reaction).
  • You push down on a chair; the chair pushes up on you with equal force.

The Three Equations of Motion

The equations of motion are mathematical relationships that link the five key quantities of motion when an object moves with uniform (constant) acceleration in a straight line. These equations were derived from the definitions of acceleration and velocity.

uInitial velocity (m/s)
vFinal velocity (m/s)
aAcceleration (m/s²)
tTime (seconds)
sDisplacement (m)
⚠️

Important: These equations only apply when acceleration is constant (uniform). If acceleration changes, you cannot use them. Also, take care with signs — if deceleration is involved, use a negative value for a.

Equation 1 — The Velocity–Time Equation

First Equation of Motion
v = u + at
Derived from the definition of acceleration: a = (v − u) / t
Multiply both sides by t: at = v − u   →   Rearrange: v = u + at

Use when: You know u, a, and t and you want to find v (or rearrange to find any other variable). The missing variable is s.

This equation simply says: final velocity = starting velocity + (how much velocity was gained per second × how many seconds passed).

Equation 2 — The Displacement–Time Equation

Second Equation of Motion
s = ut + ½at²
Derived from: average velocity = (u + v)/2. Since v = u + at, substituting gives average velocity = u + ½at.
Then s = average velocity × t = (u + ½at) × t = ut + ½at²

Use when: You know u, a, and t and want to find s. The missing variable is v.

This equation gives the displacement (how far) based on how long the object has been moving and how quickly it is accelerating. Note that distance grows with the square of time when acceleration is involved.

Equation 3 — The Velocity–Displacement Equation

Third Equation of Motion
v² = u² + 2as
Derived by combining equations 1 and 2 to eliminate t.
From eq 1: t = (v − u)/a. Substituting into eq 2 and simplifying gives v² = u² + 2as

Use when: Time (t) is NOT given and NOT required. The missing variable is t.

This equation is extremely useful in problems where you are not given time but you know the distance and need the velocity, or vice versa. Braking distance problems almost always use this equation.

EquationFormMissing VariableBest used when...
1stv = u + ats (displacement)Time is given, need final velocity
2nds = ut + ½at²v (final velocity)Time is given, need distance
3rdv² = u² + 2ast (time)No time given or needed

Worked Examples — Easy Level

In these problems we apply one equation at a time with straightforward numbers. Always write out what you are given, what you need to find, choose your equation, substitute, and calculate.

Example E1 Finding Final Velocity
A motorcycle starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 5 m/s² for 8 seconds. Find its final velocity.
Given
u = 0 m/s a = 5 m/s² t = 8 s Find: v = ?
Solution
1
We have u, a, t and need v. The missing variable is s, so we use the first equation of motion. v = u + at
2
Substitute the known values — replace each letter with its number. v = 0 + (5 × 8)
3
Perform the multiplication first (BODMAS), then add. v = 0 + 40 = 40 m/s
Final Answerv = 40 m/s
Example E2 Finding Distance Travelled
A ball rolls from rest with a uniform acceleration of 3 m/s². How far does it travel in 6 seconds?
Given
u = 0 m/s a = 3 m/s² t = 6 s Find: s = ?
Solution
1
We have u, a, t and need s. The missing variable is v — use the second equation. s = ut + ½at²
2
Substitute values. Note: t² means t × t = 6 × 6 = 36. s = (0)(6) + ½(3)(6²) s = 0 + ½ × 3 × 36
3
½ × 3 = 1.5, then 1.5 × 36 = 54. s = 0 + 54 = 54 m
Final Answers = 54 m
Example E3 Finding Acceleration
A train increases its velocity from 5 m/s to 25 m/s in 10 seconds. Calculate the acceleration.
Given
u = 5 m/s v = 25 m/s t = 10 s Find: a = ?
Solution
1
We have u, v, t and need a. Use the first equation rearranged. v = u + at  →  a = (v − u) / t
2
Substitute values. a = (25 − 5) / 10 a = 20 / 10 = 2 m/s²
Final Answera = 2 m/s²

Worked Examples — Complex and Very Complex

Example C1Force Against Friction on a Flat Surface
A 15 kg box is pushed along a horizontal floor with an applied force of 80 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the box and floor is μ = 0.35 and g = 10 m/s². Find: (a) the friction force, (b) the net force, (c) the acceleration, (d) velocity after 6 seconds from rest.
Given
m = 15 kg F_applied = 80 N μ = 0.35 g = 10 m/s² u = 0 m/s, t = 6 s
Solution
1
Normal Reaction (R): On a flat surface, R = weight = mg. R = mg = 15 × 10 = 150 N
2
Friction Force (Ff): Use Ff = μR. Ff = μR = 0.35 × 150 = 52.5 N
3
Net Force: Friction opposes motion, so subtract it from applied force. F_net = F_applied − Ff = 80 − 52.5 = 27.5 N
4
Acceleration: Apply Newton's Second Law. a = F_net / m = 27.5 / 15 = 1.83 m/s²
5
Velocity after 6 s: Use equation 1. v = u + at = 0 + 1.83 × 6 = 11 m/s
AnswersFf = 52.5 N  |  F_net = 27.5 N  |  a = 1.83 m/s²  |  v = 11 m/s
Example C2 — Very ComplexBlock on Inclined Plane with Friction
A 8 kg block slides down a plane inclined at 30° to the horizontal. The coefficient of kinetic friction is μ = 0.25 and g = 10 m/s². Find: (a) the normal reaction, (b) the friction force, (c) the net force down the slope, (d) the acceleration, (e) the velocity after the block has slid 12 m from rest.
Given
m = 8 kg θ = 30° μ = 0.25 g = 10 m/s² sin30° = 0.5 cos30° = 0.866 s = 12 m, u = 0
Fig. H.1: Forces acting on a block on an inclined plane — weight (W), normal reaction (R), friction (Ff up slope), and component of weight along slope (mg sinθ down).
Solution
1
Normal Reaction: On an incline, R equals the component of weight perpendicular to the slope. R = mg cosθ = 8 × 10 × cos30° = 80 × 0.866 = 69.3 N
2
Friction Force: Acts up the slope, opposing the downward slide. Ff = μR = 0.25 × 69.3 = 17.3 N
3
Driving Force (down slope): This is the component of weight along the slope. F_down = mg sinθ = 8 × 10 × sin30° = 80 × 0.5 = 40 N
4
Net Force: Friction opposes the downward motion, so subtract it. F_net = F_down − Ff = 40 − 17.3 = 22.7 N
5
Acceleration: a = F_net / m = 22.7 / 8 = 2.84 m/s²
6
Velocity after 12 m from rest: Use equation 3 (no time given). v² = u² + 2as = 0 + 2 × 2.84 × 12 = 68.16 v = √68.16 ≈ 8.26 m/s
AnswersR = 69.3 N  |  Ff = 17.3 N  |  F_net = 22.7 N  |  a = 2.84 m/s²  |  v ≈ 8.26 m/s

5.2 Introduction

Motion is typically described in terms of velocity, acceleration, displacement and time. An object's velocity cannot change unless it is acted upon by a force, as described by Newton's first law, also known as inertia. More generally, the term 'motion' signifies any spatial and/or temporal change in a physical system.

Many scientists have studied motion and its properties because of its importance to life. The Italian scientist, Galileo Galilei (1564–1642), did the first systematic study of motion. Also, Sir Isaac Newton did more detailed work on the study of motion.

Motion involves change of position of a body with time. It also involves how things move and what makes them move. It exists in different forms and occurs in solids, liquids and gases.

5.3

Types of Motion

(1) Random Motion

Definition

Random motion is the type of motion in which a body moves in a non-linear manner and changes direction continuously. There is no fixed or predictable path.

Examples of random motion:

  • Molecular movement in gases — gas molecules move in all directions, colliding with each other and with the walls of their container.
  • Dashing of objects in air, e.g. insects or a feather thrown into the air.
  • Brownian motion — the random movement of tiny pollen particles suspended in water.
Fig. 5.1: Random motion — the path of an insect (e.g. a butterfly) changing direction continuously.

(2) Translational Rectilinear Motion

Definition

Translational rectilinear motion occurs when an object moves in a fixed straight-line direction. When a body moves from one point (A) to another point (B), it is said to be translated from A to B.

Examples of translational rectilinear motion:

  • The movement of a car from one point to another along a straight road.
  • Human walking movement — the movement of a person along a straight path.
  • A ball rolled along a flat, straight surface.
  • A train moving along a straight railway track.
Fig. 5.2: Translational rectilinear (linear) motion — straight-line path from A to B.

(3) Rotational Motion

Definition

Rotational motion is the movement of a body in a rotational manner about its axis. Every part of the body traces a circular arc around the axis of rotation.

Examples of rotational motion:

  • The rotation of electric fan blades about the central axis.
  • The movement of car wheels as the car drives forward.
  • The rotation of a car going around a roundabout.
  • The rotation of the Earth about its own axis (one rotation every 24 hours).
  • A spinning top rotating on a surface.
Fig. 5.3(a): Rotational motion — electric fan blades rotating on their axis.

(4) Oscillatory Motion

Definition

Oscillatory motion is a to-and-fro movement about a fixed point. It arises due to a slight displacement of an object from its initial (equilibrium) position. The object moves to a new position and back to the initial position repeatedly.

Examples of oscillatory motion:

  • Simple pendulum — swings back and forth about its lowest point.
  • Vibration of a plucked guitar string.
  • Oscillation of a load tied to a vertical spring — bounces up and down.
  • Hitting or punching a punching bag — swings back and forth.
  • The vibration of a tuning fork.
Fig. 5.4: Oscillatory motion — a simple pendulum swings to and fro about its equilibrium (centre) position.

(5) Relative Motion

Definition

Relative motion is the movement of an object in relation to another body. An object may be moving relative to one observer but at rest relative to another.

A car travelling on a road is in motion with respect to the poles and the trees on the roadside. The people sitting in the car are also in motion with respect to those poles and trees. However, the people in the car are at rest with respect to one another.

Examples of relative motion:

  • Pulling on a spiral spring — the end of the spring moves relative to the fixed end.
  • Bending a ruler on a beam — one end moves relative to the supported end.
  • Pulling a string tied to a block of wood — the block moves relative to the floor.

5.4

Types of Force: Contact Force and Field Force

Causes of Motion: Force causes motion. When a force is applied on any object, it causes changes in motion. When another force is applied, the motion is changed as a result of the additional force applied in order to overcome the first force. It is a vector quantity and measured in Newton (N). 1 kg·m/s² = 1 N.

1. Contact Force

Definition

Contact force exists when there is a touch, hold, or a contact with the object in question. It requires physical contact between two bodies to exist.

Contact force is further divided into:

  • Like force (push) — a force applied in the direction of motion, e.g. pushing a box along the floor.
  • Unlike force (pull) — a force applied to draw an object towards you, e.g. pulling a cart.
  • Frictional (viscous) force — the force that opposes relative motion between surfaces in contact.

2. Field Force

Definition

Field force exists in or is confined/restricted to a given region or space — i.e. a region in space where a body experiences the effect of a force which occurs as a result of the influence of some physical agencies. It acts at a distance — no contact is needed.

Field force is further divided into:

(a) Magnetic Force

This is a force that attracts magnetic substances such as iron nails towards a magnet. It acts at a distance — you do not need to touch the iron with the magnet for it to experience the force.

Experiment — Using the effect of a magnet on nails to show field force: A bar magnet or horse shoe magnet is taken and put on a table. A sheet of paper is used to cover it. Nails are then spread on the paper. The pattern of the nails on either the bar magnet or the horse shoe magnet is seen to be in an arrangement in which the field is greatest at the pole than in the middle. This shows that magnetic field exists around a magnet — this is called the field of force.

(b) Electric Force

This is a force that acts on any two objects irrespective of their masses, attracts or pulls objects through a conductor of electricity. It keeps current (negatively charged particles) flowing through a conductor.

(c) Gravitational Force

This is a force that acts towards the centre of the Earth or any other massive body. It attracts all objects with mass towards each other. On Earth, this is the force that gives objects their weight and causes them to fall when dropped. The gravitational force of the Earth on an object is what we call its weight:

Weight Formula
W = mg
W = weight (N)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  g = gravitational acceleration = 10 m/s²

5.5

Friction and Reducing Friction

Definition

Friction is a vector quantity which exists in solids, liquids and gases. It is the frictional force that opposes relative sliding motion of two surfaces in contact. When a body slides over the surface of another, a force acts between the two surfaces to oppose the relative sliding motion of objects in contact with each other. It is a vector quantity.

Laws of Solid Friction

  1. Friction opposes motion.
  2. The limiting frictional force is independent of the surface area in contact.
  3. The ratio F/R is practically constant between any two surfaces, where F is proportional to the normal reaction R. The limiting frictional force is directly proportional to the normal reaction between the surfaces.
  4. The limiting frictional force is independent of the relative velocity between the surfaces in contact.
  5. The limiting frictional force depends on the nature of the surfaces in contact.
  6. The coefficient of sliding, dynamic or kinetic friction (μ_k) is greater than the coefficient of static or stationary friction (μ_s), i.e. μ_k > μ_s, or μ_k < μ_s.
  7. The limiting frictional force is independent of the relative velocity of the surfaces in contact.

Static (Stationary) Friction

Definition

Static friction is defined as the force that acts to prevent two surfaces in contact from sliding or a body resting on another, from sliding on a horizontal surface or an inclined plane in a straight line.

When a table (T) has a block of wood (A) on it, a string is tied to the block and passes over a pulley and carries a load. Mg is the weight of the block and R is the normal reaction. Let the block A does not move. If mass is continually added to S, there will be movement in block A.

The point at which the new added mass makes block A to move or block A to start moving is called Limiting Friction.

Static Friction Formulas
F_m = μ_s × R
On inclined plane: μ_s = tan θ
F_m = minimum force to just start motion (N)
R = normal reaction (N)  |  μ_s = coefficient of static friction
θ = angle of inclination

On flat surface: R = mg  →  μ_s = F_m / mg
On incline: R = mg cosθ  →  F_m = mg sinθ  →  μ_s = tanθ
Worked Example 5.1Static Friction on Inclined Plane
From the diagram, find: (i) the force parallel to the plane to slide it downwards, (ii) the normal reaction between the plane to slide the surfaces, (iii) the coefficient of friction between the box and the plane. (A 5 kg object on a 30° incline, g = 10 m/s²)
Given
m = 5 kg θ = 30° g = 10 m/s² sin30° = 0.5 cos30° = 0.866
Solution
i
Parallel force (force trying to slide block down the slope): F_m = mg sinθ = 5 × 10 × sin30° = 50 × 0.5 = 25 N
ii
Normal reaction (perpendicular to slope): R = mg cosθ = 5 × 10 × cos30° = 50 × 0.866 = 43.3 N
iii
Coefficient of static friction: μ_s = F_m / R = 25 / 43.3 = 0.5774 Check: μ_s = tan 30° = tan 30° = 0.5774 ✓
AnswersF_m = 25 N  |  R = 43.3 N  |  μ_s = 0.5774

Sliding, Dynamic or Kinetic Friction

Definition

Sliding (kinetic) friction is defined as the frictional force, R, that exists between two surfaces in relative motion.

Kinetic Friction
μ_k = F / R = F_m / mg
Where F is the kinetic friction force and R is the normal reaction between the surfaces in relative motion.
Also: F_k = ½ m·v² (work-energy approach) where v is the speed.
Worked Example 5.2Kinetic Friction — Box Sliding to Rest
A box of mass 4.5 kg is given an initial horizontal speed of 5 ms⁻¹. It slides along a horizontal floor to a distance of 3 m before coming to rest. What is the coefficient of kinetic friction between the box and the horizontal floor? [g = 10 m/s²]
Given
m = 4.5 kg u = 5 m/s v = 0 m/s (stops) S = 3 m g = 10 m/s²
Solution
1
Find the kinetic energy lost (work done by friction equals kinetic energy lost): F_k × S = ½mv² (initial KE) − ½mv² (final KE) = ½mv²_initial F_k × 3 = ½ × 4.5 × 5² = ½ × 4.5 × 25 = 56.25 J F_k = 56.25 / 3 = 18.75 N
2
Normal reaction: R = mg = 4.5 × 10 = 45 N
3
Coefficient of kinetic friction: μ_k = F_k / R = 18.75 / 45 = 0.4167 ≈ 0.42
Final Answerμ_k ≈ 0.42

Application (Advantages) of Friction

  1. The tyres of a motor car and a bicycle are made rough to increase friction.
  2. It is the friction between the belt and the pulley that helps in the rotation of various parts of a machine.
  3. When the ground becomes slippery after rain, it is made rough by spreading sand in order not to cause slipping.
  4. Without friction between the feet and ground, it would not be possible to walk.
  5. Without friction, the brake of a motor car cannot work.

Disadvantages of Friction

  1. It reduces the efficiency of machines.
  2. It reduces motion.
  3. It causes the heating of engines.
  4. It causes wear and tear in machines.

Methods of Reducing Friction

  1. Streamlining: Streamlining is used to reduce friction due to the shape of an object which ensures that only a small contact surface area of the object is in head-on contact with the fluid, such as air, water.
  2. Lubrication: Lubricants are viscous liquid or oil which are applied between two surfaces in contact. They help in overcoming or reducing friction. Examples: lubricating of wheels, sprocket by the use of ball bearing.
  3. Polishing: Friction between two surfaces in contact can be reduced by polishing them, for example polishing the jewel bearing in watches by minimising the interlocking and projections.
  4. Ball bearing: Shafts are fixed on ball bearings so that friction will be less than sliding friction, because rolling friction is reduced. Ball bearings are provided in: the shafts of motors, the axle of motor car, the tyre or wheel of a cycle, dynamos.
  5. Use of pencils and polystyrene: Banking — this is the method used in reducing sideways friction.
  6. Banking: This is the method used in reducing sideways friction.

Types of Solid Friction

Static (Stationary) Friction

The friction force that prevents an object from starting to move. It is the maximum friction force that must be overcome to initiate motion.

Kinetic (Dynamic/Sliding) Friction

The friction force that acts while an object is already sliding. It is always less than static friction: μ_kinetic < μ_static.

Circular Motion

Circular motion is a motion in a circular path in which the speed of the body remains constant, but which its direction continuously changes, e.g. the direction of the velocity of the body in circular motion.

💡

Key concept: Even at constant speed, a body in circular motion is always accelerating — because its direction (and therefore its velocity vector) keeps changing. This acceleration points toward the centre of the circle and is called centripetal acceleration.

Experiment: Stone attached to the end of a rope and whirled in a horizontal circle

When a stone is tied to the end of a rope and whirled round at constant (same) speed but at different directions, the stone moves in a circular path. The direction is constantly changing as the stone moves round the circle. This implies that velocity changes all the time as it moves round the circle.

Fig. 5.5: Circular motion — the stone moves at constant speed but its velocity direction is always tangent to the circle. The centripetal force points inward toward the centre.

Angular Velocity (ω)

Definition

Angular velocity (ω) is defined as the change of the angle per second, measured in rads⁻¹ or rad/s. It is the rate at which the radius sweeps out angle.

Angular Velocity Formulas
ω = θ / t      (basic definition)
v = r·ω      (linear speed from angular velocity)
T = 2π / ω      (period — time for one full revolution)
ω = angular velocity (rad/s)  |  θ = angle swept (radians)  |  t = time (s)
v = linear speed (m/s)  |  r = radius (m)  |  T = period (s)
One full revolution = 2π radians = 360°

Derivation of v = rω: In moving through an arc of length S in time t, the angle moved is θ. The arc length S = rθ. Since ω = θ/t, we get θ = ωt. Therefore S = rωt. Dividing both sides by t gives v = S/t = rω.

Dividing throughout with t, the period T of the motion is given as T = 2π/ω.

Centripetal Force

Definition

Centripetal force is the force required to keep an object moving in circular motion. It always acts toward the centre of the circle. The tension in the string that produces a force towards the centre of the circle due to the directions and acceleration (a) is called centripetal force.

Centripetal Force Formulas
F = mv² / r      or      F = mrω²
F = centripetal force (N)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  v = linear speed (m/s)
r = radius (m)  |  ω = angular velocity (rad/s)

Derived from: a = v²/r (centripetal acceleration) → F = ma = mv²/r
Since v = rω: F = m(rω)²/r = mrω²
Worked Example 5.3Circular Motion — Mass Moving in a Circle
An object of mass 4 kg moves in a circle at uniform speed of 32 m/s. The radius is 8 m. Calculate: (a) the angular velocity, (b) the centripetal force.
Given
m = 4 kg v = 32 m/s r = 8 m
Part (a) — Angular Velocity
1
Use v = rω, rearranged to give ω = v/r. ω = v / r = 32 / 8 = 4 rad/s
Part (b) — Centripetal Force
2
Use F = mrω² with ω = 4 rad/s. F = mrω² = 4 × 8 × (4)² = 4 × 8 × 16 = 512 N
Answersω = 4 rad/s  |  F = 512 N
Worked Example 5.4Circular Motion — Finding Angular Velocity and Period
An object moves in a circle of radius 5 m at a speed of 20 m/s. Calculate: (a) the angular velocity, (b) the period, (c) the centripetal force if the mass is 3 kg.
Given
r = 5 m v = 20 m/s m = 3 kg
Solution
a
Angular velocity: ω = v / r = 20 / 5 = 4 rad/s
b
Period (time for one full revolution): T = 2π / ω = 2 × 3.142 / 4 = 6.284 / 4 = 1.571 s
c
Centripetal force: F = mv² / r = 3 × 20² / 5 = 3 × 400 / 5 = 1200 / 5 = 240 N
Answersω = 4 rad/s  |  T = 1.571 s  |  F = 240 N

📋 Chapter 5 Summary — Motion

  • Motion is the change in position of a body with respect to time and a reference point.
  • 5 types of motion: Random, Translational (rectilinear), Rotational, Oscillatory, Relative.
  • Newton's 1st Law: No net force = no change in motion (inertia).
  • Newton's 2nd Law: F = ma — net force equals mass × acceleration.
  • Newton's 3rd Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction on a different body.
  • 3 Equations of Motion: v = u + at  |  s = ut + ½at²  |  v² = u² + 2as
  • Forces: Contact force (push, pull, friction) and Field force (gravity, magnetic, electric).
  • Friction: Ff = μR. Opposes motion. Static friction > Kinetic friction.
  • Circular motion: F = mv²/r = mrω²  |  ω = v/r  |  v = rω  |  T = 2π/ω
