Chapter One
Concept and Rationale for History of Education
Pages 1–7 · Understanding what education means and why we study its history

Education has no single definition; the term has been defined and described in various ways by authors, philosophers, psychologists, sociologists, and educationists. Education therefore means different things to different people depending on environment and time.

It is perceived to be a universal phenomenon that people use to determine their level of development since time immemorial. It is equally described as an instrument for national development, unity and stability.

Scholar Quote — Fafunwa (1974)

"A people with no knowledge of their past would suffer from collective amnesia, groping blindly into the future without guide-posts of precedence to shape their course."

Adesina (1985) defines education as a tool for the integration of the individual effectively into society, so that the individual can achieve self-realization, develop national consciousness, promote unity and strive for social, economic, scientific, cultural and technical progress.

Nduka (1982), in his book Western Education and the Nigerian Cultural Background, relates education to culture, defining it as the process of cultural transmission — embracing people's art, music, literature, philosophy, religion, commerce, political organization, science and technology. Education thus, is holistic.

Osokoya (1987) defines education as a continuous process which the society establishes to assist its members into the future. Education is the leading out of the in-born powers and potentialities of individuals and the acquisition of skills, aptitudes, and competencies necessary for self-realization.

Summary Definition

Education can be described as an attempt made by each society to assist its members to become functional and to maintain or promote the inherited culture of that society.

Forms / Kinds of Education

Educationists have classified education into three broad areas:

a. Non-Formal Education

An organized, systematic and coherent educational activity but outside the established school system. This type of education is received in an open system — it deals with apprenticeship and involves the acquisition of skills. It is flexible in terms of admission, curriculum, place, method, and duration.

b. Informal Education

Education that is not institutionalized. Usually referred to as traditional education. Informal education does not require the construction of any syllabus, scheme of work or lesson note. It is given through the apprenticeship system but also includes the influences of elders, parents and siblings. No formal teachers or classroom are required. No certificate is needed.

c. Formal Education

The formalized type of education which was brought to Nigeria by missionaries towards the middle of the nineteenth century. It is systematic, organized and sequentially structured, arranged in stages and received within the four walls of the educational institution. There is time limit, age limit, and a fixed curriculum. Examples include Islamic Education and Western Education.

Rationale for History of Education

The rationale for studying the history of education in Nigeria is to understand how education was in Nigeria and other nations of the world — e.g. ancient Greece and Romans. This enables a comparative study of these nations.

  • To develop an adequate national developmental plan, studying previous educational systems becomes relevant.
  • It facilitates the review of former or inadequate policies and leads to the formulation of more fine-tuned ones.
  • It enables one to know how much involvement the government, community, and NGOs have had in the funding of education.
  • It enables one to see and analyse the enrolment/population of school-age children.
  • From history, we know that Nigeria's education system and curriculum were both derived from the British system since Nigeria was a British colony up to 1960.
  • It was shortly before independence that people began to question the relevance of the existing curriculum to a Nigerian environment — this culminated in the National Curriculum Conference of 1969.

Chapter Two
Educational Practice in Ancient Greek and Roman Empire
Pages 8–13 · How the ancient world educated its people
Education in Ancient Greek Empire

Every society, whether simple or complex, has its own way of educating and training its youth. Greece and Rome are both European nations. Ancient Greece is located at the southeastern part of Europe.

History records that centuries before Christ, there arose an enlightened civilization noted for its achievements in art, architecture, literature, philosophy, science and government — thus referring to Greece as the "Birth place of Western Civilization."

The invention of writing by the Greeks was one of their great achievements. Earlier writings used only signs of consonants and moved from right to left. It was the Greeks that added signs to the vowels and changed the direction of writing to go from left to right.

Key Fact

At about 507 B.C., Athens became the leading city of Greece in physical, political, intellectual and artistic development. The Olympic Games originated from this part of the world.

Greece is also credited for the production of classical philosophers such as Plato, Socrates and Aristotle (Shaaba, 2003). These philosophers aimed at raising probable questions and seeking answers to help humanity live a good life.

Sparta and Athens were the two important cities in Greece. The Greek idea of an educated man was one who was mentally and physically well balanced. Citizens were taught subjects such as art, drama and poetry.

Sparta People (Spartans)

The Spartans were warriors and conquest was their major occupation. There were three groups of people in the Spartan city:

  1. The Invaders (Masters) — People with all civil rights and responsibilities, dominating others in the city.
  2. The Perioeci (Dwellers Abound) — Freemen living in surrounding towns and mountain villages. They did not take part in government and were prohibited from marrying into the master class.
  3. The Helots (Slaves) — Captives of war who were reduced to slavery. They were subjected to martial law, monitored by secret police, and could be killed at any time without trial.

In Sparta, the child belonged to the state. When a child was born, it was presented to ephors (government administrators), who judged its fitness to live. Those judged unfit were taken to a designated hillside to die.

At seven years, boys were placed in a boarding school. At eighteen, they were ready for strenuous military training for twelve years. At thirty, the Spartan was compelled to marry. At forty-five, he was relieved of strenuous campaigning. At sixty, he became an elder statesman.

Athens People (Athenians)

Athenians were very few in number, which made it easy to gather at the Athenian Agora (people's assembly). All Athenians had the right to vote and be voted for into elective offices. The Athenians had no public organized education for girls — unlike the Spartans, education for girls was mainly moral and domestic training given by their mothers.

School started for Athenian boys at the age of seven years. The three types of school available were:

  • a. The letters school of reading and writing
  • b. The music school for lyric, poetry and hyre
  • c. The gymnastic school or palaestra

The letters and music school were in the morning while the gymnastic school was in the afternoon. The rich Athenian had a man servant called a padagogue who helped to escort the boys to and from school.

Between the ages of eighteen and twenty years, the Athenian boy was engaged in national military service. Thereafter, the boy was free to choose a career for himself. The Athenian boys usually had their eyes on politics after their military service.

Education in Ancient Roman Empire

Early Roman Education had its base in the family. Education emphasized the 3Rs at the primary school level — Reading, Writing and Arithmetic. This is synonymous to the Missionary type of education brought to Nigeria in the 19th century.

The Romans placed emphasis on oratorical and military training. In intellectual and artistic matters, Greek leadership was acknowledged. Greek slaves were used as teachers and Greek philosophy was embraced by the Roman aristocracy.

  • Primary stage: Reading, Writing and Arithmetic; patriotism and obedience taught through Roman heroes in prose and poetry.
  • Grammar school: Followed primary school; stressed oratory training.
  • School of Rhetoric: Emphasized reading of both Greek and Latin literature.

Teaching methods were by lectures, note taking, memorization and citation. Reverend fathers in the early Christian church served as teachers. In the 4th century, St. Jerome translated the Bible into Latin and also established a school for boys. By the 6th century, priests and monks had become custodians of education in Roman society.

Chapter Three
African Indigenous System of Education (A.T.E)
Pages 14–42 · African Traditional Education — its aims, methods and impact

The traditional form of education existed in Africa long before its invasion by the colonial masters. Prior to colonization, the people of Africa had a way of training and educating their young ones and youth so as to function effectively as members of their community.

In old African society, the warrior, the hunter, the nobleman, the man of character or anyone who combined the latter feature with a specific skill was adjudged to be well-educated and well-integrated citizens of his community. Functionalism was the main guiding principle.

Fafunwa (1974) on African Traditional Education

"African society regarded education as a means to an end and not an end in itself. Education was generally for an immediate induction into society and a preparation for adulthood. African education emphasized social responsibility, job orientation, political participation and spiritual and moral values."

This type of education is often referred to as the informal type of education. Babalola (1964) opined that the Ijala chant was one of the most important intellectual spoken arts developed by the Yoruba people as part of traditional education in Nigeria.

Vocational Aspects — Three Groups (Fafunwa, 1974)
  1. Agricultural Education — farming, fishing, and veterinary science (animal care and rearing).
  2. Trades and Crafts — weaving, smithing (iron, silver, gold), hunting, carving, sculpturing, painting, building, boat-making, food selling, wine selling, dyeing, etc.
  3. Professions — priesthood, medicine, justice (police messengers, judges), hunting military, chieftaincy, kingship.
Aims and Objectives of Indigenous Education in Nigeria

Fafunwa (1974 and 1982) identified seven cardinal goals of traditional African education:

  1. To develop the child's latent physical skills;
  2. To develop character;
  3. To inculcate respect for elders and those in positions of authority;
  4. To develop intellectual skills;
  5. To acquire specific vocational training and to develop a healthy attitude towards honest labour;
  6. To develop a sense of belonging and to participate actively in family and community affairs;
  7. To understand, appreciate and promote the cultural heritage of the community at large.
Explanation of the Seven Goals
1. Physical Development

The African child likes to explore his immediate environment, observe adults in their activities and imitate them by making new discoveries. In traditional African society, the child jumps, climbs trees, dances or performs balancing acts as a natural process of growth.

The "Tente" game involves counting of 1, 2, 3, clapping and stretching of the legs — it is popular across African countries and develops the child's counting ability, mental development, and physical fitness.

2. Character Development

African society as a whole lays the greatest emphasis on character training. The absence of good character is tolerable as long as good character prevails. The parents, siblings and other members of the family want the child to be honest, sociable, courageous, humble, persevering and of good report.

In traditional Nigerian society, character training is held at very high esteem because it is believed that physical education, vocational training, religious education, respect for elders, and co-operative efforts are all considered necessary for the development of "good character."

3. Respect for Elders, Peers and Constituted Authorities

The African child is trained to respect those older than himself. The African has a complicated greeting system. For instance, there are morning, afternoon and evening greetings, special greetings for parents, elders, peers, chiefs, etc.

Examples of greetings by ethnic group:

  • Yoruba: Young ones prostrate (dobale) for elders; the Oba blesses them by saying "Mo yika otun, mo yika osi"
  • Hausa: Men raise their clenched right fist to greet a superior; young ones hold the bowl of food while eating together
  • Nupe, Hausa, Fulani: Men crouch/kneel; women kneel simultaneously
4. Intellectual Training

Fafunwa (1974) defined "intellect" as the process of reasoning abstractly, power to integrate experience, and intellectualization. The main processes of learning are by observation, imitation and participation.

The African adolescent learns local geography and history of his community. Local history is taught through folklores, songs, and so on. Botany and zoology are taught through observation; actual instruction is often accompanied by demonstration.

The Yorubas are known for their use of proverbs. There are 1,200 Yoruba proverbs. Riddles and tongue-twisters represent another set of intellectual exercises. Admissions into professional groups (herbalists, hunters, chiefs, cult leaders, priests) have complex initiation training — this is equated with the modern higher education level.

5. Vocational Training (Detailed)

The aim of education in traditional society can be summed up as character training and job orientation. Agricultural education included farming, fishing, and animal husbandry. Children were taught how to test soil quality by dipping a cutlass — if it touches stone, the soil is unsuitable for yam/cassava; if porous, it is suitable for groundnuts/melons.

6. Community Participation

The traditional society cherished the membership of the child to his immediate and extended family as well as the community at large. A newly born baby immediately becomes the child of everyone in the household. Age-groups are generally engaged in communal work — helping members in clearing, planting or harvesting, and road construction.

7. Promotion of Cultural Heritage

Education, whether ancient or modern, aims at perpetuating the culture of the society. Traditional education attaches considerable importance to the promotion of cultural heritage. Culture in traditional society is not TAUGHT but CAUGHT. The child naturally acquires his culture by observing, imitating and mimicking the actions of his elders and siblings in naming ceremonies, coronation of king or chief, yam festival, funeral ceremonies, religious services, marriage ceremonies, annual dance and acrobatic displays.

Contents of Indigenous Education in Nigeria

The curriculum of indigenous education includes:

  1. Vocational training/trades and crafts — weaving, blacksmithing, basket-making, drumming, etc.
  2. Physical training — wrestling, acrobatic displays, climbing, jumping, dancing, singing, etc.
  3. Cultural training — learning about traditions, norms, values, general ways of life of the people.
  4. Moral training — learning to distinguish between acceptable and unacceptable behaviour. Greetings play major roles.
  5. Social training/community participation — training in mutual understanding, co-operation and group participation.
  6. Political training — training in societal laws; maintaining territorial integrity of the community; preparing the individual to defend the community against external attacks.
  7. Intellectual training — local geography and history, arithmetic (counting), poetic training, incantations, proverbs, etc.
  8. Agricultural training — worshipping of past heroes and heroines, ancestors, rivers, mountains, rocks, soil, iron, thunder and many other inanimate objects.
Teachers, Methods & Evaluation
The Teachers of Indigenous Education

Every adult member of the society is considered a general teacher in the indigenous educational system. However, there are some specific teachers: the mother, the father, other family members, the friends and play mates (peer group), and professionals i.e. masters of some trades and craft and priests and traditional religious leaders.

Methodology and Technique of Teaching

The methodology and techniques of indigenous education are simple but integrated. Methods include demonstration, observation, imitation, story telling, use of proverbs, superstition, learning by doing, group work and competition. Methods also include initiation, fables, rote learning and memorization.

Teaching Materials

Teaching materials are simple and inexpensive and readily available. They include loom, dye, pot, wood, mud, animal skin, hoes, cutlass, bows and arrows, dane guns, nets, fishing etc. Some inanimate objects are also used for worshipping and learning occultism.

Evaluation

Evaluation in indigenous education is done by assessing the child on the spot immediately. Something is done either by reward (praise) or punishment (rebuke).

Merits of Indigenous Education
  1. It allows the society to continue
  2. It builds an individual up physically, morally, socially, politically etc.
  3. No protocol of being specially trained and certificated before one can teach.
  4. Simple methodology of teaching — observation and imitation
  5. Simple teaching materials
  6. Inexpensive administration and management
  7. The recipient is cultured (self discipline)
Demerits of Indigenous Education
  1. No specific aims and objectives but general ones
  2. No classification either by age or compartments
  3. No certificated teachers
  4. The content/curriculum has no formal planning
  5. The method and techniques are too much integrated
  6. Evaluation technique is informal and subjective
  7. No continuous assessment, rather the recipient is assessed on the spot
  8. No written examinations
Impacts of Indigenous Education on Modern Education
  • Indigenous languages are learnt in schools
  • Home experiences or family experience allied previous knowledge are carried on to the school system and the school builds on this.
  • Some parents especially western educated ones still train their children in home lessons.
  • Traditional language of the people helps in learning the language of modern education especially in pronunciation e.g. "Table"
  • Poems were being memorized, and in modern education memorization of poems is emphasized.
Impacts of Modern Education on Indigenous Education
  • Learners now go to other societies to learn instead of their own society — this encourages initiation into other society.
  • Boarding system has replaced apprenticeship system.
  • Teachers are now certificated and not just anybody in the society; even the teachers of indigenous languages are certificated.
  • Modern education has influenced the traditional ways of life (culture) e.g. in language, dressing, eating, healthcare, delivery system etc.
  • Methodology of teaching/learning has been affected by modern education e.g. children no longer believe in taboos and superstitions used for imparting knowledge in traditional education.

Chapter Four
Islamic Education in Nigeria
Pages 43–52 · The history, spread and characteristics of Islamic Education

The word Islam is derived from the Arabic root "SALAM" which means among other things peace, purity, submission and obedience. In the religious sense, the word Islam means submission to the will of Allah and obedience to His laws. Those who follow or practice the religion are called Muslims.

Islam is one of the two major religions practised all over the world (alongside Christianity). Islamic education is closely related to Arabic language because Arabic is the language of the Holy Qu'ran which is the Muslim's reference book.

Islam's Contribution to Mathematics

Muslims were responsible for introducing the world to Arabic numerals. The Muslim mathematician, Muhammad Ibn Musa, was the first to use decimal notation and to give digits the value of position (Fafunwa, 1974:50-51).

History of Islam and Islamic Education in Nigeria

The Islamic concept of education is that of a process of guiding and directing the development of the young ones through instruction and discipline so as to enable them live a good life here on earth with good hopes of eternal blessing in heaven. It is an education for practical moral living and preparation for life hereafter.

Islam came to Hausa land in early 14th century through traders and scholars from North Africa, especially during the reign of Ali-Yaji who ruled Kano from 1349–1385. In the 17th century, KATSINA produced native scholars who developed Islamic Education. Islamic education was taught simultaneously with Arabic as a language and Islam as a religion.

The importance of knowledge in Islam cannot be overemphasized. 'ILM' in Arabic means knowledge and it is one of the key words in the Holy Qur'an. The Prophet (SAW) had many of his traditions (hadith) emphasizing the importance of knowledge. Teaching Islam to others was considered a duty for which one should expect no reward — this is why many Islamic schools charge little or no fees.

The Spread of Islam and Islamic Education in Nigeria

Islam reached the Savannah region of West Africa and northern Nigeria in the 8th century A.D. This date marked the beginning of written history of West Africa. Islam was first spread in Kanem Borno where its first ruler Umme Jilmi (1085–1097) was the first to accept the new faith at the hand of an eminent Muslim scholar Hamed Muhammad Mani.

Islam did not become a state religion until the reign of Mai Idris Alooma (1570–1602). The spread of Islam also led to the introduction of Islamic Education — without Islamic education, one cannot understand and practice Islam. Therefore, Islamic religion and Islamic education are partners in progress.

Islam and Islamic education were brought to Hausa land in the early 14th century by traders and scholars. This led to the emergence of two types of schools:

  • The Qur'anic Schools (also known as "Makaranta Allo" or tablet school) — for beginners.
  • The Ilmi School — which embraces all aspects of Islamic studies.

By 1830, Islam reached Yoruba land from the North through Ilorin. Scholars such as Ahmad Qifu and Uthman bin Abubakar came to Ibadan. Ilorin became a centre of Islamic learning with about two hundred Qur'anic schools (Madrasahs). As a result, Qur'anic schools were found in almost every town and small village where there were only a few Muslim families.

Remuneration of the Arabic Teacher

The teachers in the traditional Arabic schools depended on charity (Sadaqah) for living; for the teacher is one of those persons to whom 'Sadaqah' is prescribed "Fi Sabil Allah" (in the way of Allah). This noble principle reduced the status of a teacher (Mu'allim) to that of a mere beggar — he had to wander from place to place looking for charitable Muslims.

His pupils (Al-Muhajir or emigrant) went door to door asking for charity. These pupils called Al-muhajir-un (emigrants) are believed to have left their homes in search of knowledge (Ilmi). The higher grade of teachers, the ULAMAS, are those deeply learned in the science of the Qur'an and Hadith, Islamic theology and etymology.

Islamic Education in Southern Nigeria

The southern part of Nigeria, particularly Yoruba land, was known to Muslims long before Islam actually reached there. By the 17th century, this part of the country was known to Muslims (Fafunwa, 1974). Uthman bin Abubakar, a learned scholar from Katsina, was made Imam of the Ibadan Muslims in 1839.

Characteristics of Qur'anic School
  1. Usually the elementary school built near the Mosque or in the Mallam's hallway, parlour, verandah, under the tree or any convenient place. The Mallams served as teachers.
  2. Children learnt to memorize verses of the Holy Qur'an as well as the Arabic alphabet to enable them read the Qur'an successfully.
  3. Admission is not restricted by age or sex but most pupils start between ages four (4) and six (6); they are expected to learn the shorter chapters of the Holy Qur'an through repetition and rote learning.
  4. The student-teacher ratio could be very high — as many as forty pupils per Mallam, though attendance could be irregular.
  5. The subject matter is principally the Qur'an and Arabic. At this stage, no attempt is made to understand the meaning of what is read.
  6. There is no fixed fees charged, although teachers may accept gifts from parents. Mallams normally had their own vocations (farming, weaving) to support themselves.
  7. Discipline is usually very strict and various forms of corporal punishments are employed.
  8. There are three (3) sessions: morning, afternoon and evening presided over by the Mallams.
  9. Most of the Qur'anic schools are run on the discretion of their individual proprietors. There were no uniforms, timetable, class size, duration, curriculum or prescribed qualifications for teachers.
  10. Teaching method: The teacher recites the verse from his slate or 'Allo' and pupils repeat it after him — this continues until they thoroughly memorize the whole Qur'an (60 chapters) off by heart.
  11. A passing out ceremony (Wolimah) is usually organized for students who successfully complete their Qur'anic education. This marks the greatest day in the Mallam's life and the pupils. The graduate recites written chapters or verses to the admiration of the audience.
Characteristics of "Ilm" School
  1. The Ilm school is the secondary level of Islamic education where the student continues his education.
  2. It has a broader, deeper or wider curriculum compared to the Qur'anic school.
