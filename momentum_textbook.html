CH 01

Foundation Concepts

1.1 Definition of Linear Momentum

Linear momentum is a fundamental concept in physics that describes the quantity of motion an object possesses. It depends on two things: how massive the object is and how fast it is moving. A heavy, fast-moving truck has much more momentum than a slow, light bicycle.

Momentum is always a vector quantity — this means it has both a size (magnitude) and a direction. The direction of momentum is always the same as the direction of the object's velocity.

▸ Formula 1 — Linear Momentum
p = m × v
p = linear momentum (kg·m/s) m = mass of the object (kg) v = velocity of the object (m/s)
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — p = mv
EX 1 A car moving on a highway
Given

mass of car, m = 1200 kg

velocity of car, v = 25 m/s (eastward)

Find: momentum p = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write down the formula
We need to find momentum. The formula for momentum is:
p = m × v
Step 2 — Identify and write all known values
From the question we know the mass and the velocity:
m = 1200 kg, v = 25 m/s
Step 3 — Substitute values into the formula
Replace m and v with their values:
p = 1200 × 25
Step 4 — Perform the multiplication
p = 30,000 kg·m/s
Step 5 — State the direction (momentum is a vector)
Since velocity is eastward, momentum is also eastward.
p = 30,000 kg·m/s eastward
EX 2 A football being kicked
Given

mass of football, m = 0.45 kg

velocity after kick, v = 30 m/s

Find: momentum p = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
p = m × v
Step 2 — Write all known quantities
m = 0.45 kg, v = 30 m/s
Step 3 — Substitute into formula
p = 0.45 × 30
Step 4 — Calculate the result
p = 13.5 kg·m/s
p = 13.5 kg·m/s
EX 3 Finding mass from momentum and velocity
Given

momentum, p = 500 kg·m/s

velocity, v = 20 m/s

Find: mass m = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Start with the momentum formula
p = m × v
Step 2 — Rearrange to make mass (m) the subject
Divide both sides of the equation by v:
m = p ÷ v
Step 3 — Substitute known values
m = 500 ÷ 20
Step 4 — Divide to get mass
m = 25 kg
m = 25 kg
EX 4 Finding velocity from momentum and mass
Given

mass of a truck, m = 5000 kg

momentum, p = 75,000 kg·m/s

Find: velocity v = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
p = m × v
Step 2 — Rearrange to make velocity (v) the subject
Divide both sides by m:
v = p ÷ m
Step 3 — Substitute all values
v = 75,000 ÷ 5,000
Step 4 — Calculate velocity
v = 15 m/s
v = 15 m/s
EX 5 Comparing momenta of two objects
Given

Object A: mass = 2 kg, velocity = 10 m/s

Object B: mass = 5 kg, velocity = 4 m/s

Find: Which has greater momentum?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula for both objects
p = m × v
Step 2 — Calculate momentum of Object A
p_A = 2 × 10 = 20 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Calculate momentum of Object B
p_B = 5 × 4 = 20 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Compare the results
Both objects have the same momentum (20 kg·m/s), even though Object A is lighter and faster while Object B is heavier and slower.
p_A = p_B = 20 kg·m/s (Equal momenta)

Given

mass of ball, m = 0.5 kg

velocity, v = −15 m/s (moving leftward, taking right as positive)

Find: momentum p = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
p = m × v
Step 2 — Note the sign of velocity
The negative sign tells us the ball moves in the leftward (negative) direction. We keep this sign in our calculation.
Step 3 — Substitute values
p = 0.5 × (−15)
Step 4 — Calculate
p = −7.5 kg·m/s
Step 5 — Interpret the sign
The negative value means the momentum is directed to the LEFT — momentum has both magnitude (7.5) and direction (leftward).
p = −7.5 kg·m/s (7.5 kg·m/s to the left)

1.2 Units and Vector Nature of Momentum

The SI unit of momentum is the kilogram metre per second (kg·m/s). This unit can also be written as a Newton-second (N·s), because 1 N·s = 1 kg·m/s.

Because momentum is a vector, when two objects move in opposite directions, we assign one direction as positive and the other as negative. This is crucial in collision and explosion problems.

Q7 Explain why momentum is described as "relatively permanent" compared to kinetic energy during a collision.
Momentum is conserved in ALL collisions (as long as no external forces act on the system) — this is the Law of Conservation of Momentum. Kinetic energy, however, may be lost during inelastic collisions (converted to heat, sound, or deformation). So while momentum is always conserved, kinetic energy is not — making momentum more "reliable" in collision analysis.

Magnitude of momentum = 20,000 kg·m/s, directed backward.
CH 02

Newton's Laws & Momentum

2.1 Newton's Second Law in Terms of Momentum

Newton's Second Law is most generally stated not as F = ma, but as: the net force on an object equals the rate of change of momentum. This is actually the original form Newton wrote.

If the mass of an object remains constant, then changing momentum means changing velocity, which is acceleration — and this gives us the familiar F = ma.

▸ Formula 2 — Newton's Second Law (Momentum Form)
F = Δp ÷ Δt = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt
F = net force (N) Δp = change in momentum (kg·m/s) Δt = time interval (s) mv = final momentum mu = initial momentum
▸ Derivation: From F = ma to F = Δp/Δt
F = m × a Newton's 2nd Law (familiar form)
a = (v − u) ÷ Δt Definition of acceleration (change in velocity / time)
F = m × (v − u) ÷ Δt Substituting acceleration into F = ma
F = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt Expanding: m×v = final momentum, m×u = initial momentum
F = Δp ÷ Δt Since Δp = mv − mu (change in momentum), this is the final form
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — F = Δp/Δt
EX 1 Force needed to stop a moving ball
Given

mass, m = 0.5 kg

initial velocity, u = 20 m/s

final velocity, v = 0 m/s (stopped)

time taken, Δt = 0.1 s

Find: force F = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
F = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt
Step 2 — Calculate initial momentum (mu)
mu = 0.5 × 20 = 10 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Calculate final momentum (mv)
mv = 0.5 × 0 = 0 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Calculate change in momentum (Δp)
Δp = mv − mu = 0 − 10 = −10 kg·m/s
Step 5 — Substitute into force formula
F = −10 ÷ 0.1 = −100 N
Step 6 — Interpret the sign
The negative sign means the force acts OPPOSITE to the initial motion — this makes sense because the force is stopping (decelerating) the ball. The magnitude of the stopping force is 100 N.
F = 100 N (opposing direction of motion)
EX 2 Calculating force on an accelerating car
Given

mass, m = 800 kg

initial velocity, u = 10 m/s

final velocity, v = 30 m/s

time, Δt = 5 s

Find: net force F = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
F = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt
Step 2 — Calculate initial momentum
mu = 800 × 10 = 8,000 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Calculate final momentum
mv = 800 × 30 = 24,000 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Find change in momentum
Δp = 24,000 − 8,000 = 16,000 kg·m/s
Step 5 — Divide by time to get force
F = 16,000 ÷ 5 = 3,200 N
F = 3,200 N (in direction of motion)
EX 3 Time taken for a force to change momentum
Given

net force, F = 500 N

change in momentum, Δp = 2,000 kg·m/s

Find: time Δt = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula
F = Δp ÷ Δt
Step 2 — Rearrange to make Δt the subject
Multiply both sides by Δt, then divide both sides by F:
Δt = Δp ÷ F
Step 3 — Substitute values
Δt = 2,000 ÷ 500
Step 4 — Calculate the time
Δt = 4 seconds
Δt = 4 s

Given

mass of ball, m = 0.45 kg

ball velocity before catch, u = −25 m/s (toward goalkeeper)

ball velocity after catch, v = 0 m/s

time of contact, Δt = 0.2 s

2.2 Newton's Third Law and Momentum

Newton's Third Law states: "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction." In terms of momentum, this means: when two objects interact, the momentum gained by one is exactly equal in size but opposite in direction to the momentum lost by the other. This is why momentum is conserved in all interactions.

▸ Formula 3 — Newton's Third Law (Momentum Form)
F_AB = −F_BA → Δp_A = −Δp_B
F_AB = force A exerts on B F_BA = force B exerts on A Δp_A = change in momentum of A Δp_B = change in momentum of B
💡
Key Insight Newton's 3rd Law is the reason momentum is conserved! When A pushes B with force F for time Δt, then B pushes back on A with force −F for the same time Δt. The momentum changes are equal and opposite, so the total momentum of the system stays the same.

Chapter 2 — Practice Questions

Q1 State Newton's Second Law of Motion in terms of momentum (not F = ma).
Newton's Second Law states that the net force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of momentum of that object. Mathematically: F = Δp ÷ Δt = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt. This is the most general and original form of Newton's 2nd Law.
Q2 A 60 kg skater increases velocity from 2 m/s to 8 m/s in 3 seconds. Calculate the net force acting on the skater.
F = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt
Initial momentum: mu = 60 × 2 = 120 kg·m/s
Final momentum: mv = 60 × 8 = 480 kg·m/s
Δp = 480 − 120 = 360 kg·m/s
F = 360 ÷ 3 = 120 N
Q3 A force of 200 N acts on a 10 kg object for 5 seconds. By how much does the momentum change?
F = Δp ÷ Δt → Δp = F × Δt
Δp = 200 × 5 = 1,000 kg·m/s
The momentum increases by 1,000 kg·m/s
Q4 Explain using Newton's Third Law why when a gun fires a bullet, the gun recoils (kicks back).
When the gun fires, it exerts a large forward force on the bullet. By Newton's 3rd Law, the bullet exerts an equal and opposite (backward) force on the gun. This backward force causes the gun to recoil — moving backward with momentum equal in magnitude to the bullet's forward momentum. The total momentum of the system (gun + bullet) remains zero, as it was before firing.
Q5 A 1,500 kg car decelerates from 20 m/s to rest in 4 seconds. Find the braking force applied.
F = (mv − mu) ÷ Δt
mu = 1500 × 20 = 30,000 kg·m/s
mv = 1500 × 0 = 0 kg·m/s
Δp = 0 − 30,000 = −30,000 kg·m/s
F = −30,000 ÷ 4 = −7,500 N
Braking force = 7,500 N (opposing motion)
CH 03

Impulse

3.1 Definition and the Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Impulse is the product of a force and the time during which that force acts. It measures the overall "push" given to an object. A large force acting for a short time can produce the same impulse as a small force acting for a long time — and both will produce the same change in momentum.

▸ Formula 4 — Impulse
J = F × Δt
J = impulse (N·s or kg·m/s) F = net force (N) Δt = time interval (s)
▸ Formula 5 — Impulse-Momentum Theorem
J = Δp = mv − mu
J = impulse (N·s) Δp = change in momentum (kg·m/s) mv = final momentum mu = initial momentum
▸ Derivation: Impulse-Momentum Theorem
F = Δp ÷ Δt Newton's 2nd Law (rate of change of momentum)
F × Δt = Δp Multiply both sides by Δt
J = Δp Since Impulse J = F × Δt, substitute to get the theorem
F × Δt = mv − mu Expanding Δp = final momentum − initial momentum
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Impulse
EX 1 Impulse from a known force and time
Given

force applied, F = 150 N

time of application, Δt = 0.4 s

Find: impulse J = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the formula for impulse
J = F × Δt
Step 2 — Identify all given values
F = 150 N, Δt = 0.4 s
Step 3 — Substitute values into formula
J = 150 × 0.4
Step 4 — Multiply to find impulse
J = 60 N·s
Step 5 — State meaning
An impulse of 60 N·s means the object's momentum changes by 60 kg·m/s in the direction of the force.
J = 60 N·s
EX 2 Using Impulse-Momentum Theorem to find final velocity
Given

mass, m = 5 kg

initial velocity, u = 3 m/s

force, F = 20 N

time, Δt = 2 s

Find: final velocity v = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the Impulse-Momentum Theorem
F × Δt = mv − mu
Step 2 — Calculate the impulse (left side)
J = F × Δt = 20 × 2 = 40 N·s
Step 3 — Calculate initial momentum (mu)
mu = 5 × 3 = 15 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Set up the equation using the theorem
40 = (5 × v) − 15
Step 5 — Solve for final velocity v
5v = 40 + 15 = 55
Step 6 — Divide to get v
v = 55 ÷ 5 = 11 m/s
v = 11 m/s
EX 3 Airbag safety — why longer contact time reduces force
Given

A 70 kg person moving at 15 m/s stops in a crash.

Scenario A (no airbag): stop time Δt = 0.02 s

Scenario B (with airbag): stop time Δt = 0.2 s

Find: Force in each scenario

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Calculate change in momentum (same for both)
Δp = mv − mu = (70 × 0) − (70 × 15) = −1,050 kg·m/s
Step 2 — Force in Scenario A (no airbag)
F_A = Δp ÷ Δt = 1,050 ÷ 0.02 = 52,500 N
Step 3 — Force in Scenario B (with airbag)
F_B = Δp ÷ Δt = 1,050 ÷ 0.2 = 5,250 N
Step 4 — Compare the forces
The airbag reduces the force by a factor of 10 — from 52,500 N down to 5,250 N. This is why airbags save lives: same impulse (same change in momentum), but much longer time = much smaller force on the body.
Without airbag: 52,500 N | With airbag: 5,250 N
EX 4 Finding the time of contact from impulse data
Given

impulse, J = 90 N·s

average force during contact, F = 450 N

Find: time of contact Δt = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the impulse formula
J = F × Δt
Step 2 — Rearrange for Δt
Divide both sides by F:
Δt = J ÷ F
Step 3 — Substitute values
Δt = 90 ÷ 450
Step 4 — Calculate
Δt = 0.2 s

3.2 Force-Time Graphs and Impulse

When force varies over time, we cannot simply multiply F × Δt. Instead, the impulse is equal to the area under the Force-Time graph. This is a very important concept for exam questions involving graphs.

▸ Formula 6 — Impulse from Force-Time Graph
J = Area under F-t graph
Rectangle: Area = base × height = Δt × F Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × Δt × F_max Trapezoid: Area = ½ × (F₁ + F₂) × Δt
⚠
Exam Tip In a Force-Time graph, ALWAYS identify the shape under the curve first (rectangle, triangle, or trapezoid) before calculating the area. Each shape has a different area formula.

Chapter 3 — Practice Questions

Q1 Define impulse and state the Impulse-Momentum Theorem clearly.
Impulse (J) is the product of force and the time for which the force acts: J = F × Δt. The unit of impulse is N·s (Newton-second), which is the same as kg·m/s. The Impulse-Momentum Theorem states that the impulse acting on an object equals the change in momentum of that object: J = Δp = mv − mu. This means impulse causes and equals the change in momentum.
Q2 A 0.2 kg ball hits a wall at 10 m/s and bounces back at 8 m/s. The contact time is 0.05 s. Find the impulse and the average force exerted by the wall on the ball.
Taking direction toward wall as positive: u = +10 m/s, v = −8 m/s
Δp = mv − mu = (0.2 × −8) − (0.2 × 10) = −1.6 − 2 = −3.6 kg·m/s
Impulse J = Δp = −3.6 N·s (magnitude = 3.6 N·s away from wall)
F = Δp ÷ Δt = 3.6 ÷ 0.05 = 72 N (away from wall)
Q3 Why does a cricket player move their hands backward when catching a fast ball? Explain using impulse theory.
By moving the hands backward, the player increases the time (Δt) over which the ball comes to rest. Since Impulse = F × Δt = Δp, and Δp is fixed (the ball must stop), increasing Δt means the average force F on the hands must DECREASE. This reduces the pain and risk of injury. Same principle applies to airbags, crumple zones, and gym mats.
Q4 A force of 300 N acts on a 15 kg object for 6 seconds. If the object starts from rest, what is its final velocity?
Using J = mv − mu and J = F × Δt
J = 300 × 6 = 1,800 N·s
mu = 15 × 0 = 0 (starts from rest)
1,800 = 15v − 0
v = 1,800 ÷ 15 = 120 m/s
Q5 A Force-Time graph shows a triangular shape with a base of 4 s and a peak force of 600 N. Calculate the impulse.
For a triangle: Area = ½ × base × height
J = ½ × 4 × 600
J = ½ × 2400 = 1,200 N·s
Q6 A 1,200 kg car is brought from 25 m/s to rest. Calculate: (a) the change in momentum, (b) the impulse, and (c) the braking force if the car stops in 5 seconds.
(a) Δp = mv − mu = (1200×0) − (1200×25) = −30,000 kg·m/s
(b) Impulse J = Δp = −30,000 N·s (magnitude = 30,000 N·s)
(c) F = Δp ÷ Δt = 30,000 ÷ 5 = 6,000 N (braking force)
CH 04

Conservation of Linear Momentum

4.1 The Law of Conservation of Momentum

This is one of the most powerful laws in all of physics. It states that in an isolated system (where no external forces act), the total momentum before any event equals the total momentum after that event. Collisions, explosions, and separations all obey this law.

An isolated system is one where no net external force acts. Gravity and normal force often cancel each other, making horizontal motion effectively isolated.

▸ Formula 7 — Conservation of Momentum
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
m₁, m₂ = masses of objects 1 and 2 u₁, u₂ = initial velocities v₁, v₂ = final velocities Left side = total momentum before Right side = total momentum after
📌
Conditions for Conservation Conservation of momentum applies ONLY when: (1) The system is closed (no mass enters or leaves), and (2) No net external force acts on the system. Internal forces between the objects do NOT affect conservation — they cancel out by Newton's 3rd Law.

4.2 Applying Conservation of Momentum

▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Conservation of Momentum
EX 1 Two trolleys colliding and sticking together
Given

Trolley 1: m₁ = 4 kg, u₁ = 6 m/s (rightward)

Trolley 2: m₂ = 2 kg, u₂ = 0 m/s (at rest)

After collision: they stick together

Find: common velocity v after collision

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the conservation of momentum formula
Since they stick together, they have a COMMON final velocity v:
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂) × v
Step 2 — Calculate total initial momentum (left side)
(4 × 6) + (2 × 0) = 24 + 0 = 24 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Set up equation for right side
(4 + 2) × v = 6v
Step 4 — Write the complete equation
24 = 6v
Step 5 — Solve for v
v = 24 ÷ 6 = 4 m/s
Step 6 — State direction
The result is positive, so the combined object moves in the original rightward direction of Trolley 1.
v = 4 m/s rightward
EX 2 Two moving objects colliding — finding final velocity of one
Given

Ball A: m₁ = 3 kg, u₁ = 8 m/s

Ball B: m₂ = 5 kg, u₂ = 2 m/s (same direction)

After collision: v₁ = 4 m/s

Find: final velocity of Ball B, v₂ = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write conservation of momentum formula
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Step 2 — Calculate left side (total initial momentum)
(3 × 8) + (5 × 2) = 24 + 10 = 34 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Calculate known part of right side (m₁v₁)
m₁v₁ = 3 × 4 = 12 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Set up equation
34 = 12 + 5v₂
Step 5 — Solve for v₂
5v₂ = 34 − 12 = 22
Step 6 — Divide to find v₂
v₂ = 22 ÷ 5 = 4.4 m/s
v₂ = 4.4 m/s (in original direction)
EX 3 Head-on collision (objects moving toward each other)
Given

Object A: m₁ = 6 kg, u₁ = +10 m/s (rightward)

Object B: m₂ = 4 kg, u₂ = −5 m/s (leftward)

After collision: they stick together

Find: common velocity v = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Note the direction signs carefully
Rightward = positive (+), leftward = negative (−). Object B moves leftward so u₂ = −5 m/s.
Step 2 — Write the formula (stick together = common velocity)
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂) × v
Step 3 — Calculate total initial momentum
(6 × 10) + (4 × −5) = 60 + (−20) = 60 − 20 = 40 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Set up and solve equation
(6 + 4) × v = 40
Step 5 — Calculate v
10v = 40 → v = 40 ÷ 10 = 4 m/s
Step 6 — Interpret the sign
Positive result means the combined object moves RIGHTWARD — in the direction that Object A was originally moving.
v = 4 m/s rightward
EX 4 Recoil — a person jumping off a boat
Given

Mass of person, m₁ = 60 kg

Mass of boat, m₂ = 120 kg

Initially everything at rest (u = 0 for both)

Person jumps with velocity v₁ = +5 m/s

Find: velocity of boat v₂ = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — State initial total momentum
Everything is at rest, so total initial momentum = zero.
Total initial momentum = 0 kg·m/s
Step 2 — Write conservation equation
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Step 3 — Substitute initial values (both at rest)
0 + 0 = (60 × 5) + (120 × v₂)
Step 4 — Expand the right side
0 = 300 + 120v₂
Step 5 — Solve for v₂
120v₂ = −300
Step 6 — Calculate v₂
v₂ = −300 ÷ 120 = −2.5 m/s
Step 7 — Interpret result
The negative sign means the boat moves in the OPPOSITE direction to the person — the boat moves backward as the person jumps forward.
v₂ = 2.5 m/s backward (opposite to person's jump)

Chapter 4 — Practice Questions

Q1 State the Law of Conservation of Momentum and give TWO conditions under which it applies.
The Law of Conservation of Momentum states that the total linear momentum of a system remains constant (does not change) provided no net external force acts on the system. Conditions: (1) The system must be closed — no mass enters or leaves. (2) No net external force acts on the system — internal forces between objects in the system do not count because they cancel out by Newton's 3rd Law.
Q2 A 5 kg object moving at 10 m/s collides with a 3 kg object at rest. They stick together. Find their common velocity after collision.
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v
(5 × 10) + (3 × 0) = (5 + 3) × v
50 = 8v
v = 50 ÷ 8 = 6.25 m/s
Q3 Object A (2 kg, 6 m/s rightward) hits Object B (4 kg, 1 m/s rightward). After the collision, A moves at 2 m/s rightward. Find B's final velocity.
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
(2×6) + (4×1) = (2×2) + 4v₂
12 + 4 = 4 + 4v₂
16 − 4 = 4v₂ → 12 = 4v₂
v₂ = 3 m/s rightward
Q4 A 80 kg astronaut in space pushes off from a 400 kg spacecraft at 2 m/s. If both were initially at rest, what is the spacecraft's recoil velocity?
Initial total momentum = 0 (both at rest)
0 = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
0 = (80 × 2) + (400 × v₂)
0 = 160 + 400v₂
v₂ = −160 ÷ 400 = −0.4 m/s
Spacecraft moves at 0.4 m/s in opposite direction to astronaut.
Q5 Two objects of 3 kg and 5 kg move toward each other at 4 m/s and 2 m/s respectively. After collision they stick together. Find the final velocity and direction.
Let rightward = positive: u₁ = +4 m/s, u₂ = −2 m/s
(3×4) + (5×−2) = (3+5)v
12 + (−10) = 8v
2 = 8v → v = 0.25 m/s
Positive result: moves rightward (direction of the 3 kg object)
Q6 Distinguish between internal forces and external forces in the context of conservation of momentum. Which one affects conservation?
Internal forces are forces that objects within the system exert on each other (e.g., collision forces between two balls). By Newton's 3rd Law, these forces always come in equal and opposite pairs, so they cancel — they do NOT change the total momentum of the system. External forces are forces from outside the system (e.g., friction, air resistance, gravity from outside). External forces CAN change the total momentum. Conservation only holds when external forces are zero or balanced.

Types of Collisions

Not all collisions are the same. While momentum is ALWAYS conserved in every type of collision (in an isolated system), kinetic energy behaves differently depending on the type of collision.

5.1 Elastic Collisions

In an elastic collision, both momentum AND kinetic energy are perfectly conserved. The objects bounce off each other without any permanent deformation or energy loss. This is an ideal situation — perfectly elastic collisions rarely happen in real life but are a useful model (e.g., billiard balls, gas molecules).

▸ Formula 8 — Elastic Collision (Momentum + KE both conserved)
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
AND ½m₁u₁² + ½m₂u₂² = ½m₁v₁² + ½m₂v₂²
▸ Formula 9 — Elastic Collision Velocity Equations (Equal masses or derivation result)
v₁ = (m₁−m₂)u₁ + 2m₂u₂) ÷ (m₁+m₂)
v₂ = (2m₁u₁ + (m₂−m₁)u₂) ÷ (m₁+m₂) These give final velocities directly without needing two simultaneous equations
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Elastic Collisions
EX 1 Equal mass elastic collision (special case)
Given

Ball 1: m₁ = 2 kg, u₁ = 10 m/s

Ball 2: m₂ = 2 kg, u₂ = 0 m/s (at rest)

Elastic collision

Find: v₁ and v₂

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — For equal masses in elastic collision, the result is: Ball 1 stops, Ball 2 moves with Ball 1's original velocity
This is a famous result. When two objects of equal mass collide elastically and one is at rest, they exchange velocities — the moving one stops and the stationary one moves at the original speed.
Step 2 — Verify with momentum formula
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Step 3 — Substitute (assuming v₁ = 0, v₂ = 10)
(2×10) + (2×0) = (2×0) + (2×10)
Step 4 — Check: left side = right side

Elastic collision

5.2 Inelastic and Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is NOT fully conserved. Some kinetic energy is converted into other forms — heat, sound, or deformation energy. Real-world collisions (car crashes, ball hitting clay) are inelastic.

A perfectly inelastic collision is the extreme case where the objects stick together after the collision, moving as one unit. This results in the MAXIMUM possible loss of kinetic energy, though momentum is still conserved.

▸ Formula 10 — Perfectly Inelastic Collision
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂) × v
v = common final velocity (both objects) KE lost = KE_before − KE_after KE = ½mv²
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Inelastic Collisions
EX 1 Finding kinetic energy lost in a perfectly inelastic collision
Given

m₁ = 3 kg, u₁ = 10 m/s

m₂ = 2 kg, u₂ = 0 m/s

They stick together after collision

Find: (a) final velocity v, (b) KE lost

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Find final velocity using momentum conservation
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂) × v
Step 2 — Calculate initial total momentum
(3 × 10) + (2 × 0) = 30 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Solve for v
(3 + 2) × v = 30 → 5v = 30 → v = 6 m/s
Step 4 — Calculate KE before collision
KE_before = ½ × 3 × 10² + ½ × 2 × 0² = ½ × 3 × 100 = 150 J
Step 5 — Calculate KE after collision
KE_after = ½ × (3+2) × 6² = ½ × 5 × 36 = 90 J
Step 6 — Find KE lost
KE_lost = KE_before − KE_after = 150 − 90 = 60 J
v = 6 m/s | KE lost = 60 J

Q1 Clearly distinguish between elastic and inelastic collisions. Which type conserves kinetic energy?
In an elastic collision, BOTH momentum and kinetic energy are conserved — objects bounce off each other with no energy loss. Example: billiard balls. The coefficient of restitution (e) is the ratio of the relative velocity of separation to the relative velocity of approach after a collision: e = (v₂ − v₁) ÷ (u₁ − u₂). It measures how "bouncy" a collision is. (a) Perfectly elastic: e = 1 (objects bounce back with same relative speed). (b) Perfectly inelastic: e = 0 (objects stick together, no separation). Real collisions have 0 < e < 1.
CH 06

Explosions

An explosion is the reverse of a perfectly inelastic collision. Objects that are initially together (at rest or moving) fly apart. Conservation of momentum still applies — this is what makes rocket science possible.

▸ Formula 11 — Explosion from Rest
0 = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Total initial momentum = 0 (system at rest before explosion) m₁v₁ = −m₂v₂ (fragments move in opposite directions) |m₁v₁| = |m₂v₂| (equal magnitudes, opposite directions)
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Explosions
EX 1 A gun firing a bullet (recoil)
Given

mass of bullet, m₁ = 0.02 kg

mass of gun, m₂ = 2 kg

velocity of bullet, v₁ = +400 m/s (forward)

Both initially at rest

Find: recoil velocity of gun, v₂ = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — State initial total momentum
Gun and bullet are both at rest before firing, so total initial momentum = 0.
Total initial p = 0 kg·m/s
Step 2 — Write conservation equation
0 = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Step 3 — Substitute known values
0 = (0.02 × 400) + (2 × v₂)
Step 4 — Simplify the left side
0 = 8 + 2v₂
Step 5 — Solve for v₂
2v₂ = −8 → v₂ = −4 m/s
Step 6 — Interpret the result
The negative sign confirms the gun recoils BACKWARD (opposite to the bullet's forward motion). The gun moves at 4 m/s backward.
v₂ = 4 m/s backward (recoil velocity)
EX 2 A bomb exploding into two fragments
Given

Total mass before explosion = 10 kg (at rest)

Fragment B: mass = 6 kg, velocity = −15 m/s (leftward)

Total initial p = 0

0 = 4v_A − 90
Step 5 — Solve for v_A
4v_A = 90 → v_A = 90 ÷ 4 = 22.5 m/s
Step 6 — Confirm direction
Fragment A moves rightward (+22.5 m/s), Fragment B moves leftward (−15 m/s). They move in opposite directions, as expected in an explosion.
v_A = 22.5 m/s rightward

Q1 Explain why a gun recoils when fired. Use the Law of Conservation of Momentum in your answer.
Before firing, both gun and bullet are at rest — total momentum = 0. When the gun fires, it gives the bullet a large forward momentum. By conservation of momentum, the total must remain zero. Therefore, the gun must receive an equal and opposite (backward) momentum to balance the bullet's forward momentum. This backward motion of the gun is called recoil. Gun recoil: m_gun × v_gun = −m_bullet × v_bullet.

The 2 kg piece moves at 15 m/s to the LEFT.

0 = (0.05 × 300) + (3 × v_rifle)
0 = 15 + 3v_rifle

The rifle recoils at 5 m/s backward.
Q4 How does rocket propulsion work in space where there is nothing to push against? Explain using momentum conservation.
A rocket works by ejecting exhaust gases backward at high speed. By conservation of momentum, the rocket gains an equal and opposite (forward) momentum. This is why rockets can work in the vacuum of space: F_rocket = −F_exhaust (Newton's 3rd Law), and total momentum is conserved throughout the flight.
CH 07

Momentum in Two Dimensions

When objects move in two dimensions (e.g., colliding at angles, not head-on), we must deal with both horizontal and vertical components of momentum separately. The key principle remains the same — total momentum is conserved in EACH direction independently.

▸ Formula 12 — Conservation of Momentum in 2D
x-direction: m₁u₁ₓ + m₂u₂ₓ = m₁v₁ₓ + m₂v₂ₓ
y-direction: m₁u₁ᵧ + m₂u₂ᵧ = m₁v₁ᵧ + m₂v₂ᵧ x-component: p_x = p × cos(θ) y-component: p_y = p × sin(θ) Resultant: |p| = √(p_x² + p_y²)

7.1 Resolving Momentum into Components

When an object moves at an angle θ to the horizontal, its momentum has two components. We split it into horizontal (x) and vertical (y) parts using trigonometry, just like we do with forces and velocities.

▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — 2D Momentum
EX 1 Object moving at an angle — resolving momentum
Given

mass, m = 3 kg

speed, v = 10 m/s at 30° above horizontal

Find: horizontal and vertical components of momentum

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Calculate total momentum magnitude
p = m × v = 3 × 10 = 30 kg·m/s
Step 2 — Find horizontal component (x)
Use cosine because the horizontal component is adjacent to the angle:
p_x = p × cos(30°) = 30 × cos(30°)
Step 3 — Calculate cos(30°) = 0.866
p_x = 30 × 0.866 = 25.98 kg·m/s ≈ 26.0 kg·m/s
Step 4 — Find vertical component (y)
Use sine because the vertical component is opposite to the angle:
p_y = p × sin(30°) = 30 × sin(30°)
Step 5 — Calculate sin(30°) = 0.5
p_y = 30 × 0.5 = 15 kg·m/s
p_x = 26.0 kg·m/s | p_y = 15 kg·m/s
EX 2 Two-dimensional collision — finding resultant momentum
Given

Object A: p_x = 20 kg·m/s, p_y = 0

Object B: p_x = 0, p_y = 15 kg·m/s

Find: total resultant momentum (magnitude and direction)

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Add x-components of momentum
Total p_x = 20 + 0 = 20 kg·m/s
Step 2 — Add y-components of momentum
Total p_y = 0 + 15 = 15 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Use Pythagoras to find resultant magnitude
|p| = √(p_x² + p_y²) = √(20² + 15²)
Step 4 — Calculate
|p| = √(400 + 225) = √625 = 25 kg·m/s
Step 5 — Find direction angle (θ from horizontal)
tan(θ) = p_y ÷ p_x = 15 ÷ 20 = 0.75
Step 6 — Calculate angle
θ = tan⁻¹(0.75) ≈ 36.9°
|p| = 25 kg·m/s at 36.9° above horizontal

CH 08

Centre of Mass

The centre of mass (COM) is the single point where the entire mass of a system can be considered to be concentrated for the purpose of analyzing motion. It behaves as if all the mass were located at that one point and all external forces acted there.

▸ Formula 13 — Centre of Mass Position
x_COM = (m₁x₁ + m₂x₂) ÷ (m₁ + m₂)
x_COM = position of centre of mass x₁, x₂ = positions of each mass m₁, m₂ = masses of each object
▸ Formula 14 — Velocity of Centre of Mass
v_COM = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂) ÷ (m₁ + m₂)
v_COM = velocity of the centre of mass = total momentum ÷ total mass This velocity does NOT change unless an external force acts
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Centre of Mass
EX 1 Finding the centre of mass of two objects
Given

m₁ = 3 kg at position x₁ = 2 m

m₂ = 6 kg at position x₂ = 8 m

Find: position of centre of mass

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the centre of mass formula
x_COM = (m₁x₁ + m₂x₂) ÷ (m₁ + m₂)
Step 2 — Calculate numerator
(3 × 2) + (6 × 8) = 6 + 48 = 54
Step 3 — Calculate denominator (total mass)
m₁ + m₂ = 3 + 6 = 9 kg
Step 4 — Divide numerator by denominator
x_COM = 54 ÷ 9 = 6 m
Step 5 — Interpret the result
The COM is at 6 m — closer to the heavier 6 kg mass (at 8 m) than to the lighter 3 kg mass (at 2 m). The COM always lies closer to the heavier mass.
x_COM = 6 m from the origin
EX 2 Finding velocity of the centre of mass
Given

m₁ = 4 kg, v₁ = 6 m/s (rightward)

m₂ = 8 kg, v₂ = 3 m/s (rightward)

Find: v_COM = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the velocity of COM formula
v_COM = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂) ÷ (m₁ + m₂)
Step 2 — Calculate numerator (total momentum)
(4 × 6) + (8 × 3) = 24 + 24 = 48 kg·m/s
Step 3 — Calculate total mass (denominator)
4 + 8 = 12 kg
Step 4 — Divide
v_COM = 48 ÷ 12 = 4 m/s
v_COM = 4 m/s rightward

Q1 What is the centre of mass? Why is it a useful concept in physics?

CH 09

Rocket Propulsion & Variable Mass Systems

Rockets are a perfect real-world application of conservation of momentum. Unlike cars that push against the ground, rockets push against their own ejected exhaust gas. This means they can work in the vacuum of space where there is nothing external to push against.

▸ Formula 15 — Thrust Force of a Rocket
F_thrust = v_exhaust × (Δm ÷ Δt)
F_thrust = thrust force on rocket (N) v_exhaust = speed of exhaust gas relative to rocket (m/s) Δm/Δt = mass flow rate of exhaust (kg/s)
▸ Formula 16 — Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation
Δv = v_exhaust × ln(m_i ÷ m_f)
Δv = change in rocket velocity (m/s) v_exhaust = exhaust velocity (m/s) m_i = initial total mass (rocket + fuel) m_f = final mass (rocket without fuel) ln = natural logarithm
▸ WORKED EXAMPLES — Rocket Propulsion
EX 1 Calculating rocket thrust force
Given

exhaust speed, v_ex = 2,500 m/s

mass flow rate, Δm/Δt = 200 kg/s

Find: thrust force F = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the thrust formula
F = v_exhaust × (Δm ÷ Δt)
Step 2 — Identify all given values
v_exhaust = 2,500 m/s, Δm/Δt = 200 kg/s
Step 3 — Substitute into formula
F = 2,500 × 200
Step 4 — Multiply to find thrust
F = 500,000 N = 500 kN
F_thrust = 500,000 N (500 kN)
EX 2 Using the Tsiolkovsky equation to find Δv
Given

initial mass (with fuel), m_i = 10,000 kg

final mass (without fuel), m_f = 2,000 kg

exhaust velocity, v_ex = 3,000 m/s

Find: change in velocity Δv = ?

▸ FULL STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
Step 1 — Write the Tsiolkovsky equation
Δv = v_ex × ln(m_i ÷ m_f)
Step 2 — Calculate the mass ratio
m_i ÷ m_f = 10,000 ÷ 2,000 = 5
Step 3 — Take the natural logarithm of 5
ln(5) ≈ 1.609
Step 4 — Multiply by exhaust velocity
Δv = 3,000 × 1.609 = 4,827 m/s
Δv ≈ 4,827 m/s

Q1 Explain how a rocket accelerates in the vacuum of space where there is nothing to push against.
A rocket carries its own fuel and burns it to produce exhaust gas. The rocket engine ejects this gas backward at extremely high speed. By conservation of momentum, the rocket gains an equal and opposite forward momentum. By Newton's 3rd Law, the ejected gas pushes the rocket forward. No external surface is needed — the rocket creates its own reaction mass (the exhaust). This is the fundamental principle that allows space travel.

CH 10

Real-Life Applications of Momentum

Linear momentum principles are found everywhere in the real world. Understanding momentum helps engineers design safer vehicles, sports scientists improve performance, and physicists understand the universe. Here are the key applications you must know.

10.1 Car Safety — Crumple Zones, Airbags, Seatbelts

All car safety features work on the same impulse principle: increasing the time of impact reduces the force on passengers. Since J = F × Δt = Δp, and Δp is fixed (the car must stop), a larger Δt means a smaller F.

  • Crumple zones: The front of the car is designed to collapse and crumple, increasing the time of the crash and reducing peak force on passengers.
  • Airbags: Inflate instantly and provide a soft cushion, increasing stopping time from ~0.02 s to ~0.2 s, reducing force by 10×.
  • Seatbelts: Spread force over a larger area of the body and allow slight stretching to increase stopping time.

10.2 Sports Applications

Momentum concepts explain many phenomena in sports:

  • Cricket/Baseball: Following through on a hit increases contact time, increasing impulse, giving the ball more momentum.
  • Catching: Moving hands back when catching reduces the stopping force.
  • Karate: Striking quickly (small Δt) with the same impulse creates a very large force — enough to break boards.
  • High jump: Deep foam mats increase stopping time, reducing force on landing.

10.3 Billiard/Snooker Ball Collisions

Billiard balls approximate elastic collisions — they bounce off each other with very little energy loss. Players exploit this by predicting angles and final velocities using conservation of momentum in 2D.

10.4 Jet Engines and Propulsion

Jet engines work by taking in air at the front and ejecting it at the back at high speed. The backward ejected air creates a forward thrust on the aircraft — exactly like a rocket but using atmospheric oxygen instead of carrying its own oxidizer.

Chapter 10 — Final Comprehensive Questions

Q1 A car of mass 1,000 kg traveling at 20 m/s crashes into a wall and stops in 0.1 s. Calculate the average force on the car. How would crumple zones help?
Δp = mv − mu = (1000×0) − (1000×20) = −20,000 kg·m/s
F = Δp ÷ Δt = 20,000 ÷ 0.1 = 200,000 N (200 kN)
With crumple zones (Δt = 0.5 s): F = 20,000 ÷ 0.5 = 40,000 N
Crumple zones reduce force from 200,000 N to 40,000 N — 5× safer!
Q2 Explain why a karate expert can break a thick board with their hand, using impulse and momentum concepts.
The karate expert's hand has a fixed amount of momentum change (Δp) from fast-moving to stop. By striking very quickly (very small Δt), the force F = Δp/Δt becomes extremely large. The expert trains to make contact very briefly and retract quickly, maximizing force. Also, they aim to hit THROUGH the board, not at the surface — this maintains high velocity during contact, keeping Δp large.
Q3 A 70 kg goalkeeper dives to catch a 0.45 kg ball moving at 20 m/s. The goalkeeper catches it and skids for 2 seconds on the ground with friction force 50 N. (a) Find momentum of ball before catch. (b) After catching, find their combined velocity (ignore goalkeeper's initial velocity). (c) Find final velocity after 2 s of sliding.
(a) p_ball = 0.45 × 20 = 9 kg·m/s
(b) Conservation: 9 = (70 + 0.45) × v → v = 9 ÷ 70.45 ≈ 0.128 m/s
(c) F = ma → a = 50 ÷ 70.45 = 0.71 m/s² (deceleration)
v_final = v − a×t = 0.128 − (0.71 × 2) = 0.128 − 1.42 = negative
→ They stop before 2 s (friction is too strong). They stop when v = 0.
Q4 List all the conditions that must be satisfied for the law of conservation of momentum to hold. Give one example where it does NOT hold and explain why.
Conditions: (1) The system must be isolated — no net external force acts on it. (2) The system is closed — no mass enters or leaves. Example where it does NOT hold: A ball rolling along the ground slows down and stops. Momentum is NOT conserved here because friction from the ground is an external force acting on the ball — it removes momentum from the ball. The ball-ground system together would conserve momentum, but the ball alone does not.
Q5 Summarize ALL the different types of collisions in a table showing: type, whether momentum is conserved, whether KE is conserved, and whether objects stick together.
ELASTIC: Momentum ✓ | KE ✓ | Objects separate | Example: billiard balls
INELASTIC: Momentum ✓ | KE ✗ (some lost) | Objects separate | Example: most real collisions
PERFECTLY INELASTIC: Momentum ✓ | KE ✗ (maximum lost) | Objects stick together | Example: car crash, clay
NOTE: Momentum is ALWAYS conserved in all collision types (provided no external forces). Only KE varies.
Q6 A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s east collides with a 3 kg ball at rest. They stick together. The combined object then slides on a rough surface with deceleration of 0.5 m/s² for 3 seconds. Find: (a) velocity just after collision, (b) final velocity after 3 s of sliding.
(a) Conservation: (2×5) + 0 = (2+3)v → 10 = 5v → v = 2 m/s eastward
(b) v_final = v − a×t = 2 − (0.5 × 3) = 2 − 1.5 = 0.5 m/s eastward
