Understanding what complex numbers are, where they come from, and how to identify their parts

1.1 What is a Complex Number?

When you try to solve an equation like x² = −1, you run into a problem. No real number multiplied by itself gives a negative answer. To solve this kind of equation, mathematicians introduced a new idea — the imaginary unit.

The imaginary unit is defined as:

i = √(−1) which means i² = −1

Any number that comes with √(−) in it stands as √(−1), so we write it as i.

A Complex Number is any number that combines a real part and an imaginary part together. We write it as:

Z = x + iy

— Z is the complex number
— x is the Real Part, written as Re(Z)
— y is the Imaginary Part, written as Im(Z)
— i is the imaginary unit, i = √(−1)

Note: The imaginary part is just the number y, not iy. For example in Z = 3 + 5i, the imaginary part Im(Z) = 5, not 5i.

i²⁰ = (i⁴)⁵

Break i⁶⁰ into equal chunks
i⁶⁰ = i²⁰ × i²⁰ × i²⁰

The powers of i always follow this 4-step cycle: i¹ = i, i² = −1, i³ = −i, i⁴ = 1, then it repeats. To simplify any high power of i, divide the power by 4 and look at the remainder.

2.1 Conjugate of a Complex Number

The conjugate of a complex number Z is written as Z̄ (Z-bar). To find the conjugate, you simply change the sign of the imaginary part. The real part stays exactly the same.

If Z = x + iy, then the conjugate is:

Z̄ = x − iy

Positive imaginary → becomes Negative  |  Negative imaginary → becomes Positive

Complex Number ZConjugate Z̄What changed
Z₁ = 3 + 2iZ̄₁ = 3 − 2i+2i became −2i
Z₂ = −3 + 4iZ̄₂ = −3 − 4i+4i became −4i
Z₃ = −2 − 3iZ̄₃ = −2 + 3i−3i became +3i
Z₄ = 5 − 7iZ̄₄ = 5 + 7i−7i became +7i
Z₅ = 4 + 0iZ̄₅ = 4 + 0iNo change (no imaginary)
Addition

2.2 Addition of Complex Numbers

To add two complex numbers, you add the real parts together and add the imaginary parts together separately. Never mix real and imaginary parts.

If Z₁ = x₁ + iy₁ and Z₂ = x₂ + iy₂, then:

Z₁ + Z₂ = (x₁ + x₂) + (y₁ + y₂)i
EX 1
If Z₁ = 3 + 2i and Z₂ = 2 + 4i, find Z₁ + Z₂
1
Write out the addition
Z₁ + Z₂ = (3 + 2i) + (2 + 4i)
2
Group the real parts together and the imaginary parts together
= (3 + 2) + (2 + 4)i
3
Do the arithmetic in each bracket
= 5 + 6i
Z₁ + Z₂ = 5 + 6i
EX 2
If Z₁ = 3 + 2i and Z₃ = 2 − i, find Z₁ + Z₃
1
Write out the addition
Z₁ + Z₃ = (3 + 2i) + (2 − i)
2
Group real parts: 3 + 2 = 5  |  Group imaginary parts: 2 + (−1) = 1
= (3 + 2) + (2 + (−1))i = (5) + (1)i
Z₁ + Z₃ = 5 + i
EX 3
If Z₁ = −4 + 3i and Z₂ = 6 − 5i, find Z₁ + Z₂
1
Write out the addition
Z₁ + Z₂ = (−4 + 3i) + (6 − 5i)
2
Group real parts: −4 + 6 = 2  |  Group imaginary parts: 3 + (−5) = −2
= (−4 + 6) + (3 + (−5))i = (2) + (−2)i
Z₁ + Z₂ = 2 − 2i
Subtraction

2.3 Subtraction of Complex Numbers

To subtract, you subtract the real parts and subtract the imaginary parts. Be very careful with the signs when the second number has negatives.

If Z₁ = x₁ + iy₁ and Z₂ = x₂ + iy₂, then:

Z₁ − Z₂ = (x₁ − x₂) + (y₁ − y₂)i
EX 4
If Z₁ = 3 + 4i and Z₂ = 2 − i, find Z₁ − Z₂
1
Write out the subtraction
Z₁ − Z₂ = (3 + 4i) − (2 − i)
2
Expand the brackets — be careful: minus × minus = plus
= 3 + 4i − 2 + i
3
Group real parts: 3 − 2 = 1  |  Group imaginary parts: 4 + 1 = 5
= (3 − 2) + (4 + 1)i = 1 + 5i
Z₁ − Z₂ = 1 + 5i
EX 5
If Z₁ = 6 + 5i and Z₂ = 2 + 3i, find Z₁ − Z₂
1
Write out the subtraction
Z₁ − Z₂ = (6 + 5i) − (2 + 3i)
2
Expand: remove the brackets, change signs of the second group
= 6 + 5i − 2 − 3i
3
Group real: 6 − 2 = 4  |  Group imaginary: 5 − 3 = 2
= (6 − 2) + (5 − 3)i = 4 + 2i
Z₁ − Z₂ = 4 + 2i
EX 6
If Z₂ = 2 + 3i and Z₁ = 6 + 5i, find Z₂ − Z₁
1
Write out — note the order is reversed now
Z₂ − Z₁ = (2 + 3i) − (6 + 5i)
2
Expand the brackets
= 2 + 3i − 6 − 5i
3
Group real: 2 − 6 = −4  |  Group imaginary: 3 − 5 = −2
= (2 − 6) + (3 − 5)i = −4 + (−2)i
Z₂ − Z₁ = −4 − 2i
Multiplication

2.4 Multiplication of Complex Numbers

To multiply, you use the FOIL method (First, Outside, Inside, Last) — exactly like expanding double brackets in algebra. The key step is always replacing i² with −1 at the end.

Multiply out every bracket fully, then replace i² = −1, then collect like terms.

Remember: i² = −1 always!
EX 7
If Z₁ = 2 + 3i and Z₂ = 4 − 8i, find Z₁ × Z₂
1
Write out the multiplication
Z₁ × Z₂ = (2 + 3i)(4 − 8i)
2
Expand using FOIL — multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second
First: 2 × 4 = 8 Outside: 2 × (−8i) = −16i Inside: 3i × 4 = 12i Last: 3i × (−8i) = −24i² = 8 − 16i + 12i − 24i²
3
Replace i² with −1: −24i² = −24 × (−1) = +24
= 8 − 16i + 12i + 24
4
Group real parts: 8 + 24 = 32  |  Group imaginary: −16i + 12i = −4i
= (8 + 24) + (−16 + 12)i = 32 + (−4)i
Z₁ × Z₂ = 32 − 4i
EX 8
If Z₁ = 3 + 4i and Z₂ = 2 − i, find Z₁ × Z₂
1
Write out the multiplication
Z₁ × Z₂ = (3 + 4i)(2 − i)
2
Expand using FOIL
First: 3 × 2 = 6 Outside: 3 × (−i) = −3i Inside: 4i × 2 = 8i Last: 4i × (−i) = −4i² = 6 − 3i + 8i − 4i²
3
Replace i² = −1: −4i² = −4 × (−1) = +4
= 6 − 3i + 8i + 4
4
Group: real = 6 + 4 = 10  |  imaginary = −3 + 8 = 5
= 10 + 5i
Z₁ × Z₂ = 10 + 5i
EX 9
If Z₁ = 3 + 4i, find Z̄₁ × Z₁ (Z₁ times its own conjugate)
1
First find the conjugate: Z₁ = 3 + 4i, so Z̄₁ = 3 − 4i
Z̄₁ × Z₁ = (3 − 4i)(3 + 4i)
2
Expand using FOIL
First: 3 × 3 = 9 Outside: 3 × 4i = 12i Inside: −4i × 3 = −12i Last: −4i × 4i = −16i² = 9 + 12i − 12i − 16i²
3
The +12i and −12i cancel out. Replace i² = −1
= 9 + 0 − 16(−1) = 9 + 16
Z̄₁ × Z₁ = 25 (a real number — always happens with conjugate pairs!)
Division

2.5 Division of Complex Numbers

To divide complex numbers, you multiply both the top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator. This makes the bottom become a real number (no i), which you can then divide normally.

Steps for division:

1. Find the conjugate of the denominator (bottom number)
2. Multiply both top and bottom by that conjugate
3. Expand both top and bottom using FOIL
4. Replace i² = −1 everywhere
5. Simplify to get a + bi form

EX 10
If Z₁ = 3 + 2i and Z₂ = 1 − i, find Z₁ ÷ Z₂
1
Write as a fraction
Z₁/Z₂ = (3 + 2i) / (1 − i)
2
The conjugate of the bottom (1 − i) is (1 + i). Multiply top and bottom by (1 + i)
= (3 + 2i)(1 + i) / (1 − i)(1 + i)
3
Expand the TOP using FOIL
Top: (3 + 2i)(1 + i) First: 3 × 1 = 3 Outside: 3 × i = 3i Inside: 2i × 1 = 2i Last: 2i × i = 2i² Top = 3 + 3i + 2i + 2i² Top = 3 + 3i + 2i + 2(−1) Top = 3 + 5i − 2 Top = 1 + 5i
4
Expand the BOTTOM using FOIL
Bottom: (1 − i)(1 + i) First: 1 × 1 = 1 Outside: 1 × i = i Inside: −i × 1 = −i Last: −i × i = −i² Bottom = 1 + i − i − i² Bottom = 1 + 0 − (−1) Bottom = 1 + 1 = 2
5
Put it together: Top ÷ Bottom
= (1 + 5i) / 2 = 1/2 + 5/2 · i
Z₁/Z₂ = 1/2 + 5/2 i
EX 11
If Z₁ = 3 + 4i and Z₂ = 2 − i, find Z₂/Z₁
1
Write as a fraction (Z₂ on top, Z₁ on bottom)
Z₂/Z₁ = (2 − i) / (3 + 4i)
2
Conjugate of bottom (3 + 4i) is (3 − 4i). Multiply both by (3 − 4i)
= (2 − i)(3 − 4i) / (3 + 4i)(3 − 4i)
3
Expand the TOP
First: 2 × 3 = 6 Outside: 2 × (−4i) = −8i Inside: (−i) × 3 = −3i Last: (−i) × (−4i) = 4i² Top = 6 − 8i − 3i + 4i² Top = 6 − 11i + 4(−1) Top = 6 − 11i − 4 Top = 2 − 11i
4
Expand the BOTTOM
First: 3 × 3 = 9 Outside: 3 × (−4i) = −12i Inside: 4i × 3 = 12i Last: 4i × (−4i) = −16i² Bottom = 9 − 12i + 12i − 16i² Bottom = 9 + 0 − 16(−1) Bottom = 9 + 16 = 25
5
Divide each part by 25
= (2 − 11i) / 25 = 2/25 − 11/25 · i
Z₂/Z₁ = 2/25 − 11/25 i

3.1 What is an Argand Diagram?

An Argand Diagram is like a coordinate graph, but instead of x and y axes, we use the Real axis (horizontal) and the Imaginary axis (vertical). Every complex number Z = x + iy becomes a point at position (x, y) on this diagram.

Z = x + iy → Point (x, y) on Argand Diagram

— The x value (real part) tells you how far to go LEFT or RIGHT
— The y value (imaginary part) tells you how far to go UP or DOWN

3.4 Modulus — The Distance from the Origin

The modulus of a complex number Z = x + iy is written |Z| and it tells you the distance from the origin (0,0) to the point Z on the Argand diagram. We find it using Pythagoras' theorem.

If Z = x + iy, then:

|Z| = √(x² + y²)
EX 5
Find the modulus of Z = 3 + 4i
1
Identify: x = 3, y = 4
2
Apply the formula
|Z| = √(x² + y²) |Z| = √(3² + 4²) |Z| = √(9 + 16) |Z| = √25
|Z| = 5
EX 6
Find the modulus of Z = −5 + 12i
1
Identify: x = −5, y = 12
2
Apply the formula (squares remove the negative)
|Z| = √((−5)² + 12²) |Z| = √(25 + 144) |Z| = √169
|Z| = 13

The most powerful tool in complex numbers — raising complex numbers to any power elegantly

4.1 Polar Form of a Complex Number

Before using De Moivre's theorem, we must understand the polar form of a complex number. Every complex number Z = x + iy can also be written using its modulus r and argument θ (the angle it makes with the positive real axis).

The polar form of Z = x + iy is:

Z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)

Where:
— r = |Z| = √(x² + y²)    (the modulus)
— θ = arg(Z) = tan⁻¹(y/x)    (the argument — the angle)

4.2 De Moivre's Theorem — The Statement

If Z = r(cos θ + i sin θ), then for any integer n:

Zⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ)

In words: raise the modulus to the power n, and multiply the angle by n.
That is the whole theorem.

Why it is beautiful: Without this theorem, computing something like (cos 20° + i sin 20°)⁹ would require multiplying complex numbers nine times. With De Moivre's theorem, you just write cos(180°) + i sin(180°) in one step!

4.3 Examples — Applying De Moivre's Theorem

EX 1
Use De Moivre's theorem to simplify (cos 30° + i sin 30°)⁶
1
Here r = 1 (the modulus is 1) and θ = 30° and n = 6
2
Apply De Moivre's: raise r to the power 6 and multiply angle by 6
= 1⁶ × (cos(6 × 30°) + i sin(6 × 30°)) = 1 × (cos 180° + i sin 180°)

Evaluate: cos 180° = −1 and sin 180° = 0
= 1 × (−1 + i × 0) = −1 + 0i

5.1 What are the nth Roots of Unity?

The nth roots of unity are all the complex numbers Z that satisfy the equation Zⁿ = 1. There are exactly n solutions, and when you plot them on the Argand diagram, they form equally-spaced points on a circle of radius 1 centred at the origin.

The nth roots of unity are found using the formula:

Z_k = cos(2kπ/n) + i sin(2kπ/n)

where k = 0, 1, 2, 3, … , (n−1)
Each value of k gives one root. There are n roots in total.

In degrees: The formula becomes Z_k = cos(360°k/n) + i sin(360°k/n). Each root is separated from the next by an angle of 360°/n on the unit circle.

5.2 Examples — Finding nth Roots of Unity

EX 1
Find the 2nd roots of unity (square roots of 1)
1
n = 2, so k goes from 0 to 1 (giving us 2 roots)
2
Each root is separated by 360°/2 = 180°
3
Find each root:
k = 0: Z₀ = cos(0°) + i sin(0°) = 1 + 0i = 1 k = 1: Z₁ = cos(180°) + i sin(180°) = −1 + 0i = −1
The 2nd roots of unity are: 1 and −1
EX 2
Find the 4th roots of unity (all Z where Z⁴ = 1)
1
n = 4, so k goes from 0 to 3 (giving 4 roots)
2
Each root is separated by 360°/4 = 90°
3
Find each root using the formula
k = 0: Z₀ = cos(0°) + i sin(0°) = cos 0° + i sin 0° = 1 + 0i = 1 k = 1: Z₁ = cos(90°) + i sin(90°) = 0 + i(1) = i k = 2: Z₂ = cos(180°) + i sin(180°) = −1 + 0i = −1 k = 3: Z₃ = cos(270°) + i sin(270°) = 0 + i(−1) = −i
The 4th roots of unity are: 1, i, −1, −i

6 roots evenly spaced at 60° apart around the unit circle

5.3 Interactive — nth Roots on the Unit Circle

Key facts about nth roots of unity: (1) There are always exactly n roots. (2) They are all equally spaced by 360°/n degrees apart. (3) They all lie on the unit circle (radius = 1). (4) One root is always Z = 1 (at angle 0°). (5) When you add all the roots together, they always sum to zero.
